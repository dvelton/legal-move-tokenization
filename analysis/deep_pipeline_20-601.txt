DEEP PIPELINE ANALYSIS
Cameron v. EMW Women's Surgical Center, S. Ct. Docket No. 20-601
Decided March 3, 2022 | 8-1 reversal (Alito, J., majority)
Tracing moves from briefs -> oral argument -> opinion

================================================================================
SECTION 1: MOVE ENGAGEMENT AT ORAL ARGUMENT
================================================================================

This section maps each major move from the brief annotations to the justice
questions at oral argument. Petitioner's counsel was Matthew F. Kuhn.
Respondent's counsel was Alexa Kolbi-Molinas. The argument ran 238 turns.

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
P-SOVEREIGNTY (AU-P1.1 through AU-P1.3)
States are sovereigns with special status in federal litigation; "normal
rules" must accommodate sovereign interests.
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

This was the most heavily engaged move at oral argument and received the
most favorable treatment.

  GORSUCH -- ENGAGED_FAVORABLY
    Turn 94: Asked where sovereignty interests "run out" and when a court of
    appeals could properly deny state intervention. The question presupposes
    the validity of the sovereignty frame and asks only about its limits.
    Turn 176: Pressed Kolbi-Molinas directly: "should this Court give any
    weight to the fact that we are dealing with a sovereign?"
    Turn 186: Called intervention "particularly powerful as a sovereign."

  BARRETT -- ENGAGED_FAVORABLY
    Turn 103: Suggested the case implicates SCOTUS's "inherent supervisory
    authority" over circuit courts and raised the concern that the Sixth
    Circuit was "trying to evade our review." This actively builds a
    rationale for petitioner by linking sovereignty to institutional concerns.
    Turn 105: Accepted the sovereignty framing while probing whether the
    principle extends to private parties.

  ROBERTS -- ENGAGED_FAVORABLY
    Turns 220-224: During respondent's argument, Roberts reasoned toward
    petitioner's sovereignty position. Turn 220: "once the Secretary's out
    of it, Kentucky ought to -- maybe ought to be there in some form."
    Turn 224: Adopted the "deck reshuffled" metaphor and framed the question
    in terms favorable to petitioner. These turns came late, during the
    respondent's time, which amplifies their signal.

  BREYER -- ENGAGED_FAVORABLY
    Turn 134: Extended hypothetical that recast the case from petitioner's
    vantage: "if there's no prejudice to anybody, and I can't see where
    there is, why can't he just come in and defend the law?"

  KAGAN -- ENGAGED_MIXED
    Turn 164: Told Kolbi-Molinas the jurisdictional rule would be "extremely
    harsh" if it prevented anyone from defending Kentucky's law "even though
    there are significant parts of Kentucky's government that still want
    its law defended." Sympathetic to the practical result, but her framing
    was about outcomes, not constitutional status.
    Turn 196: Tested whether Torres forecloses the sovereignty argument
    entirely. Question cut both ways.

  SOTOMAYOR -- ENGAGED_SKEPTICALLY
    Turns 25, 29: "Why would we call it an abuse of discretion for a court
    of appeals... to say we don't really care what has happened in the
    political arena." Direct rejection of the sovereignty/political-
    transition framing.
    Turns 66-68: Pressed on sovereign immunity waiver, which threatens to
    make the sovereignty move costly rather than free.

  THOMAS -- NEUTRAL/PROBING
    Turns 2, 4: Foundational questions about the doctrinal basis for
    appellate intervention and the standard for timeliness. Not signaling
    direction, but his lack of hostility to Kuhn and his sustained pressure
    on Kolbi-Molinas (Turns 110-128) suggest alignment with petitioner.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
P-BETHUNE-HILL-FLIP (AU-P2.1, AU-P2.4)
"Flip side" of Bethune-Hill: Kentucky's agent wants to "fight on."
"Intramural" decisions are the state's business.
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  KAGAN -- ENGAGED_SKEPTICALLY
    Turns 79, 83, 85: Sustained attack on the "two hats theory." Turn 85:
    "the Secretary's role in this entire litigation pretty much proves that
    the two hats theory doesn't work because your theory is that the
    Attorney General was stepping in to replace the Secretary... But the
    Secretary was sued in his capacity as a state official who could enforce
    state law." This was the most direct challenge to any petitioner move
    during the argument.

  SOTOMAYOR -- ENGAGED_SKEPTICALLY
    Turns 70, 72: "If you had stayed in this litigation, would you have been
    defending this law?" and "You were being sued as an agent of the state,
    correct?" Collapses the distinction between the AG's two capacities.

  GORSUCH -- ENGAGED_FAVORABLY
    Turn 94: By asking where sovereignty interests "run out," Gorsuch
    implicitly accepted the Bethune-Hill frame that sovereignty interests
    are at stake.

  BARRETT -- NEUTRAL/PROBING
    Turn 64: "Could you have intervened on behalf of the state qua state?"
    Probing the mechanics without endorsing or rejecting.

  NOTE: Respondent's counsel tried to flip Bethune-Hill at Turn 165: "the
  decision not to appeal is as much an exercise of sovereign authority as
  the decision to appeal." No justice picked this up.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
P-SIXTH-CIRCUIT-ERROR (AU-P2.2, AU-P2.3)
The Sixth Circuit "folded Kentucky's hand" / "refused" / "required" the
Commonwealth to accept the Secretary's decision.
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  ROBERTS -- ENGAGED_FAVORABLY
    Turn 216: Identified the catch-22: respondent had opposed the AG's
    intervention in a prior case (Friedlander litigation) before the panel
    opinion, then opposed it here after the opinion. "I wonder if that's
    really putting him in a catch-22." This tracks petitioner's "folded
    Kentucky's hand" move precisely.
    Turns 220-224: Pressed the point that the Sixth Circuit's denial was
    unreasonable given no delay and no prejudice.

  BARRETT -- ENGAGED_FAVORABLY
    Turn 103: Raised SCOTUS supervisory authority and the possibility that
    the Sixth Circuit was insulating its decision from review.

  THOMAS -- ENGAGED_FAVORABLY
    Turn 124: Questioned the Sixth Circuit's reliance on "prejudice" from
    the third-party standing argument, exposing the weakness of the lower
    court's reasoning.

  BREYER -- ENGAGED_FAVORABLY
    Turn 134: His hypothetical presupposed the Sixth Circuit should have
    let the AG in. Turn 212: Suggested remanding, which implies the
    timeliness holding was wrong.

  SOTOMAYOR -- ENGAGED_SKEPTICALLY
    Turn 25: Framed the denial as reasonable: the AG "agreed to be bound"
    and "didn't appeal."


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
P-TIMELY-10-DAYS (AU-P3.1 through AU-P3.4)
AG moved 10 days after Secretary's decision -- the "absolute earliest"
point. McDonald analogy.
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  ROBERTS -- ENGAGED_FAVORABLY
    Turn 222: "the Attorney General filed a petition for rehearing on the
    same date that it would have been due if the Secretary had still been
    in the case. So it seems a bit much to say that they were delaying
    the proceedings." Direct endorsement.

  BREYER -- ENGAGED_FAVORABLY
    Turn 134: "two days after he learns that nobody's going to defend it,
    he comes in and says, let me defend it."

  GORSUCH -- ENGAGED_FAVORABLY
    Turn 172: Hypothetical stripping away complications: "Assume the
    Attorney General hadn't been involved initially. Would it have been
    proper for the Attorney General then to intervene on appeal two days
    after getting notice?" Forces respondent's counsel to concede the
    point in the abstract.

  SOTOMAYOR -- ENGAGED_SKEPTICALLY
    Turns 37, 43: "But you didn't appeal on time." She runs the 30-day
    clock from final judgment, not the 10-day clock from the Secretary's
    decision.

  KAGAN -- ENGAGED_SKEPTICALLY
    Turns 50-60: Extended Torres hypothetical. Tests whether the AG's
    failure to appeal within 30 days is dispositive. Her clock runs from
    judgment, but she frames it as a hypothetical rather than a commitment.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
P-CONCEDE-AND-REDIRECT (AU-P3.1)
"Even if sovereignty doesn't apply, we still win on timeliness."
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  KAGAN -- ENGAGED_PROBING
    Turns 79-85: Forced Kuhn to articulate the two-prong structure
    explicitly. She tested what remained after each prong failed.

  No other justice explicitly engaged with this as a structural move,
  though all engaged with the substance of the individual prongs.

  Assessment: NOT_ENGAGED as a structural technique. The justices cared
  about the substance of each argument, not the rhetorical architecture.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
R-JURISDICTIONAL-REFRAME (AU-R1.1 through AU-R1.3)
AG's intervention is jurisdictionally barred -- agreed to be bound,
didn't timely appeal. New analytical category.
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

This was the respondent's central analytical innovation. It was the most
heavily engaged respondent move and received predominantly skeptical
treatment -- from almost every direction.

  BARRETT -- ENGAGED_SKEPTICALLY
    Turn 192: "I'm struggling to see why 28 U.S.C. 2107 is the right way
    to think about this, because it doesn't seem to me that intervention
    necessarily overlaps with 2107... He's not filing a notice of appeal.
    He's seeking to intervene. It seems like a different thing." This was
    the single most damaging question respondent's counsel faced. Barrett
    rejected the entire analytical category.

  THOMAS -- ENGAGED_SKEPTICALLY
    Turns 110-120: Pressed for precedent supporting the proposition that a
    dismissed party can simultaneously be bound and remain a party.
    Turn 116: "an example of another case where a party was dismissed but
    also remained a party?" Got no satisfactory answer.

  GORSUCH -- ENGAGED_SKEPTICALLY
    Turn 172: Stripped away the stipulation entirely to get respondent's
    counsel to concede no jurisdictional bar absent it.
    Turn 190: "So we should ignore the reservation of rights here? Is that
    the argument?" Rhetorically devastating.

  BREYER -- ENGAGED_SKEPTICALLY
    Turns 148-156: Probed "final judgment" meaning in ways that undermine
    the 30-day premise.
    Turns 200-208: "I'm trying to find in your brief where you make this
    jurisdictional argument." Openly confused about whether the argument
    was adequately presented.

  ROBERTS -- ENGAGED_SKEPTICALLY
    Turns 158, 160: Read the stipulation's reservation-of-rights language
    as preserving the AG's ability to act further, undercutting the
    jurisdictional bar.

  KAGAN -- ENGAGED_MIXED
    Turns 50-60: Took the jurisdictional premise seriously enough to test
    it during Kuhn's argument. But Turns 194, 196: "We didn't talk about
    intervention in Torres, correct?" -- skeptical of extending Torres to
    intervention.

  SOTOMAYOR -- ENGAGED_FAVORABLY
    Turns 19-25, 37: Only justice who embraced the premise. "You signed a
    stipulation... would be bound by any final judgment... You didn't
    appeal the judgment, correct?" But even she shifted to the discretion
    question by Turn 229, suggesting uncertain footing on jurisdiction.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
R-COMPETING-CLOCK (AU-R2.1, AU-R2.2)
30 days from final judgment under 28 U.S.C. 2107(a).
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  SOTOMAYOR -- ENGAGED_FAVORABLY (Turns 37, 43)
  KAGAN -- ENGAGED_PROBING (Turns 50-60, tested as hypothetical)
  BARRETT -- ENGAGED_SKEPTICALLY (Turn 192, rejected 2107 entirely)
  ROBERTS -- ENGAGED_SKEPTICALLY (Turn 222, endorsed 10-day clock)
  BREYER -- ENGAGED_SKEPTICALLY (Turns 148-152, questioned "final judgment")
  GORSUCH -- ENGAGED_SKEPTICALLY (Turn 172, eliminated the predicate)

  Assessment: One clear adherent (Sotomayor). One tester (Kagan). Four
  skeptics or outright rejectors (Barrett, Roberts, Breyer, Gorsuch).


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
R-END-RUN (AU-R3.1, AU-R3.2)
Appellate intervention can't circumvent jurisdictional deadlines.
Stringfellow cite.
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  BARRETT -- ENGAGED_SKEPTICALLY (Turn 192: if intervention and appeal are
  different procedural vehicles, there's no "end-run")
  KAGAN -- ENGAGED_PROBING (Turn 194: "we didn't talk about intervention
  in Torres" -- weakening the end-run analogy)

  Assessment: No justice endorsed the end-run framing. Respondent's counsel
  deployed it (Turns 109, 193, 197) but gained no traction.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
R-SLIPPERY-SLOPE (AU-R3.3)
If AG can do this, any tardy party could revive forfeited appeals.
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  KAVANAUGH -- ENGAGED_PROBING (Turn 97: tested whether the same rule
  applies in private litigation)
  GORSUCH -- ENGAGED_PROBING (Turn 94: asked for limiting principles, which
  is the inverse of the slippery slope -- presupposes limits exist)

  Assessment: NOT_ENGAGED in any meaningful way. No justice endorsed the
  parade of horribles. Kuhn successfully distinguished sovereign from
  private parties. Dead on arrival.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
R-ABUSE-OF-DISCRETION (AU-R4.1 through AU-R4.3)
Sixth Circuit weighed factors reasonably, didn't abuse discretion.
Uses Bethune-Hill for deference standard.
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  SOTOMAYOR -- ENGAGED_FAVORABLY
    Turns 25, 29, 229: "Why would we call it an abuse of discretion?"
    The only justice who clearly endorsed the position that the lower
    court's exercise of discretion should be undisturbed.

  ROBERTS -- ENGAGED_SKEPTICALLY (Turns 220-224: catch-22, no delay,
  "deck reshuffled" -- all aimed at showing the Sixth Circuit was wrong)
  GORSUCH -- ENGAGED_SKEPTICALLY (Turn 176: pressed whether the Sixth
  Circuit gave any weight to sovereignty)
  THOMAS -- ENGAGED_SKEPTICALLY (Turn 124: questioned the prejudice
  reasoning)

  Assessment: 1 favorable (Sotomayor), 3+ skeptical.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
R-CONCEDE-AND-REDIRECT (AU-R4.1)
"Even if not jurisdictionally barred, it was within discretion to deny."
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  SOTOMAYOR -- ENGAGED_FAVORABLY (Turn 229: performed the redirect for
  respondent's counsel by saying "assuming there's no jurisdictional
  argument..." and asking Kolbi-Molinas to defend the discretion prong)

  Assessment: Most justices focused on the jurisdictional question rather
  than the fallback, suggesting the redirect structure was less effective
  for respondent because the newer, more contested jurisdictional argument
  consumed the oxygen.


================================================================================
SECTION 2: MOVE ADOPTION IN THE OPINION
================================================================================

The opinion has four components: Alito's majority (8-1), Thomas's
concurrence, Kagan's concurrence in judgment (joined by Breyer), and
Sotomayor's dissent.

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
P-SOVEREIGNTY -- ADOPTED (majority)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

This is the structural backbone of Section III.A of the majority opinion.
Alito builds a sustained constitutional argument using language that tracks
the brief closely:

  - "our Constitution 'spli[t] the atom of sovereignty'" (quoting Alden)
  - "'retained a residuary and inviolable sovereignty'" (quoting Murphy,
    which quotes Federalist No. 39 -- the same chain the brief used)
  - "a State 'clearly has a legitimate interest in the continued
    enforceability of its own statutes'" (quoting Maine v. Taylor)
  - "a State's opportunity to defend its laws in federal court should not
    be lightly cut off"
  - 28 U.S.C. 2403(b) invoked as evidence of "the weighty interest that
    a State has in protecting its own laws"

The brief's citation set (Printz, Coleman, Hollingsworth, The Federalist)
overlaps but doesn't perfectly match the opinion's (Alden, Murphy/Fed 39,
Maine v. Taylor, Arizonans for Official English). The doctrinal argument
is the same; Alito selected his own authorities but tracked the brief's
analytical structure.

Kagan concurrence: Partially rejected. She agrees states have a
"significant interest in enforcing [their] own laws" but argues the
opinion didn't need to reach the constitutional register. Alito responds
in footnote 4, defending the sovereignty framing. This footnote exchange
exposes a real methodological disagreement.

Sotomayor dissent: Engaged and rejected. She concedes the general
principle ("All agree that States and their authorized officials have
substantial sovereign interests in defending their laws") but argues it
doesn't override estoppel on these facts.

Status: ADOPTED by 6 justices in the majority's constitutional framing.
Kagan agrees with a narrower version. Sotomayor accepts the principle but
rejects its application.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
P-BETHUNE-HILL-FLIP -- ADOPTED (majority)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Majority: "Respect for state sovereignty must also take into account the
  authority of a State to structure its executive branch in a way that
  empowers multiple officials to defend its sovereign interests in federal
  court. See Virginia House of Delegates v. Bethune-Hill."

The "flip side" framing prevails: Bethune-Hill denied intervention to a
legislative body when the AG was already defending; here, the AG should
step in when the other official stops. The majority also cites Kentucky
statutes establishing the AG as "chief law officer" with authority to
represent the Commonwealth "in all cases."

Kagan concurrence: Ignored. No reference to Bethune-Hill.

Sotomayor dissent: Engaged and reframed. Without citing Bethune-Hill by
name, she flips the principle against petitioner: "The Court makes much
of the attorney general's role in defending Kentucky law, but gives short
shrift to the manner in which Kentucky has structured its law to allow
other state officers to represent the State's interests." If the AG
deferred to that structure via stipulation, the "intramural decision"
was his original decision to step aside.

Status: ADOPTED in the majority. Sotomayor's counter-flip gets her
own vote only.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
P-SIXTH-CIRCUIT-ERROR -- ADOPTED (majority, 8-1 with Kagan)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  "[A] court fails to exercise its discretion soundly when it 'base[s]
  its ruling on an erroneous view of the law,' and that is what happened
  here. The Sixth Circuit panel failed to account for the strength of the
  Kentucky attorney general's interest."

The opinion systematically catalogs three errors: (1) wrong on
timeliness (III.B), (2) wrong on prejudice (III.C), (3) wrong on the
weight of the AG's interest (III.A). The brief's framing of the Sixth
Circuit as the agent that erred carries into every section.

Kagan concurrence: Agrees the Sixth Circuit erred, through different
reasoning (anti-circumvention rationale doesn't apply here).

Sotomayor dissent: Rejects. Defends the Sixth Circuit's exercise of
discretion.

Status: ADOPTED 8-1.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
P-TIMELY-10-DAYS -- ADOPTED (majority, Kagan)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  "[T]he most important circumstance relating to timeliness is that the
  attorney general sought to intervene 'as soon as it became clear' that
  the Commonwealth's interests 'would no longer be protected' by the
  parties in the case." -- quoting McDonald

  "The attorney general sought to intervene two days after learning that
  the secretary would not continue to defend HB 454."

  "[T]he timeliness of his motion should be assessed in relation to that
  point in time."

The majority adopts petitioner's clock completely: the trigger event is
the secretary's decision to stop defending, not the entry of judgment or
any earlier date. The McDonald analogy is cited and applied. The 10-day /
2-day timeline is the lead fact.

The majority also rejects respondents' counterargument that the AG should
have anticipated the secretary's change of course: "respondents do not
explain why the attorney general should have known that the secretary
would change course after the panel's decision."

Kagan concurrence: Reaches the same result: "The motion was instead a
response to a major shift in the litigation, creating a new demand for
the attorney general's participation."

Sotomayor dissent: Rejects. Measures timeliness from the original
stipulated dismissal years earlier.

Status: ADOPTED 8-1.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
P-CONCEDE-AND-REDIRECT -- MOOTED
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The majority adopts both the sovereignty argument AND the timeliness
argument, so the fallback structure is unnecessary. Both prongs win.

Kagan performs her own version: she drops the sovereignty framing but
still reaches reversal on procedural grounds. This is a third
concede-and-redirect that neither brief anticipated.

Status: MOOTED by total victory.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
R-JURISDICTIONAL-REFRAME -- REJECTED (9-0)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The entire Section II of the majority (Parts A, B, C) is devoted to
rejecting this argument. Key language:

  "This argument fails for the simple reason that no provision of law
  limits the jurisdiction of the courts of appeals in the way respondents
  suggest."

  "[R]espondents cite no provision of law that deprives a court of appeals
  of jurisdiction to entertain a motion for intervention that is filed by
  a non-party who is bound by the judgment that is appealed."

The majority also notes respondents "never advanced this argument below."
Even treated as a non-jurisdictional claims-processing rule, it fails
because the stipulation preserved the AG's appellate rights.

Thomas concurrence: Adds a second independent ground for rejection. Rules
3 and 4 apply only to "parties," and the AG was a non-party after
dismissal. Thomas also uses the occasion to call for overruling Devlin v.
Scardelletti.

Sotomayor dissent: Agrees. "I have no quarrel with the Court's holding
that no jurisdictional bar precluded the attorney general's intervention."

Status: REJECTED 9-0. The respondent's most ambitious analytical move
received zero votes.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
R-COMPETING-CLOCK -- REJECTED
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Rejected as part of the jurisdictional analysis. The 30-day appeal
deadline is irrelevant because the AG was not a party who needed to file
a notice of appeal. Thomas's concurrence drives this point home: "A
'party' dismissed from a lawsuit is no longer a 'party' to it after
his dismissal."

Even Sotomayor abandons the 30-day clock (she concedes the jurisdictional
point) and runs timeliness from the AG's original actions, not from the
appeal deadline.

Status: REJECTED across all opinions.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
R-END-RUN -- REJECTED (8-1, with Kagan's detailed engagement)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Kagan provides the most granular treatment. She concedes the "end run"
concern is legitimate in the abstract, but:

  "[The anti-circumvention rationale] does not sensibly apply here because
  of the change in circumstances between the time to appeal and the time
  of the motion to intervene."

  "Granting the motion would not countenance an end-run around the timely-
  appeal rule by giving the attorney general a do-over."

Sotomayor adopts the end-run framing: she quotes the Sixth Circuit's
language about intervenors having "every incentive to sit out litigation
until [a court of appeals] issue[s] a decision contrary to their
preferences, whereupon they can spring to action."

Status: REJECTED 8-1. Kagan's treatment is notable because she takes the
argument seriously and explains exactly why it fails here, rather than
dismissing it outright.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
R-SLIPPERY-SLOPE -- IGNORED (majority), REJECTED (Kagan)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The majority does not address the parade of horribles. The opinion limits
its holding to the facts: "we do not attempt to set out a general rule
governing the right of non-parties to appeal or to move for appellate
intervention."

Kagan's changed-circumstances framework implicitly provides the limiting
principle that defuses the slippery slope without naming it.

Sotomayor is the sole voice adopting this concern.

Status: IGNORED by majority. The brief's most alarmist argument was dead
before the opinion was drafted.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
R-ABUSE-OF-DISCRETION -- REJECTED (8-1)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The majority finds the Sixth Circuit abused its discretion on every
factor it considered: interest (failed to account for sovereignty),
timeliness (wrong triggering event), and prejudice (flawed analysis
of the third-party standing argument).

Sotomayor's dissent is built on this move. She argues the panel
"correctly identified" the AG's change of position and exercised
"sound discretion."

Status: REJECTED 8-1. This was the respondent's fallback position, and
it failed everywhere except the dissent.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
R-CONCEDE-AND-REDIRECT -- REJECTED (8-1)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Both prongs failed. The jurisdictional bar failed 9-0. The discretion
defense failed 8-1. Sotomayor's dissent adopts the redirect structure:
she concedes jurisdiction and pivots to discretion.

Status: REJECTED 8-1. The respondent lost on both the primary and
fallback arguments.


================================================================================
SECTION 3: CROSS-BRIEF RESOLUTION
================================================================================

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1. COMPETING CLOCKS
Pet (AU-P3.3): 10 days from Secretary's decision not to appeal.
Resp (AU-R2.1): 30 days from final judgment under 28 U.S.C. 2107(a).
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

WINNER: Petitioner (8-1)

The majority adopts the 10-day clock completely. The opinion's timeliness
analysis (Section III.B) is built around the trigger event of the
secretary's decision. The 30-day appeal clock is irrelevant because
(a) the AG was not a party who needed to file a notice of appeal, and
(b) even on timeliness, the relevant moment is when the AG learned the
defense was abandoned.

At oral argument, the 30-day clock had only Sotomayor. Roberts endorsed
the 10-day clock explicitly (Turn 222). Breyer accepted the "prompt
response" narrative (Turn 134). Gorsuch's hypothetical stripped away
the complications (Turn 172). Barrett rejected 2107 entirely (Turn 192).

Pipeline: The 10-day clock moved from brief (AU-P3.3) through favorable
oral argument engagement (Roberts Turn 222, Breyer Turn 134) into
majority adoption as the organizing fact of the timeliness analysis.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2. BETHUNE-HILL TUG-OF-WAR
Pet (AU-P2.1, AU-P2.4): Bethune-Hill's "intramural" language supports
the AG's right to take over the defense.
Resp (AU-R4.2): Bethune-Hill supplies the abuse-of-discretion standard,
which is deferential to the Sixth Circuit.
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

WINNER: Petitioner

Petitioner's use of Bethune-Hill (for the sovereignty/intramural
principle) was adopted by the majority. Respondent's use of Bethune-Hill
(for the deferential standard of review) was effectively irrelevant: the
majority found abuse of discretion on every factor, so the deferential
standard didn't save the Sixth Circuit.

At oral argument, Kagan challenged the "two hats" mechanism (Turns 79-85)
but was sympathetic to the underlying sovereignty concern. Sotomayor
rejected the framing outright. Gorsuch, Barrett, and Roberts accepted the
sovereignty premise.

Interesting wrinkle: respondent's counsel tried to flip Bethune-Hill at
oral argument (Turn 165: "the decision not to appeal is as much an
exercise of sovereign authority as the decision to appeal"). No justice
picked this up. Sotomayor attempted a version of this in her dissent but
was alone.

Pipeline: Petitioner claimed Bethune-Hill in the brief (AU-P2.1, AU-P2.4),
faced a challenge from Kagan at oral argument on the "two hats" mechanism
(Turns 79-85), and prevailed in the majority opinion which cites
Bethune-Hill for the "empowers multiple officials" principle.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3. CHARACTERIZATION CONTEST
Pet: "fight on," "pick up the mantle," sovereign succession.
Resp: "belated effort," "circumvent," "evade," "end-run."
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

WINNER: Petitioner (8-1)

The majority characterizes the AG as "taking up the defense" after the
secretary "elected to acquiesce." Language throughout is about the AG
stepping forward, not circumventing rules.

Kagan: "a response to a major shift in the litigation, creating a new
demand for the attorney general's participation." Explicitly rejects
circumvention: "Granting the motion would not countenance an end-run."

Sotomayor: Sole voice for "evade/circumvent." She calls it an
"impermissible change in position" and quotes the Sixth Circuit's
"spring to action" language.

At oral argument, more justices spoke in terms of Kentucky wanting to
defend its law (Roberts, Breyer, Gorsuch) than in terms of circumventing
jurisdictional rules. Sotomayor was the only justice who adopted "evade"
language.

Pipeline: Petitioner's characterization moved from brief through favorable
oral argument (where Roberts adopted "deck reshuffled" and Breyer said "he
wants to defend it") into the majority opinion's "taking up the defense"
/ "elected to acquiesce" framing.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4. AGENCY INVERSION
Pet: Sixth Circuit is the actor (it "folded Kentucky's hand," "refused").
Resp: AG is the actor (he "agreed to be bound," "failed to file,"
"seeks to circumvent").
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

WINNER: Petitioner (majority), Respondent gets Sotomayor only

The majority frames the Sixth Circuit as the erring agent throughout: it
"failed to account for," its assessment "was mistaken," its finding "was
similarly flawed." The AG is cast as a legitimate actor defending
sovereign interests.

Sotomayor flips this: the AG "eschewed" arguments, "changed" his
position, tries to "evade" consequences. The Sixth Circuit responded
reasonably.

At oral argument, Roberts's catch-22 framing (Turn 216) placed agency on
the court system that trapped the AG. Breyer's "why can't he just come
in?" (Turn 134) treated the denial as the obstacle. Only Sotomayor
adopted the AG-as-actor framing (Turns 25, 29, 37).

Pipeline: Petitioner's agency framing moved from brief (AU-P2.2, AU-P2.3)
through Roberts's catch-22 at oral argument (Turn 216) into the majority's
systematic attribution of error to the Sixth Circuit.

5. STRUCTURAL MIRROR
Both sides use CONCEDE_AND_REDIRECT as their transition to alternative
arguments.
Pet (AU-P3.1): "Even if sovereignty, we win on timeliness."
Resp (AU-R4.1): "Even if not jurisdictional, we win on discretion."
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

RESOLUTION: Both structures are visible in the opinion, but they yield
opposite results.

Petitioner's redirect: Mooted. Both prongs won, so the fallback was
unnecessary.

Respondent's redirect: Both prongs failed. Jurisdiction failed 9-0.
Discretion failed 8-1.

Kagan generated a third concede-and-redirect that neither brief
anticipated: she dropped sovereignty but reached reversal on procedural
anti-circumvention reasoning.

At oral argument, Kagan was the justice who surfaced petitioner's
structure (Turns 79-85). Sotomayor performed respondent's structure at
Turn 229.

Pipeline: The structural mirror was not discussed as a meta-pattern in
either the oral argument or the opinion. The justices engaged with
substance, not rhetorical architecture.

================================================================================
SECTION 5: INSIGHTS
================================================================================

What follows is drawn from a single case. Patterns identified here are
hypotheses for testing across the full dataset, not conclusions.

1. SOVEREIGNTY AND STRUCTURAL FRAMING DOMINATED THE PIPELINE

   Petitioner's brief opened with a sovereignty argument at the
   highest level of constitutional abstraction (Printz, Federalist No. 39,
   Coleman), rather than engaging the procedural question on the
   respondent's terrain. This move did three things: it reframed the entire
   dispute as a sovereignty case rather than a timeliness case; it gave the
   majority a constitutional vocabulary for the opinion that carried
   normative weight beyond the facts; and it provided an organizing
   principle that connected the specific intervention question to a body
   of law the Court cares about. Of all the moves in the petitioner's
   brief, this was the one most directly adopted by the opinion.

   Research question: Do moves that elevate the doctrinal altitude of a
   dispute (INVOKE_AUTHORITY at the constitutional level, ESCALATE_STAKES)
   outperform moves that engage on the opponent's procedural terrain?

2. THE RESPONDENT'S MOST AMBITIOUS MOVE FAILED HARDEST

   The JURISDICTIONAL_REFRAME was the respondent's most analytically
   creative argument and the one that would have been most powerful if
   accepted. Recategorizing the dispute from discretionary timeliness to
   mandatory jurisdiction would have rendered most of the petitioner's
   brief irrelevant. But it received zero votes.

   Two features of this failure are worth tracking. First, the argument
   was not made below, and the majority noted this fact. Analytical
   innovations that appear for the first time at the Supreme Court face
   a higher burden. Second, the argument required the Court to accept
   a novel legal category (dismissed-but-still-bound parties face
   jurisdictional limits on intervention) for which respondent could point
   to no precedent. Thomas exposed this at oral argument (Turn 116: "an
   example of another case where a party was dismissed but also remained a
   party?" -- getting no answer).

   Research question: Do NEW_MOVEs that create novel analytical categories
   face higher rejection rates than moves built on existing doctrinal
   frameworks? Is there a correlation between the ambition of a move and
   the risk of total failure?

3. ORAL ARGUMENT SIGNALS PREDICTED THE OPINION WITH HIGH ACCURACY

   The oral argument engagement mapped cleanly onto the opinion's
   treatment. Moves that received favorable engagement from multiple
   justices (P-SOVEREIGNTY, P-TIMELY-10-DAYS, P-SIXTH-CIRCUIT-ERROR)
   were adopted by the majority. Moves that received primarily skeptical
   engagement (R-JURISDICTIONAL-REFRAME, R-COMPETING-CLOCK) were rejected.
   Moves that were not engaged (R-SLIPPERY-SLOPE) were ignored.

   The justice-by-justice alignment at oral argument also predicted the
   final vote: Roberts, Thomas, Gorsuch, Barrett, Kavanaugh, and Breyer
   signaled petitioner-favorable at argument and joined the majority.
   Sotomayor signaled respondent-favorable and dissented. Kagan was the
   swing -- the most analytically rigorous questioner who challenged both
   sides -- and she concurred in the judgment through reasoning that
   diverged from both briefs.

   Kagan's position is the most interesting data point. She rejected the
   petitioner's "two hats" / Bethune-Hill mechanism at oral argument
   (Turns 79-85) and then wrote separately to reject the sovereignty
   framing in the opinion. But she still ruled for the petitioner,
   constructing a narrower procedural path (anti-circumvention doesn't
   apply given changed circumstances). This suggests that favorable OA
   engagement with a move's framing is not the only path to adoption -- a
   justice can reject the framing but arrive at the same destination
   through alternative reasoning.

   Research question: How often do justices who challenge a move at oral
   argument still rule for the side that made the move? Is there a
   category of "skeptical but persuaded on outcome"?

4. FACT-FRAMING MOVES OPERATED AS FORCE MULTIPLIERS

   The competing clocks (10 days vs 30 days) and the characterization
   contest ("fight on" vs "evade/circumvent") are pure fact-framing moves.
   They don't cite new authority or propose new doctrine. They select
   which facts to foreground and which to background, and they choose
   loaded or neutral language for describing identical conduct.

   These moves appear to have had outsized influence relative to their
   doctrinal content. The 10-day framing is arguably the single most
   effective move in the case because it made the timeliness argument
   intuitively obvious: 10 days sounds prompt; "years of litigation"
   sounds tardy. The choice of which clock to run determined which side's
   narrative felt natural.

   Research question: Do FOREGROUND/BACKGROUND and RECHARACTERIZE moves
   have disproportionate impact relative to their apparent analytical
   weight? Are fact-framing moves underappreciated because they don't look
   like "legal argument"?

5. THE STRUCTURAL MIRROR REVEALS ASYMMETRIC CONFIDENCE

   Both sides used CONCEDE_AND_REDIRECT as their transition to alternative
   arguments, which might suggest equal confidence. But the quality of
   the fallback positions was asymmetric. Petitioner's fallback (timeliness
   even without sovereignty) was strong -- it won on its own merits.
   Respondent's fallback (discretion even without jurisdiction) required
   deference to the Sixth Circuit and a favorable reading of the record.

   The structural similarity masked a substantive asymmetry: petitioner
   had two independently strong arguments, while respondent had one
   ambitious argument (jurisdiction) propping up a weaker one (discretion).
   When the ambitious argument collapsed 9-0, the fallback had no
   independent support.

   Research question: When both sides use CONCEDE_AND_REDIRECT, does the
   relative strength of the fallback position predict the outcome? Is
   CONCEDE_AND_REDIRECT more effective when the fallback is genuinely
   independent rather than parasitic on the primary argument?

6. KAGAN'S CONCURRENCE IS A METHODOLOGICAL CASE STUDY

   Kagan's concurrence performs an operation that neither brief anticipated.
   She rejects the majority's constitutional framing while reaching the
   same result through a procedural path -- the anti-circumvention
   rationale fails here because of changed circumstances, not because
   of sovereignty. She engages the respondent's END-RUN argument more
   seriously than the majority does, concedes it has force in the abstract,
   and then explains why it doesn't apply on these facts.

   For the move-tracking project, this raises a question about
   categorization. When a justice reaches the same outcome through
   reasoning that neither brief proposed, should that be coded as the
   opinion "ignoring" the brief moves? Or as the opinion engaging with the
   moves but reconstructing them? Kagan's concurrence appears to reject
   petitioner's framing while adopting petitioner's factual narrative
   (the "10-day" / "changed circumstances" story). The doctrinal move
   is rejected; the fact-framing move is adopted. This suggests fact-
   framing and doctrinal moves can travel independently through the
   pipeline.

7. AUDIENCE-TARGETING: WHICH JUSTICES ENGAGED WHICH MOVES

   If the brief was designed to target specific justices, P-SOVEREIGNTY
   appears designed for the conservative bloc (Gorsuch, Barrett, Roberts,
   Thomas, Kavanaugh) and succeeded with all of them. P-TIMELY-10-DAYS
   appears designed as a universal argument and succeeded universally
   (everyone except Sotomayor accepted some version of it, including
   Kagan). P-BETHUNE-HILL-FLIP was the most vulnerable move at oral
   argument (Kagan's sustained challenge) but survived into the opinion
   through the majority's adoption.

   The respondent's R-JURISDICTIONAL-REFRAME appears designed to win
   Kagan and Sotomayor by offering a doctrinal path that avoids the
   sovereignty question entirely. It got Sotomayor but not Kagan. Kagan
   took it seriously enough to test extensively at oral argument but
   ultimately declined to adopt it.

   Research question: Can we identify audience-targeting patterns in brief
   moves, and do those patterns predict which justices engage favorably?

8. DEAD-ON-ARRIVAL MOVES

   R-SLIPPERY-SLOPE received essentially no engagement at oral argument
   and was ignored by the majority opinion. R-END-RUN received skeptical
   engagement only. Both are moves that depend on downstream consequences
   rather than the facts of this case. The Court appeared uninterested in
   hypothetical future cases and focused on the specific facts before it.

   The majority reinforced this by limiting its holding: "we do not
   attempt to set out a general rule governing the right of non-parties
   to appeal or to move for appellate intervention." This case-specific
   framing directly neutralizes both consequence-based moves.

   Research question: Do moves premised on consequences in future cases
   (slippery slope, parade of horribles) perform worse than moves grounded
   in the facts and doctrine of the present case?
