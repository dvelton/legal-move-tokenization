NARRATIVE:

--- ARGUMENT UNIT P3: Section B — Intentional Property Destruction Falls Within the Local Interest Exception ---

DOCTRINAL:

- ALTERNATIVE_HOLDING: This entire section is the alternative holding argument — it only matters if petitioner loses on the "arguably protected" prong.
- INVOKE_AUTHORITY: Deep historical chain: Laburnum (1954), Fansteel (pre-Garmon), Lodge 76 (1976), Bill Johnson's Restaurants (1983). Each reinforces the same principle.
- STARE_DECISIS_SHIELD: Traces the principle to before Garmon was decided. "Even before Garmon, this Court recognized that the NLRA could not be read to preclude state tort claims for property destruction."
- APPLY_TO_FACTS: Moves from the general principle (states may regulate property destruction) to the specific claim (Glacier's tort action falls squarely within that principle).

NARRATIVE:
- PREEMPTIVE_REBUTTAL: "Even if the Union's intentional destruction of Glacier's property could somehow be deemed 'arguably protected'" — concedes arguendo only to show the concession doesn't help the Union.
- ESCALATE_STAKES: Frames the question as one of state sovereignty. Property protection is "deeply rooted in local feeling and responsibility."

FACT-FRAMING:
- FOREGROUND: "Actual or threatened violence to persons or destruction of property" — the Laburnum language is foregrounded and emphasized.
- SEQUENCE: Arranges the case law chronologically (pre-Garmon through post-Garmon) to show an unbroken historical line.

STYLISTIC:

DOCTRINAL:

- APPLY_TO_FACTS: Applies the distinction to Glacier's specific situation — concrete ordered, delivery timed, strike called to ensure concrete would harden and be destroyed.

- CONCEDE_AND_REDIRECT: Acknowledges economic harm is a permissible consequence of lawful strikes, then redirects to the categorical distinction — deliberate destruction of property is different in kind, not degree.

FACT-FRAMING:

- FOREGROUND: Emphasizes the deliberate planning and timing element — this wasn't incidental damage but intentional destruction.

1. THE CHARACTERIZATION WAR: "Intentional Property Destruction" vs. "Work Stoppage"
   - Petitioner (P1-P5): RECHARACTERIZE + FOREGROUND — Consistently labels the conduct "intentional property destruction" or "deliberate destruction of property." Never uses "strike" or "work stoppage" without qualification. This is the single most important framing move in the Petitioner's brief.
   - Respondent (R2-R4): RECHARACTERIZE + DIMINISHING_MODIFIER — Consistently labels the same conduct "the drivers' conduct," "work stoppage," or "the mere act of stopping work." 2. COMPETING USES OF DAVIS
   - Petitioner (P2): Uses Davis's "arguably protected" standard to argue the Union fails the test — "If the word 'arguably' is to mean anything," the party must show the Board could legally decide in its favor.
   - Respondent (R1, R4): Uses the same Davis standard to argue the Union satisfies it — respondent has advanced a non-frivolous interpretation not "authoritatively rejected." This is an AGREEMENT_TRAP: respondent takes petitioner's own cited authority and shows it cuts the other way.

3. THE LOCAL INTEREST EXCEPTION: SCOPE BATTLE
   - Petitioner (P3): BROADEN_HOLDING — Argues the exception is expansive, covering property destruction as the archetypal state regulatory interest. Cites Lodge 76, Laburnum, Fansteel.
   - Respondent (R2-R3): NARROW_HOLDING + DISTINGUISH — Argues the exception applies only to "arguably prohibited" conduct (under Section 8), not "arguably protected" conduct (under Section 7). Cites Farmer v. United Brotherhood for the distinction. If respondent's reading holds, petitioner's entire alternative argument collapses.

4. STARE DECISIS: COMPETING 80-YEAR CLAIMS
   - Petitioner (P2): STARE_DECISIS_SHIELD — "An unbroken 80-year string of decisions" rejects protection for property destruction.
   - Respondent (R2-R3): STARE_DECISIS_SHIELD — Congress "implicitly ratified" Garmon by amending the NLRA without changing the arguably protected standard. Both sides claim the weight of history, but they're pointing at different historical strands: petitioner at the substantive rule (property destruction is unprotected), respondent at the procedural framework (NLRB decides first).

5. THE GENERAL COUNSEL'S COMPLAINT
   - Respondent (R1, R4, R5): FOREGROUND + INVOKE_AUTHORITY — The General Counsel's complaint is respondent's factual centerpiece. After independent investigation, the agency concluded the conduct was actually protected. This "conclusively establishes" arguable protection.
   - Petitioner (P1-P5): STRATEGIC_SILENCE — In the excerpted sections, petitioner does not directly engage with the General Counsel's complaint. This silence is itself a strategic choice — engaging with the complaint would require petitioner to argue either that the General Counsel was wrong or that her determination is irrelevant, either of which carries costs.

6. LOWER COURT TREATMENT
   - Petitioner (P4-P5): FRAME_AS_ERROR + INSTITUTIONAL_REBUKE — The Washington Supreme Court "went off the rails" and "erred on both fronts."
   - Respondent (R1, R4): APPLY_STANDARD — Defends the lower court's reasoning as a straightforward application of Garmon. The lower court simply followed the framework this Court established.

7. CONSTITUTIONAL AVOIDANCE
   - Petitioner (referenced by respondent): Invokes the Takings Clause — if the Board sides with the Union, it would raise constitutional concerns, so the Court should avoid that outcome.
   - Respondent (R2-R3): CONST_AVOIDANCE (dismissal) — Premature. The Board hasn't ruled yet. Petitioner is asking the Court to avoid a constitutional problem that doesn't exist yet.

NEW MOVES
================================================================================

NEW_MOVE_PROCESS_LEGITIMATION (identified in R5):
Definition: Detailed narration of an administrative or institutional process — including specific procedural steps, regulatory citations, and evidentiary standards — deployed not primarily as factual argument but to establish the legitimacy and rigor of the process itself. The process becomes its own evidence. Distinguished from INVOKE_AUTHORITY (which cites the conclusion of an authority) in that PROCESS_LEGITIMATION argues from the thoroughness of the procedure rather than the weight of the institution. The implicit claim: an outcome produced by this process deserves deference because the process itself is sound.

Evidence: Respondent's section-by-section narration of the General Counsel's investigation (29 C.F.R. §§ 101.4-101.8), including interviews with both parties' witnesses, the evidentiary-sufficiency standard, and the dismissal threshold. Each procedural step is surfaced to argue that the complaint's issuance, as a product of this process, should be treated as dispositive on the "arguably protected" question.
