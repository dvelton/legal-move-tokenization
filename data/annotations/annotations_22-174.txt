================================================================================
LEGAL MOVE ANNOTATIONS
Groff v. DeJoy, S. Ct. Docket No. 22-174
Issue: Whether the Court should disapprove the de minimis cost test stated in
Trans World Airlines, Inc. v. Hardison, 432 U.S. 63 (1977), for evaluating
Title VII religious-accommodation claims, and instead require employers to
show "significant difficulty or expense" to establish "undue hardship."
================================================================================

NOTE ON METHODOLOGY
Each section is broken into argument units (AU). Each AU is annotated across
four layers: Doctrinal, Narrative, Fact-Framing, Stylistic. Where no move
applies, the layer is marked "--". Cross-brief interactions are flagged
with [CROSS-BRIEF] tags at the end of the relevant AU.

PET. SECTION 1: Roadmap — Hardison's Unsound Definition Of "Undue Hardship"
Lacks Precedential Force, And The Court Should Construe That Term According
To Its Plain Meaning
================================================================================

--- AU-P1.1 ---
TEXT: "Because Title VII does not define the phrase 'undue hardship,' the Court
would ordinarily construe it according to its plain meaning."

DOCTRINAL:  APPLY_STANDARD — The brief opens by invoking the default rule of
            statutory construction: undefined terms receive their ordinary
            meaning. This is not argumentative so much as it is establishing
            the baseline analytical framework. The move positions Hardison as
            the deviation and plain-meaning construction as the norm.

NARRATIVE:  AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME — The brief leads with what the Court "would
            ordinarily" do, establishing the petitioner's position as the
            unremarkable default rather than as a challenge to settled law.

FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND_ABSENCE — The brief foregrounds a statutory gap
              (Title VII's failure to define "undue hardship") and treats it
              as the controlling fact. The absence of a definition becomes
              affirmative evidence for the petitioner's interpretive approach.

STYLISTIC:  COMPRESSION — A single sentence performs multiple functions:
            identifies the statutory gap, invokes the interpretive default,
            and implicitly frames Hardison as aberrant.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--- AU-P1.2 ---
TEXT: "The Court's statement in Trans World Airlines, Inc. v. Hardison,
432 U.S. 63, 84 (1977), that an employer suffers 'undue hardship' whenever a
religious accommodation imposes 'more than a de minimis cost' departs from that
approach and contradicts Title VII's text, structure, history, and purpose."

DOCTRINAL:  DISTINGUISH — The brief characterizes Hardison's de minimis
            formulation as a departure from the standard interpretive method,
            setting up the later argument that it is dicta. The word
            "statement" rather than "holding" is doing doctrinal work.

            APPLY_STANDARD — The four-part reference to "text, structure,
            history, and purpose" invokes the full toolkit of statutory
            construction, positioning the petitioner's reading as the product
            of rigorous methodology rather than policy preference.

NARRATIVE:  FRAME_AS_ERROR — "Departs from" and "contradicts" are chosen to
            cast Hardison not as a reasonable interpretive choice but as a
            mistake. The brief is asserting error, not disagreement.

FACT-FRAMING: RECHARACTERIZE — Hardison's de minimis test is recharacterized
              from a longstanding interpretive framework into a "statement"
              that "departs" from proper method. The brief is relabeling
              what lower courts have treated as binding precedent.

STYLISTIC:  AUTHORITY_SIGNALING — The full citation (case name, volume,
            page, year) followed by the four-category statutory indictment
            signals that the brief is mounting a comprehensive textual attack,
            not a narrow doctrinal quibble.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--- AU-P1.3 ---
TEXT: "Stare decisis does not mandate adherence to Hardison's misguided dicta."

DOCTRINAL:  NARROW_HOLDING — The core doctrinal move of the entire brief is
            compressed into this sentence. By labeling Hardison's de minimis
            language "dicta," the brief removes it from the stare decisis
            calculus entirely. If the statement is dicta, the Court need not
            even perform the usual stare decisis balancing.

NARRATIVE:  FRAME_AS_ERROR — "Misguided" is a judgment on the merits layered
            on top of the procedural characterization ("dicta"). The brief is
            saying: not only does stare decisis not apply, but the underlying
            reasoning was wrong anyway.

FACT-FRAMING: --

STYLISTIC:  COMPRESSION — One sentence carries two independent arguments
            (dicta classification + substantive wrongness). The brevity
            signals confidence.

            UNDERSTATED_DEVASTATION — Calling a 45-year-old SCOTUS
            formulation "misguided dicta" in a single flat sentence. No
            hedging, no qualification.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--- AU-P1.4 ---
TEXT: "Rather, applying statutory-construction principles, 'undue hardship'
must mean, as it regularly does throughout the U.S. Code, significant
difficulty or expense in light of the employer's financial resources, the
number of individuals it employs, and the nature of its operations and
facilities."

DOCTRINAL:  BROADEN_STATUTE — The brief proposes replacing the de minimis
            test with a multi-factor "significant difficulty or expense"
            standard, substantially broadening the protection for religious
            employees.

            ANALOGIZE — The reference to how "undue hardship" is "regularly"
            defined "throughout the U.S. Code" treats other federal statutes
            (principally the ADA) as persuasive authority for construing the
            same phrase in Title VII. The move is: Congress uses this term
            elsewhere with a consistent meaning; Title VII should be read
            the same way.

NARRATIVE:  AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME — The brief states what the standard "must
            mean," projecting certainty rather than advocacy.

FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND — The brief foregrounds cross-statutory consistency
              as a factual observation ("as it regularly does throughout
              the U.S. Code"), treating the multi-factor standard as the
              established norm from which Hardison departed.

STYLISTIC:  CONCRETE_ANCHOR — The three-factor formulation (financial
            resources, number of employees, nature of operations and
            facilities) gives the proposed standard tangible content. This
            is not an abstract "totality of the circumstances" test; it
            names specific variables.

            PARALLEL_STRUCTURE — The three factors are presented in a
            parallel series that reads as a ready-made legal test, designed
            for direct adoption.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--- AU-P1.5 ---
TEXT: "Anything less disrespects the text and upsets the balance Congress
struck. This Court should faithfully construe the statute and disapprove
Hardison's flawed reading of Title VII."

DOCTRINAL:  DISTINGUISH_PURPOSE — "The balance Congress struck" invokes
            legislative purpose as an independent constraint, positioning
            the de minimis test as not merely textually wrong but as
            contrary to the statutory design.

NARRATIVE:  ESCALATE_STAKES — "Disrespects the text" and "upsets the balance
            Congress struck" elevate the consequences beyond an incorrect
            interpretation. The brief frames continued adherence to Hardison
            as institutional failure.

            FRAME_AS_ERROR — "Flawed reading" is the brief's third distinct
            characterization of Hardison as wrong (after "misguided" and
            "departs from"), reinforcing the error frame through repetition
            without identical language.

FACT-FRAMING: --

STYLISTIC:  CADENCE_SHIFT — After the dense, factor-laden preceding sentence,
            the brief closes the roadmap with two short, declarative
            sentences. The shift in rhythm signals the conclusion of the
            argument's setup.

            REGISTER_CONTRAST — "Disrespects" is a register departure from
            the otherwise formal statutory-construction vocabulary. The word
            carries a moral charge that "contradicts" or "conflicts with"
            would not.

PET. SECTION 2: Hardison's undue hardship test is dicta that lacks stare
decisis effect
================================================================================

--- AU-P2.1 ---
TEXT: "The Hardison Court was not interpreting Title VII's 1972 amendment that
defined 'religion,' rendering its undue-hardship remarks dicta with respect
to the meaning of that statute."

DOCTRINAL:  NARROW_HOLDING — The section opens with the core classificatory
            move: Hardison was construing an EEOC guideline, not the statute
            itself. If the Court was not interpreting the statute, its
            statements about the statute's meaning cannot be holdings.

NARRATIVE:  AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME — Stated as a factual conclusion ("was not
            interpreting") rather than as an argument ("should be understood
            as not interpreting"). The brief presents the dicta
            classification as self-evident.

FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND — The chronological fact that the events in Hardison
              predated the 1972 amendment is the linchpin. The brief places
              this fact at the front of the analysis.

              SEQUENCE — The implicit timeline (events occur, EEOC guideline
              applies, 1972 amendment later enacted, Hardison decides under
              old guideline) structures the entire argument. The temporal
              sequence does the argumentative work.

STYLISTIC:  --

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--- AU-P2.2 ---
TEXT: "Hardison, 432 U.S. at 66 ('At the time of the events involved here, a
guideline of the [EEOC] required, as the Act itself now does, that an
employer, short of "undue hardship," make "reasonable accommodations" to the
religious needs of its employees.') (internal citations omitted)."

DOCTRINAL:  INVOKE_AUTHORITY — The brief quotes the Hardison opinion itself
            to establish the dicta argument. The move is to use the Court's
            own words to demonstrate that the opinion was aware it was not
            applying the statute.

NARRATIVE:  --

FACT-FRAMING: STRATEGIC_QUOTATION — The key phrase is "as the Act itself now
              does," which the brief uses to show that Hardison acknowledged
              the distinction between the EEOC guideline it was applying
              and the statute as subsequently amended.

STYLISTIC:  STRATEGIC_QUOTATION — Letting Hardison's own language carry the
            argument rather than paraphrasing. The brief is signaling: I do
            not need to characterize; the text speaks for itself.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--- AU-P2.3 ---
TEXT: "'Because the employee's termination had occurred before the 1972
amendment, Hardison applied the then-existing EEOC guideline—which also
contained an "undue hardship" defense—not the amended statutory definition.'
EEOC v. Abercrombie & Fitch Stores, Inc., 575 U.S. 768, 787 n. (2015)
(Thomas, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part); see also Patterson
v. Walgreen Co., 140 S. Ct. 685, 686 n. (2020) (Alito, J., concurring in
denial of certiorari)."

DOCTRINAL:  INVOKE_AUTHORITY — Two sitting Justices (Thomas and Alito) have
            already endorsed the dicta characterization in separate
            writings. The brief stacks these citations to show the argument
            is not novel but has internal support on the Court.

            NARROW_HOLDING — The Thomas quotation restates the dicta
            argument with the authority of a Justice's concurrence, adding
            institutional weight to the classification.

NARRATIVE:  PREEMPTIVE_REBUTTAL — By showing that Justices have already
            articulated this position, the brief preempts any suggestion
            that the dicta argument is a stretch or a litigation invention.

FACT-FRAMING: OPPONENT_CONCESSION_CAPTURE — Though Thomas and Alito are not
              opponents, using their words functions similarly: members of
              the very institution being asked to overrule have already
              acknowledged the weakness in Hardison's precedential force.

STYLISTIC:  AUTHORITY_SIGNALING — The stacking of Thomas (Abercrombie) and
            Alito (Patterson) is a targeted signal to the current Court's
            composition. These are not random lower-court citations; they
            are sitting Justices on record.

[CROSS-BRIEF: Respondent's Section 1 will concede the merits arguendo
("Whatever the merits of those criticisms") but redirect to vehicle problems,
declining to engage the dicta argument directly. See AU-R1.1.]

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--- AU-P2.4 ---
TEXT: "'[E]xpressions' in an opinion that 'are beyond the point involved do
not come within the rule of stare decisis.' Humphrey's Ex'r v. United States,
295 U.S. 602, 626 (1935); see also Seminole Tribe of Florida v. Florida,
517 U.S. 44, 66-67 (1996) (Court is 'bound' by the 'result' and 'those
portions of the opinion necessary to that result'). ... if the prior case
'did not address the point now at issue,' the Court is 'not bound to follow
any dicta in the case.' Van Buren v. United States, 141 S. Ct. 1648, 1660
(2021)."

DOCTRINAL:  INVOKE_AUTHORITY — Three additional precedents establishing the
            general principle that dicta carries no stare decisis weight.
            The brief is building redundancy: even if one citation is
            distinguishable, two others stand behind it.

            APPLY_STANDARD — Having classified Hardison's statement as
            dicta (AU-P2.1 through P2.3), the brief now applies the
            established rule about dicta's precedential status. The
            two-step structure (classify, then apply consequences) mirrors
            standard legal reasoning.

NARRATIVE:  AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME — The brief frames these principles as settled
            and uncontroversial, not as contested propositions. The
            argument is: given that Hardison is dicta (established above),
            the consequence follows automatically from black-letter law.

FACT-FRAMING: --

STYLISTIC:  AUTHORITY_SIGNALING — Three SCOTUS citations spanning 1935 to
            2021 (Humphrey's Ex'r, Seminole Tribe, Van Buren). The
            temporal range signals that the principle is durable and
            repeatedly reaffirmed.

PET. SECTION 4: Requiring significant difficulty or expense accords with
the ordinary meaning
================================================================================

--- AU-P4.1 ---
TEXT: "'When a statute does not define a term, we typically give the phrase
its ordinary meaning.' FCC v. AT&T Inc., 562 U.S. 397, 403 (2011). Because
Title VII does not define the term 'undue hardship,' the Court must determine
'the ordinary public meaning of [that] term[] at the time of its enactment.'
Bostock v. Clayton County, 140 S. Ct. 1731, 1738 (2020)."

DOCTRINAL:  APPLY_STANDARD — The section opens by restating the interpretive
            default with additional authority. FCC v. AT&T and Bostock are
            recent, uncontroversial applications of the ordinary-meaning
            principle.

            INVOKE_AUTHORITY — Bostock is a strategically chosen citation.
            It is a recent Title VII case, and its textualist methodology
            was embraced by a 6-3 majority. Citing it signals alignment
            with the Court's current interpretive approach.

NARRATIVE:  AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME — The brief frames the analysis as
            compelled by method ("must determine") rather than chosen
            for convenience.

FACT-FRAMING: --

STYLISTIC:  --

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--- AU-P4.2 ---
TEXT: "'Hardship' ordinarily means 'a condition that is difficult to endure,'
'suffering,' or 'something hard to bear.' The Random House Dictionary of the
English Language 602 (1968). This word alone conveys that a business must
incur 'some pretty substantial costs' before it is excused from offering an
accommodation. Small v. Memphis Light, Gas & Water, 952 F.3d 821, 827
(6th Cir. 2020) (Thapar, J., concurring). Nothing less than a true
'hardship' can justify refusing a religious accommodation."

DOCTRINAL:  APPLY_STANDARD — Dictionary definition analysis, the canonical
            method for establishing ordinary meaning. The brief uses a
            1968 dictionary to fix meaning at the time of enactment, as
            Bostock prescribes.

            APPLY_TO_FACTS — The brief draws a direct inferential line
            from the dictionary definition to the legal standard: if
            "hardship" means "something hard to bear," then de minimis
            costs cannot qualify.

NARRATIVE:  --

FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND — The dictionary definition is foregrounded as
              objective evidence of meaning, positioned ahead of
              judicial interpretation.

STYLISTIC:  CONCRETE_ANCHOR — The 1968 Random House Dictionary citation,
            with page number, provides a tangible, verifiable source. The
            date (1968, before the 1972 amendment) is itself an argument
            for contemporaneous meaning.

            STRATEGIC_QUOTATION — Judge Thapar's colloquial gloss ("some
            pretty substantial costs") translates the dictionary
            definition into practical terms without the brief itself
            adopting informal language.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--- AU-P4.3 ---
TEXT: "But Congress went further, specifying that the 'hardship' must also be
'undue.' 'Undue' means 'unwarranted,' 'excessive,' 'inappropriate,'
'unjustifiable,' or 'improper.' The Random House Dictionary of the English
Language 1433 (1968)."

DOCTRINAL:  APPLY_STANDARD — The brief disaggregates "undue hardship" into
            its component words and defines each separately, a standard
            textualist technique.

NARRATIVE:  --

FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND — Five synonyms for "undue" are presented,
              each one carrying more weight than "more than de minimis."
              The accumulation of synonyms creates an implicit contrast
              with Hardison's low threshold.

STYLISTIC:  CONCRETE_ANCHOR — Same dictionary, same year, specific page
            number. The parallel citation structure (AU-P4.2 and P4.3
            both citing Random House 1968) creates a sense of
            methodological rigor.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--- AU-P4.4 ---
TEXT: "Accordingly, the term 'undue' indicates that a hardship must be judged
against some set of factors to determine whether it is 'excessive.' The
statute provides the context for this determination: 'the conduct of the
employer's business.'"

DOCTRINAL:  APPLY_TO_FACTS — The brief bridges from word meaning to
            statutory context. The phrase "conduct of the employer's
            business" provides the benchmark against which "undue" is

NARRATIVE:  --

FACT-FRAMING: --

STYLISTIC:  --

--- AU-P4.5 ---

DOCTRINAL:  BROADEN_STATUTE — The full proposed standard is articulated

FACT-FRAMING: --

STYLISTIC:  PARALLEL_STRUCTURE — The three-factor formulation appears for
            the third time in the brief with nearly identical wording.
            The repetition is strategic: it builds familiarity and makes
            the test feel established before the Court has adopted it.

PET. SECTION 5: Requiring significant difficulty or expense honors Title VII's
unique treatment of religion
================================================================================

--- AU-P5.1 ---
TEXT: "Defining 'undue hardship' to mean significant difficulty or expense
also accords with Title VII's unique treatment of religion. For Title VII's
other protected categories, Congress mandated mere neutrality: Employers may
not 'discriminate' on account of race, sex, etc. But Congress recognized
that religion is different."

DOCTRINAL:  DISTINGUISH_PURPOSE — The brief draws a structural distinction
            within Title VII itself: religion receives different treatment
            than other protected categories. The "mere neutrality" vs.
            "accommodation" distinction is used to argue that the de
            minimis test collapses the two categories into one.

NARRATIVE:  ESCALATE_STAKES — By positioning religion as categorically
            different from race and sex under Title VII, the brief raises
            the stakes: getting the "undue hardship" standard wrong does
            not merely under-protect religious employees; it contradicts
            Congress's deliberate structural choice.

FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND — The distinction between neutrality and
              accommodation is presented as a structural fact about the
              statute, not as a contested interpretation.

STYLISTIC:  --

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--- AU-P5.2 ---
TEXT: "To avoid religious discrimination, mere neutrality toward religion is
not enough; active 'accommodat[ion]' of religious practices is required. By
making it easy to avoid accommodations, Hardison's de minimis standard
overrode Congress's design and deferred to employers' neutral treatment of
religious and secular practices."

DOCTRINAL:  DISTINGUISH_PURPOSE — The brief sharpens the structural
            argument: if Title VII requires affirmative accommodation
            (not mere neutrality), then a standard that permits employers
            to avoid accommodation at de minimis cost effectively reads
            the accommodation requirement out of the statute.

            FRAME_AS_ERROR — "Overrode Congress's design" characterizes
            Hardison as judicial overreach, a court substituting its own
            policy judgment for the legislature's.

NARRATIVE:  FRAME_AS_ERROR — The brief frames Hardison as having frustrated
            congressional intent, converting the case from a dispute about
            word meaning into a separation-of-powers concern.

FACT-FRAMING: RECHARACTERIZE — "Making it easy to avoid accommodations"
              recharacterizes the de minimis test's operation. Employers
              applying Hardison might describe themselves as meeting a
              legal standard; the brief describes them as avoiding a
              statutory obligation.

STYLISTIC:  --

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--- AU-P5.3 ---
TEXT: "Abercrombie points the way. There, the Court explained that 'Title VII
does not demand mere neutrality with regard to religious practices—that they
be treated no worse than other practices.' Abercrombie, 575 U.S. at 775.
'Rather, it gives them favored treatment.'"

DOCTRINAL:  ANALOGIZE — The brief deploys Abercrombie as a recent majority
            opinion that supports the structural distinction drawn in
            AU-P5.1 and P5.2. The "favored treatment" language is taken
            from the majority opinion, making it binding rather than
            persuasive authority.

            INVOKE_AUTHORITY — Abercrombie is a 2015 majority opinion;
            its characterization of Title VII's treatment of religion as
            "favored treatment" is binding law that the brief uses as a
            doctrinal anchor for its broader argument.

NARRATIVE:  --

FACT-FRAMING: --

STYLISTIC:  STRATEGIC_QUOTATION — "Favored treatment" is the key phrase,
            and the brief lets the Court's own words carry the weight of
            the argument. The quotation is short enough to be memorable
            and pointed enough to do independent argumentative work.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--- AU-P5.4 ---
TEXT: "In requiring 'favored treatment' for religious practices, Title VII
recognizes that formally neutral workplace rules may discriminate in practice
against religious employees. For example, a rule barring head coverings may
appear neutral, but that prohibition will penalize Muslim employees who wear
headscarves."

DOCTRINAL:  APPLY_TO_FACTS — The brief applies the "favored treatment"
            principle to a concrete scenario, illustrating how neutrality

CROSS-BRIEF INTERACTION SUMMARY
################################################################################

1. DICTA vs. VEHICLE (Core Strategic Divergence)
   Petitioner (AU-P2.1 through P2.4) builds a comprehensive argument that
   Hardison's de minimis test is dicta lacking stare decisis force.
   Respondent (AU-R1.1) concedes the point arguendo ("Whatever the merits
   of those criticisms") and redirects to vehicle problems. This is a
   deliberate non-engagement: the respondent treats the dicta argument as
   potentially correct but irrelevant to whether certiorari should be
   granted. The concede-redirect avoids a fight respondent may not win
   while preserving the argument for another case.

2. PLAIN MEANING vs. FACTUAL SUFFICIENCY
   Petitioner (AU-P3.1 through P4.5) builds a textual case for "significant
   difficulty or expense." Respondent (AU-R1.3) counters not by defending
   the de minimis standard but by arguing that petitioner loses under any
   standard: the hardship here "far surpasses a de minimis burden." This is
   a factual flanking maneuver. Rather than defending Hardison's textual
   interpretation, the respondent argues the case is moot on its own
   facts. The interaction illustrates a common pattern: when the doctrinal
   argument favors one side, the other side retreats to the factual record.

3. ACCOMMODATION STRUCTURE vs. CBA HOLDING
   Petitioner (AU-P5.1 through P5.6) argues that Title VII's structure
   requires "favored treatment" for religion, citing Abercrombie.
   Respondent (AU-R1.2, R1.5) does not engage this structural argument.
   Instead, it identifies an independent holding (CBA preservation) that
   petitioner does not challenge. The interaction reveals that the briefs
   are operating on different planes: petitioner is litigating the
   meaning of "undue hardship" while respondent is arguing the case never
   reaches that question.

4. RFRA AS ESCAPE VALVE
   Petitioner's brief does not mention RFRA. Respondent (AU-R1.4)
   introduces it as a third vehicle objection, arguing that RFRA makes
   the Title VII question "largely academic" for federal employers. This
   is a flanking move that petitioner left unaddressed, opening a new
   argumentative front that petitioner's merits-focused brief did not
   anticipate.

5. COWORKER BURDEN: CONCESSION vs. CAPTURE
   Petitioner (Pet. 27-31, referenced in AU-R2.1) argues that coworker
   burden cannot independently establish undue hardship. Respondent
   (AU-R2.5) captures petitioner's own concession (Pet. 30) that coworker
   impact "can be relevant" and uses it to argue that no legal
   disagreement exists between the parties on this point. This is a
   classic opponent-concession-capture that converts a merits argument
   into a vehicle objection: if the parties agree on the legal standard,
   there is no question for the Court to resolve.

6. GOVERNMENT'S PRIOR POSITION (Unrebutted)
   Petitioner (AU-P3.1) highlights the U.S. government's prior position
   in Patterson calling Hardison "incorrect." Respondent's brief does not
   address the government's prior position at all. This silence is
   notable: the respondent (a federal agency) does not attempt to explain
   or distinguish the government's prior litigation posture. Whether this
   silence reflects a strategic choice (engaging would draw more attention
   to the inconsistency) or an oversight is unclear, but the result is
   that petitioner's strongest institutional-credibility argument goes
   unrebutted.

NEW MOVES
################################################################################

NEW_MOVE_[AGREEMENT_TRAP]
Definition: A move in which the respondent argues that the lower court already
applied the petitioner's proposed legal standard, thereby eliminating the
need for Supreme Court review. Unlike CONCEDE_REDIRECT (which concedes one
point to redirect to another), AGREEMENT_TRAP asserts that no disagreement
exists on the legal question presented. The move converts what appears to be
a merits dispute into a vehicle objection by claiming the parties and the
lower court all agree on the applicable rule.

Observed in: AU-R2.3, where the respondent argues that the lower court "set
forth precisely the rule that petitioner presses." If accepted, this
characterization eliminates the second question presented.

Taxonomic note: AGREEMENT_TRAP sits between CONCEDE_REDIRECT and
OPPONENT_CONCESSION_CAPTURE. It differs from CONCEDE_REDIRECT because the
respondent is not conceding and redirecting; it is asserting agreement on
the disputed point. It differs from OPPONENT_CONCESSION_CAPTURE because it
is not capturing a concession from the opponent's brief; it is
recharacterizing the lower court's holding to match the opponent's proposed
standard.
