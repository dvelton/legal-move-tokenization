PETITIONER BRIEF (Docket22-166_Brief008.pdf)

SECTION 1: I. Government Takes Property Without Just Compensation When It Collects a Debt and Keeps More than It Is Owed
============================================================

DOCTRINAL:
- BROADEN_HOLDING: Opens with Horne's statement that the Fifth Amendment protects property "without any distinction between different types." Broadens Horne's holding (which concerned personal property — raisins) to encompass financial interests like home equity.
- ANALOGIZE: Links equity to other already-protected property interests — profits and proceeds (Russello), liens (Armstrong) — positioning equity as simply another member of an established class.
- INVOKE_AUTHORITY: Stacks five SCOTUS citations (Horne, Russello, Armstrong, Phillips, Hall) in rapid succession to build a wall of precedential support before the argument even begins.
- APPLY_TO_FACTS: Applies the constitutional principle directly to Tyler's numbers: she owed $15,000, the County kept $25,000 more than that. The gap does the argumentative work.
- STARE_DECISIS_SHIELD: Invokes Phillips for the proposition that the government cannot redefine property away by legislation — preemptively blocking any argument that Minnesota's statute legitimately extinguished the equity interest.

NARRATIVE:
- AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME: The header itself does the framing — "Government Takes Property Without Just Compensation When It Collects a Debt and Keeps More than It Is Owed." The case is about government overreach, not about a delinquent taxpayer.
- ESCALATE_STAKES: Invokes Armstrong's language about barring government "from forcing some people alone to bear public burdens which, in all fairness and justice, should be borne by the public as a whole." This is not a dispute over one condo — it is a constitutional principle about fundamental fairness.

FACT-FRAMING:
- FOREGROUND: The $15,000 debt and $25,000 surplus are placed front and center. These numbers appear in the opening section and carry the intuitive force of the argument.
- RECHARACTERIZE: The County's retention of the surplus is labeled a "windfall" — a term that implies unearned gain and unfairness. The County "used" the windfall "for the public," recharacterizing routine government revenue as unjust enrichment.
- FOREGROUND_ABSENCE: No justification is offered by the County for keeping the surplus, and petitioner implicitly highlights that gap — the County simply "extinguished her equity interest."

STYLISTIC:
- CONCRETE_ANCHOR: The specific dollar figures ($15,000 debt, $25,000 windfall) make the constitutional violation tangible and immediately graspable.
- STRATEGIC_QUOTATION: Pulls Armstrong's "bar Government from forcing some people alone to bear public burdens" — the most emotionally resonant line available from the precedent.
- AUTHORITY_SIGNALING: Dense citation to SCOTUS cases in the opening paragraph signals that this is a well-grounded constitutional argument, not a policy plea.

SECTION 2: A. Home Equity Is Private Property
============================================================

DOCTRINAL:
- BROADEN_HOLDING: Invokes Gen. Motors Corp. for the broadest possible definition of protected property — "every sort of interest the citizen may possess" in a "physical thing." This breadth makes it difficult to exclude equity.
- APPLY_STANDARD: Methodically applies the Court's property criteria to equity: it can be possessed, used, and transferred. The conclusion follows syllogistically — equity "bears all the hallmarks of a property interest."
- ANALOGIZE: Connects equity to money (Koontz), drawing on the most recent and broadest Takings Clause precedent on monetary interests.
- INVOKE_AUTHORITY: Layers SCOTUS precedent (Gen. Motors, Jago, Phillips, Koontz, Crane), federal statutes (12 U.S.C. § 1715z-20, 11 U.S.C. § 522(d)), Black's Law Dictionary, and secondary sources.

NARRATIVE:
- AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME: Equity is presented as something ordinary Americans use every day — mortgages, loans, savings, financial planning. This makes it intuitively obvious that equity is "property" and puts any contrary argument in the position of denying common sense.

FACT-FRAMING:
- FOREGROUND: Emphasizes the practical, everyday uses of equity (reverse mortgages for elderly homeowners, collateral for loans, savings vehicle) to make the abstract concept feel concrete and familiar.
- SEQUENCE: Builds from abstract definition (Crane, Black's Law Dictionary) → practical uses (loans, savings) → legal conclusion (equity is "private property" under the Takings Clause). Each step makes the next feel inevitable.

STYLISTIC:
- CONCRETE_ANCHOR: References to reverse mortgages, home equity loans, and bankruptcy exemptions ground the legal abstraction in real financial instruments that any reader can understand.
- AUTHORITY_SIGNALING: Mixes SCOTUS precedent with federal statutes, state court decisions, Black's Law Dictionary, and secondary sources (National Council on Aging, Harvard Joint Center for Housing Studies). The breadth of sources signals that equity-as-property is not a contested proposition anywhere except Minnesota's tax forfeiture statute.

============================================================
SECTION 3: 1. This Court's decisions support the recognition of home equity as a property interest protected by the Takings Clause
============================================================

DOCTRINAL:
- ANALOGIZE: The central move. Equity is placed in a class with money (Koontz), interest (Webb's, Phillips), liens (Armstrong), and mortgages (Louisville Joint Stock Land Bank). If those are protected, equity must be too — it shares the same characteristics.
- INVOKE_AUTHORITY: Five SCOTUS cases cited for protected financial interests, plus partition and just compensation cases from circuit courts. The weight of authority does much of the work.
- APPLY_TO_FACTS: United States v. Rodgers serves as a direct factual parallel — the government forced the sale of a widow's home to collect her late husband's debts, but was required to return the widow's share of proceeds. The implication: if the government must protect equity there, it must protect equity here.
- BROADEN_HOLDING: Concedes the Court has not "directly said" equity is a discrete property interest, then argues the Court "consistently treats it as such." Asks the Court to make explicit what has been implicit — a classic move to extend precedent without appearing to break new ground.

NARRATIVE:
- PREEMPTIVE_REBUTTAL: Addresses the objection that equity is only a "partial interest" in property. Response: the law "routinely recognizes partial interests in property," with citations to partition statutes and just compensation distribution cases.
- CONCEDE_AND_REDIRECT: Acknowledges the Court hasn't directly held equity is protected, then immediately redirects to the consistent pattern of treatment — turning the gap in precedent from a weakness into a strength (the Court has been doing this all along; it just hasn't said so explicitly).

FACT-FRAMING:
- FOREGROUND: The Rodgers facts are spotlighted — forced sale of a widow's home, government required to return her share. This is the closest factual parallel to Tyler's situation, and petitioner gives it prominent placement.
- RECHARACTERIZE: The absence of a direct holding is recharacterized as implicit recognition — what might be seen as a gap in the law becomes evidence of consistent practice.

STYLISTIC:
- AUTHORITY_SIGNALING: Heavy citation density — five SCOTUS cases, three circuit court cases, and a state statute in a single section. The volume signals that the weight of authority is overwhelming.
- PARALLEL_STRUCTURE: The list of protected financial interests (money, interest, liens, mortgages) creates a pattern into which equity slots naturally. Each item added makes equity's inclusion feel more inevitable.

SECTION 4: 2. History and tradition confirm that equity in real estate is private property
============================================================

DOCTRINAL:
- INVOKE_AUTHORITY: Deep historical sources — Magna Carta, Blackstone's Commentaries, colonial-era cases. This isn't just legal authority; it's civilizational authority.
- STARE_DECISIS_SHIELD: Uses Horne's own methodology ("looking to history and tradition") to justify looking to history here. If the Court used this approach in Horne for personal property, it should use the same approach for financial interests in real property.
- ANALOGIZE: Draws a continuous line from Magna Carta's limits on tax collectors → Blackstone's "implied contract in law" → American practice of refunding surplus at tax sales → Tyler's case. Each historical source maps onto the current dispute.

NARRATIVE:
- ESCALATE_STAKES: By rooting the principle in Magna Carta, petitioner elevates the protection of surplus from a statutory question to a foundational principle of Anglo-American liberty. Ruling against Tyler means breaking with an 800-year tradition.
- AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME: The protection of equity isn't a modern innovation or an activist reading of the Takings Clause — it's a bedrock principle that predates the Constitution itself.

FACT-FRAMING:
- SEQUENCE: Chronological progression from Magna Carta (1215) → Blackstone (1760s) → American practice → modern cases. The unbroken timeline makes any departure feel like a rupture.
- FOREGROUND: Blackstone's "implied contract in law" to "render back the overplus" is given prominent placement. It states the rule petitioner wants the Court to adopt, and it comes with Blackstone's authority behind it.

STYLISTIC:
- STRATEGIC_QUOTATION: Blackstone's language — "bound, by an implied contract in law" to "render back the overplus" — carries rhetorical weight precisely because it comes from Blackstone.
- AUTHORITY_SIGNALING: Citations spanning centuries (Magna Carta, Blackstone, 1868 Virginia case, 2020 Michigan case, 2022 Alabama case) signal an unbroken historical consensus.
- CADENCE_SHIFT: Moves from rapid-fire modern case citations in earlier sections to the slower, more deliberate pace of historical exposition. The shift in register signals that the argument is moving to deeper ground.

SECTION 5: 3. Current Minnesota law treats equity as private property in other contexts
============================================================

DOCTRINAL:
- APPLY_TO_FACTS: Shows that Minnesota's own legal system recognizes equity as property across multiple contexts — divorce, child support, indigency determinations, reverse mortgages, judgment execution.
- DISTINGUISH_PURPOSE: Distinguishes Minnesota's treatment of equity in tax forfeiture from its treatment in every other legal context. The forfeiture statute is the outlier, not the norm.
- JURISDICTIONAL_REFRAME: Uses state law as evidence of what counts as "property" under the federal Takings Clause. Since the Court often looks to state law to identify property interests (Phillips, 524 U.S. at 164), Minnesota's own recognition of equity strengthens the constitutional claim.

NARRATIVE:
- FRAME_AS_ERROR: Minnesota's tax forfeiture statute is framed as an aberration — inconsistent with the state's own treatment of equity in every other legal context. The state can't have it both ways.
- INSTITUTIONAL_REBUKE: Implicit criticism of Minnesota for recognizing equity when it serves the state's interests (calculating child support, determining indigency) but refusing to recognize it when recognition would cost the state money.

FACT-FRAMING:
- FOREGROUND: A comprehensive list of Minnesota legal contexts where equity is treated as property: marital dissolution, child support, criminal indigency, reverse mortgages, judgment execution. The accumulation makes the tax forfeiture exception increasingly difficult to justify.
- RECHARACTERIZE: The tax forfeiture rule is recharacterized as the exception, not the norm. Minnesota "ordinarily recognizes" equity — the forfeiture statute is the anomaly.

STYLISTIC:
- PARALLEL_STRUCTURE: Repeated examples of Minnesota recognizing equity (divorce, child support, indigency, mortgages, judgment execution) create a cumulative rhythm that makes the tax forfeiture exception feel arbitrary.
- CONCRETE_ANCHOR: Specific legal contexts — reverse mortgages for elderly homeowners, child support calculations, appointment of counsel for indigent defendants — ground the argument in situations any reader can understand.
- AUTHORITY_SIGNALING: Heavy citation to Minnesota statutes and state court decisions shows petitioner has done the work of demonstrating internal inconsistency within Minnesota's own legal framework.

SECTION 1: III. Tyler Fails to State a Claim Under the Excessive Fines Clause
============================================================

DOCTRINAL:
- APPLY_STANDARD: Applies Bajakajian's remedial/punitive distinction — the Excessive Fines Clause applies only to punitive measures. If forfeiture is remedial, the Clause has no purchase.
- DISTINGUISH: Separates Minnesota's forfeiture from punitive forfeitures using three criteria: (1) it serves revenue recovery and productive land use; (2) it has no connection to criminal conduct; (3) it can benefit owners by canceling debts exceeding property value. Each criterion pushes forfeiture further from the "punitive" category.
- ALTERNATIVE_HOLDING: Hedges with a fallback — even if the Court finds the Excessive Fines Clause applies, the case should be remanded for factual development. This limits petitioner's potential win.

NARRATIVE:
- AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME: Forfeiture is framed as serving three public purposes — recovering revenue, restoring abandoned property to productive use, and ensuring finality for public revenue streams. The frame casts forfeiture as good governance, not punishment.
- CONCEDE_AND_REDIRECT: "If the Court disagrees, however, it must remand." Respondent doesn't fight to the last on the legal question — instead, it redirects to a procedural off-ramp that would prevent a ruling on the merits.

FACT-FRAMING:
- RECHARACTERIZE: What petitioner calls a "windfall" is recharacterized as "remedial" revenue recovery. The same dollars look different depending on the label.
- DIMINISHING_MODIFIER: Forfeiture "may result in a windfall for former owners because it may cancel more debt than delinquent property is worth." This flips petitioner's framing — sometimes forfeiture actually benefits owners, not the government.

STYLISTIC:
- COMPRESSION: Three independent reasons are compressed into a single sentence with numbered parentheticals. The format signals efficiency and confidence — respondent doesn't need to belabor the point.

CROSS-BRIEF INTERACTION: Directly responds to petitioner's "windfall" framing (Pet. Sections 1, 5). Petitioner says the government gets a windfall; respondent says owners sometimes get one. The word "windfall" itself becomes contested ground. Also responds to petitioner's implicit Excessive Fines argument by preemptively classifying the forfeiture as remedial.

SECTION 2: I. Petitioner Has Not Adequately Alleged Article III Standing
============================================================

DOCTRINAL:
- JURISDICTIONAL_REFRAME: The most consequential doctrinal move in the brief. Reframes the entire case from a substantive takings question to a threshold standing question. If Tyler lacks standing, the Court never reaches the merits — and every argument petitioner made about history, tradition, and Minnesota law becomes irrelevant.
- APPLY_STANDARD: Applies Iqbal/Twombly pleading standards with precision — complaint must contain "sufficient factual matter" to state a claim "plausible on its face." Allegations "merely consistent with" injury are insufficient. Must rule out "obvious alternative" explanations.
- BURDEN_SHIFT: Shifts the burden to Tyler to affirmatively prove she had equity. She hasn't alleged what liens encumbered the property or in what amounts. The absence of those allegations is treated as a failure of proof.
- INVOKE_AUTHORITY: Lujan, Summers, Iqbal, Twombly — the standard standing and pleading framework. The Court has "an independent obligation to assure that standing exists" (Summers).

NARRATIVE:
- FRAME_AS_ERROR: Tyler's complaint is framed as fundamentally deficient — she hasn't done the basic work of alleging the facts necessary for standing. This casts her as a careless litigant, not a sympathetic homeowner.
- PREEMPTIVE_REBUTTAL: Anticipates that the Court might not want to reach standing (the issue wasn't raised below). Counters with Summers — the Court has an "independent obligation" to assure standing regardless.
- AGREEMENT_TRAP: Opens with "Petitioner does not dispute that she failed to pay taxes, or the State's power to divest her of title to and control over her condominium." Uses Tyler's own concessions to narrow the battlefield and undermine sympathy.

FACT-FRAMING:
- FOREGROUND_ABSENCE: The central move of this section. Tyler's complaint doesn't allege what liens encumbered her property. It doesn't state their amounts. It doesn't affirmatively allege that she had equity after all encumbrances. Respondent treats these silences as fatal.
- FOREGROUND: "Public records, moreover, establish that Petitioner's property was encumbered" — hints that additional liens may wipe out any equity entirely. The sentence trails off, inviting the Court to wonder what those public records show.
- OPPONENT_CONCESSION_CAPTURE: "Petitioner does not dispute that she failed to pay taxes, or the State's power to divest her of title." Tyler's own concessions are captured and deployed against her.
- RECHARACTERIZE: The "$25,000 difference" between debt and sale price — which petitioner treats as self-evident proof of equity — is recharacterized as meaningless without accounting for other encumbrances.

STYLISTIC:
- UNDERSTATED_DEVASTATION: "The complaint does not even state what liens in what amount encumbered the property." The word "even" does the work — it implies this is such a basic factual allegation that its absence is remarkable.
- CONCRETE_ANCHOR: "In a single paragraph, the complaint states that 'the outstanding taxes and fees were only $15,000,' and that the property eventually sold for $40,000. That's it." The brevity of the complaint is made to feel damning.
- STRATEGIC_QUOTATION: Uses Twombly's "merely consistent with" and "obvious alternative" language — these are the phrases that transform a plausible-sounding complaint into an insufficient one.

CROSS-BRIEF INTERACTION: Directly attacks the factual foundation of petitioner's entire brief. Petitioner's Sections 1-5 all assume Tyler had $25,000 in equity. Respondent argues she never actually alleged that — she stated a $15,000 debt and a $40,000 sale, but never accounted for other encumbrances. If respondent is right, every doctrinal, historical, and state-law argument petitioner made is built on an unproven factual premise.

SECTION 3: A. Minnesota's Forfeiture Law Is Deeply Rooted In Anglo-American History
============================================================

DOCTRINAL:
- STARE_DECISIS_SHIELD: Claims deep historical roots for forfeiture — "From thirteenth century England to the present." If the practice has persisted for 700+ years, the Court should be reluctant to strike it down.
- DISTINGUISH: Distinguishes Tyler's situation from a compensable taking by introducing the concept of "constructive abandonment." Tyler didn't just fail to pay — she failed to act for five years despite ample warning. She "constructively abandoned her interest."
- BROADEN_HOLDING: Broadens the historical tradition to encompass forfeiture of "an owner's entire interest in land" — not just the tax debt, but everything. This directly contests petitioner's claim that history supports returning the surplus.

NARRATIVE:
- AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME: Forfeiture is framed as serving twin public purposes — returning "abandoned land to productive use and the tax rolls." The frame positions forfeiture as responsible land management, not government greed.
- PREEMPTIVE_REBUTTAL: Addresses the fairness concern head-on by emphasizing procedural protections — "ample notice and a meaningful opportunity to act." Minnesota gave Tyler five years, including the option to sell the property herself. The system was fair; Tyler chose not to use it.
- CONCEDE_AND_REDIRECT: The emphasis on notice and opportunity implicitly concedes that forfeiture without adequate process would be problematic. But respondent redirects: Minnesota's process was more than adequate.

FACT-FRAMING:
- RECHARACTERIZE: Tyler's failure to pay or sell is recharacterized as "constructive abandonment." She is no longer a vulnerable homeowner — she is someone who neglected her property for years despite every opportunity to act.
- STRATEGIC_SILENCE: The specific dollar amounts that petitioner foregrounds ($15,000 debt, $25,000 surplus, $40,000 sale) are completely absent from this section. Respondent refuses to engage on petitioner's chosen terrain.
- BACKGROUND: Tyler's personal circumstances — elderly, owing only $15,000, losing $25,000 — are pushed entirely offstage. The section discusses "owners" and "occupants" in the abstract.

STYLISTIC:
- COMPRESSION: Seven centuries of legal history compressed into a single paragraph. The compression itself signals confidence — respondent doesn't need to belabor the historical case.
- REGISTER_CONTRAST: The formal, institutional tone ("ensuring ample notice and a meaningful opportunity to act to avert forfeiture") contrasts sharply with petitioner's more personal register. Respondent writes as a government defending a system; petitioner writes as a person who lost her home.

CROSS-BRIEF INTERACTION: Directly contests petitioner's historical narrative (Pet. Section 4). Both sides claim Anglo-American history as support. Petitioner cites Magna Carta and Blackstone for the principle that surplus must be returned. Respondent cites the same era for the principle that failure to pay results in total forfeiture. The historical argument becomes a battle of competing strands within the same legal tradition.

SECTION 4: 1. Minnesota's forfeiture practices trace back to the Statute of Gloucester
============================================================

DOCTRINAL:
- INVOKE_AUTHORITY: Goes deeper into historical sources than petitioner — the Statute of Gloucester (1278), the writ of cessavit per biennium, Roman law (6th century), and the doctrine of escheat. The depth of historical research signals that respondent has done the more thorough work.
- ANALOGIZE: Draws a direct line from the medieval writ of cessavit to modern Minnesota forfeiture. Both involve failure to pay obligations on land, a redemption period, and total forfeiture if the deadline passes.
- STARE_DECISIS_SHIELD: The historical pedigree of total forfeiture is used as a constitutional shield — a practice this old carries a presumption of validity.
- DISTINGUISH: The writ of cessavit allowed redemption only "before judgment." After judgment, the occupant "shall be barred for ever from his entire interest." This distinguishes from petitioner's claim that surplus must always be returned — the historical rule is that the deadline is the deadline.

NARRATIVE:
- PREEMPTIVE_REBUTTAL: Directly counters petitioner's historical argument by claiming the same centuries of history support total forfeiture, not surplus return. Petitioner cited Magna Carta and Blackstone; respondent goes even further back to the Statute of Gloucester and Roman law.
- ESCALATE_STAKES: Links Minnesota's law to the fundamental sovereign power of escheat — "an English lord's authority to reclaim unoccupied and unproductive land." This isn't just a tax collection mechanism; it's an exercise of sovereign authority over land use.

FACT-FRAMING:
- SEQUENCE: Chronological narrative from 6th century Roman law → medieval English law → Statute of Gloucester (1278) → writ of cessavit. The progression creates a sense of deep and unbroken tradition.
- RECHARACTERIZE: What petitioner frames as "government taking surplus" is recharacterized as the exercise of an ancient escheat power over land that has been effectively abandoned through non-payment.
- FOREGROUND: The finality rule — delay "until [the land] be recovered by judgment" results in permanent forfeiture — is given prominent placement. The historical principle is that deadlines matter and that missing them has consequences.

STYLISTIC:
- AUTHORITY_SIGNALING: Academic legal history sources (Ibbetson, Pollock and Maitland) signal scholarly depth. These are heavyweight sources in English legal history, and citing them signals that respondent's historical argument rests on serious scholarship, not selective quotation.
- STRATEGIC_QUOTATION: "shall be barred for ever" — the medieval language carries rhetorical force precisely because of its age and finality. The archaic phrasing makes the rule feel ancient and settled.

CROSS-BRIEF INTERACTION SUMMARY
============================================================

1. THE BATTLE OF HISTORIES
Petitioner (Section 4) and Respondent (Sections 3-4) both claim Anglo-American legal history as support, but they draw on different strands. Petitioner cites Magna Carta and Blackstone for the principle that tax collectors must return surplus. Respondent cites the Statute of Gloucester, the writ of cessavit, and the doctrine of escheat for the principle that non-payment results in total forfeiture. The conflict turns on which historical strand controls — and the answer likely depends on whether the Court views this as a debt-collection case (petitioner's frame) or an abandonment/escheat case (respondent's frame).

2. STANDING AS THRESHOLD DEFENSE vs. MERITS AS ASSUMED
Petitioner's entire brief assumes Tyler had $25,000 in equity (the difference between $15,000 debt and $40,000 sale price) and builds its doctrinal, historical, and state-law arguments on that foundation. Respondent (Section 2) attacks the foundation itself — Tyler's complaint never alleges she had equity after all encumbrances, and public records suggest additional liens existed. If respondent's standing argument prevails, every substantive argument petitioner makes becomes irrelevant. Petitioner treats equity as self-evident from the numbers; respondent treats it as an unproven factual assertion.

3. COMPETING CHARACTERIZATIONS OF THE SURPLUS
Petitioner repeatedly characterizes the County's retention of surplus as a "windfall" (Sections 1, 5). Respondent's Excessive Fines section (Section 1) flips this — forfeiture sometimes creates a windfall for owners (when it cancels debts exceeding property value). The word "windfall" is contested terrain: each side assigns it to the other's beneficiary.

4. TYLER AS VICTIM vs. TYLER AS ABANDONER
Petitioner foregrounds the dollar disparity and frames Tyler as someone whose property was confiscated by a government that kept more than it was owed. Respondent (Section 3) recharacterizes Tyler as someone who "constructively abandoned" her interest by failing to act for five years despite "ample warning." This is the narrative axis of the case — and it maps onto the doctrinal axis (takings vs. escheat) and the historical axis (Blackstone's surplus return vs. Gloucester's total forfeiture).

5. INTERNAL INCONSISTENCY ARGUMENT vs. SOVEREIGN TRADITION ARGUMENT
Petitioner (Section 5) argues that Minnesota itself treats equity as property everywhere except tax forfeiture — making the forfeiture statute an indefensible anomaly. Respondent never directly addresses this inconsistency. Instead, Respondent (Sections 3-4) argues that forfeiture is a separate sovereign tradition with its own deep historical roots — the escheat power operates differently from ordinary debt collection. These arguments pass each other: petitioner focuses on Minnesota's internal logic, while respondent focuses on the distinct historical pedigree of forfeiture as a category.

6. PROCEDURAL OFF-RAMPS
Respondent builds in multiple fallback positions: (a) Tyler lacks standing (Section 2); (b) even if the Excessive Fines Clause applies, the case should be remanded for factual development (Section 1); (c) even on the merits, forfeiture is historically rooted and not a taking (Sections 3-4). Petitioner's brief is structured as a single sustained argument — equity is property, history confirms it, Minnesota agrees, and the County took it. The structural difference reflects the parties' respective positions: petitioner needs the Court to reach and rule on the merits; respondent wins by preventing that from happening.
