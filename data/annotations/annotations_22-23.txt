SECTION 1: A. The Ordinary Meaning Of "Obstruction Of Justice" Requires Interference With A Pending Or Ongoing Investigation Or Proceeding
==================================================

ARGUMENT UNIT 1.1: The generic definition of "obstruction of justice" — derived from dictionary definitions contemporaneous with the 1996 statute — requires interference with an existing process. Dictionary definitions of "obstruction," "interference," and "impede" all presuppose something already in motion.

DOCTRINAL:
- APPLY_STANDARD: Applies the standard tools of statutory construction (Esquivel-Quintana v. Sessions, 581 U.S. 385) to derive the ordinary meaning of "obstruction of justice." The framework is the Court's own: begin with statutory language, consult contemporaneous dictionaries, identify the ordinary meaning. Every step follows precedent the Court has already endorsed.
- NARROW_STATUTE: Reads "obstruction of justice" to require a nexus to a pending or ongoing proceeding. The narrowing is textually grounded — "interfering," "impeding," and "meddling" all presuppose a process that is already underway. The argument is that the statute's words have a built-in temporal requirement: you cannot obstruct something that does not yet exist.
- INVOKE_AUTHORITY: Deploys three dictionary sources: Merriam-Webster's Dictionary of Law (1996), Webster's Third New International Dictionary (1993), and Black's Law Dictionary (6th ed.). The three sources converge on the same requirement, giving the ordinary-meaning argument the quality of independent confirmation from multiple authorities.

NARRATIVE:
- AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME: Leads with the affirmative proposition — generic obstruction of justice requires interference with a pending proceeding — rather than reacting to the government's contrary position. The argument is structured as an independent derivation of meaning, not a response to a competing interpretation.
- ESCALATE_STAKES: The stakes are framed in immigration terms: if the government's broader reading prevails, then state criminal statutes that sweep beyond generic obstruction would categorically render noncitizens removable as aggravated felons. The broadening is not academic — it changes who gets deported.

FACT-FRAMING:
- FOREGROUND: Foregrounds the definitional convergence. Three independent dictionary sources all point to the same requirement. The convergence is the central fact of the argument — it is what transforms a plausible reading into the only reasonable one.
- CONCRETE_ANCHOR: The California witness-dissuasion statute (Cal. Penal Code 136.1(b)(1)) serves as the concrete example of an offense whose elements sweep beyond generic obstruction. The example grounds the abstract definitional argument in a real statute with identifiable elements. A California prosecutor need not prove interference with a pending proceeding — and that is exactly why the California offense is broader than generic obstruction.

STYLISTIC:
- AUTHORITY_SIGNALING: Esquivel-Quintana provides the methodological template. By following the Court's own interpretive roadmap step by step, respondent presents the argument as an application of settled method, not a novel construction.
- STRATEGIC_QUOTATION: "The crime or act of willfully interfering with the process of justice and law esp[ecially] by influencing, threatening, harming, or impeding a witness, potential witness, juror, or judicial or legal officer or by furnishing false information in or otherwise impeding an investigation or legal process." The full dictionary definition is quoted because its internal structure — the use of "process," "investigation," "proceeding" — does the interpretive work. Selective quotation would be less persuasive.
- COMPRESSION: The transition from dictionary definitions to real-world application (the California statute) is immediate. There is no abstract discussion of interpretive theory between the two — the dictionary says what it says, and the application follows.

ARGUMENT UNIT 1.2: The government's counterarguments rest on circular reasoning and misapplication of the categorical approach. The government does not offer a generic definition of obstruction of justice — it argues backward from specific offenses it considers "paradigmatic."

DOCTRINAL:
- FRAME_AS_ERROR: The government's argument is structurally flawed: it starts from specific offenses (like witness tampering) and reasons backward to a generic definition, rather than starting from the generic definition and testing specific offenses against it. The categorical approach requires the second method, not the first.
- APPLY_STANDARD: Restates the categorical approach's methodology — identify the generic offense, then compare state statutes element-by-element — and argues the government has inverted it. The government's error is methodological, not just substantive.
- DISTINGUISH_PURPOSE: Distinguishes between what Congress actually did (identify "obstruction of justice" as an aggravated felony) and what it did not do (identify "witness tampering" as an aggravated felony). The distinction matters because the government's argument collapses the two — if witness tampering is "paradigmatic" obstruction, then Congress effectively added witness tampering to the list without saying so.

NARRATIVE:
- FRAME_AS_ERROR: The government "does not even purport to identify a generic definition" — the criticism is that the government's argument lacks the required foundation. Before arguing about what qualifies, you must first define the category. The government skipped that step.
- PREEMPTIVE_REBUTTAL: Anticipates the government's strongest example (witness tampering as "paradigmatic" obstruction) and defuses it. Congress chose its words — "obstruction of justice," not "witness tampering" — and the categorical approach requires courts to respect that choice.

FACT-FRAMING:
- RECHARACTERIZE: Recharacterizes the government's argument as "assuming its own desired conclusion." What the government presents as an application of the categorical approach is actually a circumvention of it. The recharacterization strips the argument of its methodological legitimacy.
- FOREGROUND_ABSENCE: Foregrounds the absence of a generic definition in the government's brief. The government argues about specific offenses without first establishing what the generic category contains. The absence is not accidental — it is the structural weakness of the government's position.

STYLISTIC:
- UNDERSTATED_DEVASTATION: "The government does not even purport to identify a generic definition of 'obstruction of justice.'" A single sentence that identifies the foundational gap in the government's argument. No elaboration is needed — the observation is self-executing.
- REGISTER_CONTRAST: Shifts from the measured, dictionary-driven analysis of the preceding section to a more pointed critique of the government's methodology. The tonal shift signals that respondent is moving from construction to demolition.

Using the BIA's own prior position against its current one is a form of doctrinal judo.

The move is significant because Section 1503's omnibus clause requires a nexus to a "pending" judicial proceeding. If Section 1503 is the generic statute, then a pending-proceeding requirement is baked into the generic definition.

NARRATIVE:
- AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME: Frames the Chapter 73 analysis as confirmatory rather than primary. STYLISTIC:

Rev. 657 (2021)) — signals that the pending-proceeding requirement is not a fringe position but the settled understanding across institutional perspectives.
- CONCRETE_ANCHOR: Section 1503 and its omnibus clause provide the concrete statutory anchor.
