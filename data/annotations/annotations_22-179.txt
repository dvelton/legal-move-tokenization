LEGAL MOVE ANNOTATIONS
United States v. Hansen, S. Ct. Docket No. 22-179
Issue: Whether 8 U.S.C. 1324(a)(1)(A)(iv), which criminalizes encouraging
or inducing a noncitizen to come to, enter, or reside in the United States
in violation of law, is facially unconstitutional under the First Amendment
overbreadth doctrine.
================================================================================

NOTE ON METHODOLOGY
Each section is broken into argument units (AU). Each AU is annotated across
four layers: Doctrinal, Narrative, Fact-Framing, Stylistic. Where no move
applies, the layer is marked "--". Cross-brief interactions are flagged
with [CROSS-BRIEF] tags at the end of the relevant AU.

--- AU-P1.1 (Inferred from Table of Authorities and Respondent's Citations) ---
TEXT: [No direct text available. Inferred from authority citations and
respondent's references to "U.S. Br. 21-24" and the government's proposed
narrowing construction.]

The petitioner argues that "encourage" should be construed as a term of art
in criminal law that narrowly prohibits only solicitation or aiding and
abetting. (See Resp. Br.: "it argues that the statute can be saved by
construing 'encourage' as a term of art in criminal law that narrowly
prohibits only solicitation or aiding and abetting.")

DOCTRINAL:  NARROW_STATUTE — The central doctrinal move is to narrow the
            statutory term "encourage" from its ordinary dictionary meaning
            to a term of art carrying only the technical criminal-law meaning
            of solicitation or facilitation. This transforms the scope of the
            statute from covering all speech that inspires or emboldens to
            covering only conduct-adjacent speech.

            CONST_AVOIDANCE — The narrowing construction is offered as a
            constitutional-avoidance maneuver: if the statute can be read
            narrowly, the Court need not reach the overbreadth question.
            The citation of INS v. St. Cyr, 533 U.S. 289 (2001) in the
            Table of Authorities confirms this doctrinal tool is in play.

NARRATIVE:  AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME — Rather than defending the statute's breadth,
            the government recharacterizes the statute as always having
            meant solicitation or aiding and abetting. The position is: the
            statute is not broad; it has been misread as broad.

FACT-FRAMING: RECHARACTERIZE — The ordinary-meaning reading of "encourage"
              is recharacterized as a misinterpretation. The government
              treats the narrower criminal-law meaning as the statute's
              actual meaning rather than as a saving gloss imposed on top
              of the text.

DOCTRINAL:  APPLY_STANDARD — Williams provides the test: overbreadth

NARRATIVE:  FRAME_AS_ERROR — The implicit frame is that the Ninth Circuit
            committed a methodological error in the overbreadth analysis,

FACT-FRAMING: --

STYLISTIC:  AUTHORITY_SIGNALING — Heavy citation of this Court's own

RESP. SECTION 1: Introduction — The Encouragement Provision is Overbroad
Because It Prohibits a Substantial Amount of Protected Speech Well Beyond
Solicitation or Aiding and Abetting
================================================================================

--- AU-R1.1 ---
TEXT: "The statute Mr. Hansen was convicted of violating makes it a crime to
encourage noncitizens to reside in the country in violation of law. It
criminalizes such speech whether or not the speaker intends a law violation
to occur, and whether or not the violation is even a crime."

DOCTRINAL:  BROADEN_STATUTE — The respondent opens by stating the statute's
            scope at its broadest: it criminalizes "encouragement" without
            requiring intent or a predicate crime. This reading maximizes
            the surface area of the First Amendment conflict by removing
            both scienter and conduct elements that might limit the
            statute's reach.

NARRATIVE:  ESCALATE_STAKES — The twin "whether or not" clauses escalate
            the constitutional problem. The statute does not merely
            overcriminalize; it criminalizes speech regardless of two
            features (intent and predicate criminality) that are normally
            prerequisites for removing speech from First Amendment
            protection.

FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND — The respondent foregrounds the statute's
              structural deficiencies (no intent requirement, no predicate-
              crime requirement) as the lead facts. These are presented
              as features of the statute itself, not as contested
              characterizations.

STYLISTIC:  PARALLEL_STRUCTURE — "Whether or not the speaker intends a law
            violation to occur, and whether or not the violation is even a
            crime." The parallel clauses create an accumulating-deficiency
            structure: each "whether or not" adds a missing constitutional
            safeguard.

            COMPRESSION — The entire overbreadth argument is compressed
            into two sentences. The respondent opens with the complete
            theory rather than building to it.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--- AU-R1.2 ---
TEXT: "As the court of appeals held, it prohibits 'knowingly telling an
undocumented immigrant "I encourage you to reside in the United States."'
Pet. App. 11a. Yet 'such a statement is protected by the First Amendment.'
Id. (citing Williams, 553 U.S. at 300)."

DOCTRINAL:  APPLY_TO_FACTS — The respondent converts the abstract
            overbreadth argument into a concrete hypothetical drawn from
            the court of appeals' own analysis. The move is doctrinal
            because it tests the statute against a specific application
            and shows that application is unconstitutional.

            INVOKE_AUTHORITY — The citation to Williams is critical.
            Williams is the government's own favored precedent in the
            overbreadth context, and the respondent turns it against the
            government by citing the passage where the Court said
            encouraging illegal conduct can be protected speech.

NARRATIVE:  CONCRETE_ANCHOR — The quoted hypothetical ("I encourage you to
            reside in the United States") gives the constitutional
            argument a tangible example. The overbreadth claim is no
            longer abstract; it has a face.

FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND — The respondent foregrounds the most sympathetic
              application of the statute: criminalizing a bare statement
              of encouragement. This is chosen because it maximizes the
              gap between the statute's reach and the First Amendment's
              protections.

STYLISTIC:  STRATEGIC_QUOTATION — The respondent quotes both the lower
            court and this Court (via Williams). The effect is to present
            the unconstitutionality finding not as the respondent's
            argument but as a conclusion already reached by two courts.

            CADENCE_SHIFT — The short declarative "Yet 'such a statement
            is protected by the First Amendment'" follows the longer
            setup sentence. The brevity does the work of emphasis.

[CROSS-BRIEF: This AU directly responds to petitioner's narrowing-
construction argument (AU-P1.1). The respondent's point is that under
the statute's actual text, the hypothetical is criminal. The narrowing
construction must be assessed against this textual reality.]

TEXT: "The government does not dispute that if the encouragement provision
is interpreted according to its ordinary meaning, it is unconstitutional."

DOCTRINAL:  --

NARRATIVE:  OPPONENT_CONCESSION_CAPTURE — This is the most important

FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND — The government's non-dispute is foregrounded as

STYLISTIC:  COMPRESSION — A single sentence captures the entire strategic

            UNDERSTATED_DEVASTATION — The sentence is flat and declarative.

'encourage' as a term of art in criminal law that narrowly prohibits only

NARRATIVE:  PREEMPTIVE_REBUTTAL — The respondent preempts the narrowing

FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND — The legislative-history fact (deletion of

              SEQUENCE — The respondent sequences the legislative history

STYLISTIC:  CONCRETE_ANCHOR — "Congress deleted the terms 'solicit' and
            'assist'" is a specific, verifiable fact. The argument does
            not rest on interpretive theory; it rests on what Congress

[CROSS-BRIEF: This AU is the direct counter to petitioner's central

RESP. SECTION 2: A. The Statute's Plain Meaning Reaches Any Speech That
Persuades or Encourages a Noncitizen to Remain in Violation of Law
================================================================================

--- AU-R2.1 ---
TEXT: "The ordinary meaning of the encouragement provision prohibits mere
encouragement of violations of immigration law. The statute requires no
proof of intent to solicit, of the likelihood of an imminent violation,
or of conduct constituting aiding and abetting. It proscribes encouragement,
full stop, and therefore includes a substantial amount of protected speech."

DOCTRINAL:  BROADEN_STATUTE — The respondent restates the broadening
            argument with greater precision. Three elements that would
            normally limit a speech-restricting statute are identified as
            absent: (1) intent to solicit, (2) imminence of violation,
            (3) aiding-and-abetting conduct. Each absent element maps
            onto a distinct doctrinal tradition (solicitation, Brandenburg
            incitement, accomplice liability).

NARRATIVE:  AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME — "It proscribes encouragement, full stop"
            presents the statute's breadth as a plain textual fact, not
            as the respondent's contestable reading. The period after
            "full stop" is doing argumentative work: the brevity asserts
            that no further analysis is required.

FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND_ABSENCE — The respondent foregrounds three
              elements the statute lacks. The argument is built on
              absences: what the statute does not require is more
              important than what it does require.

STYLISTIC:  PARALLEL_STRUCTURE — "No proof of intent to solicit, of the
            likelihood of an imminent violation, or of conduct constituting
            aiding and abetting." The three-part series catalogues the
            missing safeguards in a structure that invites comparison
            with standards that do include them.

            CADENCE_SHIFT — "It proscribes encouragement, full stop."
            After the longer analytical sentence, the short declarative
            sentence closes the argument with finality.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--- AU-R2.2 ---
TEXT: "The primary dictionary definition of 'encourage' is 'to inspire with
courage, spirit, or hope . . . to spur on . . . to give help or patronage
to.' Merriam-Webster's Collegiate Dictionary 380 (10th ed. 2001); see also
Oxford English Dictionary 216 (2d ed. 1989) ('[t]o inspire with courage,
animate, inspirit'); Webster's Third New International Dictionary 747 (15th
ed. 1966) ('[to] inspire with courage, spirit, or hope')."

DOCTRINAL:  APPLY_STANDARD — The respondent applies the standard tool of
            ordinary-meaning statutory construction: dictionary definitions.
            The move is conventional, but the execution is strategic.
            Multiple dictionaries are cited to establish that "encourage"
            carries the broad meaning across every major lexicographic
            source.

NARRATIVE:  --

FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND — The respondent foregrounds the broadest available
              definitions. Every dictionary cited defines "encourage" in
              terms that encompass protected speech (inspire, spur on,
              embolden). The definitions are selected to maximize the gap
              between the word's ordinary meaning and the government's
              proposed criminal-law term-of-art construction.

STYLISTIC:  AUTHORITY_SIGNALING — The stacking of four dictionaries
            (Merriam-Webster, Oxford, Webster's Third, and below Black's)
            serves a cumulative-authority function. The respondent is
            arguing that every lexicographic source agrees on the broad
            meaning; the government's narrow reading is an outlier.

            CONCRETE_ANCHOR — Direct dictionary quotations anchor the
            interpretive argument in verifiable textual sources rather
            than in the respondent's characterization.

--- AU-R2.3 ---
TEXT: "Even Black's Law Dictionary, which the government cites, defines
'encourage' in the first instance as '[t]o instigate; to incite to action;
to embolden; to help.' Black's Law Dictionary 620 (11th ed. 2019). The
edition in force when Congress in 1986 enacted the provision in its current
state used additional expansive verbs to define 'encourage': 'to instigate;
to incite to action; to give courage to; to inspirit; to embolden; to raise
confidence; to make confident; to help; to forward; to advise.' Black's Law

DOCTRINAL:  APPLY_STANDARD — Continued dictionary analysis, but now
            targeted at the government's own cited source.

            DISTINGUISH — The respondent distinguishes the government's

            The government cited Black's for the proposition that
            "encourage" is a criminal-law term of art; the respondent

NARRATIVE:  OPPONENT_CONCESSION_CAPTURE — "Even Black's Law Dictionary,
            which the government cites" turns the government's own source
            against it. The word "Even" signals that the respondent is
            working with the government's preferred authority and still
            winning the textual argument.

FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND — The respondent foregrounds the 1979 edition of
              Black's (the edition contemporaneous with the 1986 statutory

              current edition. The temporal alignment between the
              dictionary edition and the enactment date strengthens the

              SEQUENCE — The respondent sequences the dictionary evidence
              to tell a damaging story for the government: the current
              Black's definition is broad, and the definition from the

STYLISTIC:  STRATEGIC_QUOTATION — The respondent quotes Black's at length,

--- AU-R2.4 ---
TEXT: "The disjunctive prohibition on 'induce[ment]' likewise reaches beyond
criminal solicitation. 'Induce' means '[t]o lead on, to influence, to

DOCTRINAL:  BROADEN_STATUTE — The respondent extends the broadening
            argument to the statute's second operative verb, "induce."
            Like "encourage," "induce" carries an ordinary meaning far
            broader than criminal solicitation. The respondent is closing
            off the government's fallback: even if "encourage" could be
            narrowed, "induce" independently sweeps in protected speech.

NARRATIVE:  --

FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND — The respondent foregrounds the disjunctive
              statutory structure. Because the statute prohibits

              term if the other remains broad.

STYLISTIC:  COMPRESSION — A short, efficient unit. The respondent does
            not belabor the point; the dictionary definition speaks for

RESP. SECTION 3: B. Speech Encouraging Violations of Law is Protected by
the First Amendment Except in Very Narrow Circumstances
================================================================================

--- AU-R3.1 ---
TEXT: "This Court has long held that encouraging lawless action is
constitutionally protected except in exceedingly narrow circumstances. See
Brandenburg, 395 U.S. at 447 ('advocacy of the use of force or of law
violation' is protected by the First Amendment) (emphasis added); Williams,
553 U.S. at 298-99 (noting 'an important distinction between a proposal
to engage in illegal activity and the abstract advocacy of illegality')."

DOCTRINAL:  INVOKE_AUTHORITY — Brandenburg and Williams are the two
            canonical authorities on the distinction between protected
            advocacy and unprotected incitement/solicitation. The
            respondent invokes both at the section's outset to establish
            the constitutional baseline: speech encouraging law violation
            is the norm for protection, not the exception.

            APPLY_STANDARD — The respondent frames the First Amendment
            test as requiring "exceedingly narrow circumstances" before
            advocacy of illegality loses protection. This is an accurate
            statement of the standard that simultaneously maximizes its
            protective scope.

NARRATIVE:  AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME — "This Court has long held" positions the
            respondent's constitutional argument as settled law, not as a
            novel proposition. The respondent is not asking for new
            protection; it is asking for the application of existing
            doctrine.

FACT-FRAMING: --

STYLISTIC:  AUTHORITY_SIGNALING — Brandenburg and Williams are cited
            together in the opening sentence. The pairing signals that
            the respondent's First Amendment argument rests on the
            Court's most authoritative speech-protection precedents.

            STRATEGIC_QUOTATION — The respondent quotes Brandenburg's
            reference to "law violation" (emphasis added) to draw an
            explicit link between the Brandenburg principle and the
            statute at issue. The emphasis signals that this is not a
            general citation but a targeted application.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--- AU-R3.2 ---
TEXT: "Indeed, in its canonical First Amendment precedent, Brandenburg,
395 U.S. 444, the Court held that even speech that directly advocates
violent crime is protected unless it is (1) intended to and (2) likely to
produce (3) imminent lawless action. Id. at 447. The encouragement provision
contains none of these elements."

DOCTRINAL:  APPLY_STANDARD — The respondent sets out the three-part
            Brandenburg test: (1) intent, (2) likelihood, (3) imminence.
            Each element is a limiting principle that the encouragement
            provision lacks.

            BROADEN_HOLDING — The respondent broadens the force of
            Brandenburg by emphasizing that it protects "even speech that
            directly advocates violent crime." If advocacy of violent crime
            is protected absent intent/likelihood/imminence, then advocacy
            of civil immigration violations is a fortiori protected.

NARRATIVE:  ESCALATE_STAKES — The a fortiori structure escalates the
            constitutional stakes: the government is asking for less First
            Amendment protection for immigration-law speech than the Court
            provides for advocacy of violent crime. The implicit
            comparison makes the government's position seem extreme.

FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND_ABSENCE — "The encouragement provision contains
              none of these elements." The respondent foregrounds the
              statute's failure to include any of the three Brandenburg
              requirements. Three absences, catalogued.

STYLISTIC:  COMPRESSION — "The encouragement provision contains none of
            these elements." After the three-part Brandenburg test, a
            single sentence delivers the conclusion: zero out of three.

            UNDERSTATED_DEVASTATION — The flat factual sentence following
            the formal recitation of the three-element test. No argument
            is needed; the gap between the standard and the statute is
            self-evident.

[CROSS-BRIEF: This AU responds to petitioner's likely argument (AU-P1.3)
that the statute targets unprotected conduct-adjacent speech. The
respondent counters that even under the government's own doctrinal
framework (integral-to-criminal-conduct), the statute fails because it
lacks the limiting elements Brandenburg requires.]

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--- AU-R3.3 ---
TEXT: "The First Amendment also does not protect 'speech . . . integral [to]
conduct in violation of a valid criminal statute.' Giboney, 336 U.S. at 498;
United States v. Stevens, 559 U.S. 460, 471 (2010) (explaining that speech
loses its constitutional protection only when 'used as an integral part of
conduct in violation of a valid criminal statute') (emphasis added). But the
encouragement provision extends far beyond speech 'integral' to criminal
conduct because it reaches all encouragement."

DOCTRINAL:  CONCEDE_REDIRECT (used as NARRATIVE here but operating
            doctrinally) — The respondent concedes the existence of the
            integral-to-criminal-conduct exception, then immediately
            distinguishes it. The concession is genuine: speech integral
            to crime is unprotected. The redirect is that the statute
            sweeps far past that exception.

            DISTINGUISH — The respondent distinguishes the encouragement
            provision from statutes that target speech "integral" to
            criminal conduct. The statute's reach is not limited to
            speech integral to crime; it reaches "all encouragement."

NARRATIVE:  CONCEDE_REDIRECT — The "also does not protect" language
            concedes the government's strongest doctrinal point, then the
            "But" pivots to the respondent's answer: the statute is not
            limited to that unprotected category.

FACT-FRAMING: RECHARACTERIZE — The respondent recharacterizes the statute's
              scope. Under the government's framing, the statute targets
              the unprotected integral-to-conduct category. Under the
              respondent's framing, the statute happens to include that
              category but extends far beyond it.

STYLISTIC:  STRATEGIC_QUOTATION — The respondent quotes Stevens with the
            Court's own emphasis on "integral part." The emphasis is
            Stevens's, but the respondent deploys it to underscore how
            far the encouragement provision departs from the integral-
            part standard.

            REGISTER_CONTRAST — "All encouragement" following the formal
            legal standard ("integral part of conduct in violation of a
            valid criminal statute") creates a register contrast. The
            formality of the legal standard next to the plain breadth of
            "all encouragement" makes the statute's overreach audible.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--- AU-R3.4 ---
TEXT: "In Williams, this Court stated that the First Amendment protects the
statement, 'I encourage you to obtain child pornography,' absent proof of
intentional solicitation of a specific crime. 553 U.S. at 294, 298, 299-300.
Yet the encouragement provision criminalizes the statement 'I encourage you
to reside in the United States,' see Pet. App. 11a, without proof of intent
to solicit an unlawful act; the law requires merely that the defendant know
or recklessly disregard the unlawful status of the noncitizen."

DOCTRINAL:  ANALOGIZE — The respondent analogizes the encouragement
            provision to the hypothetical in Williams. If the First
            Amendment protects "I encourage you to obtain child
            pornography" (absent solicitation intent), then it necessarily
            protects "I encourage you to reside in the United States"
            (a far less serious category of illegal conduct).

            APPLY_TO_FACTS — The respondent applies the Williams principle
            to the specific statutory language at issue, producing a
            concrete constitutional clash.

NARRATIVE:  ESCALATE_STAKES — The juxtaposition of child pornography
            encouragement (protected under Williams) with immigration
            encouragement (criminalized under the statute) creates an
            irresistible a fortiori argument. If encouraging the more
            serious crime is protected, criminalizing encouragement of
            the less serious violation is constitutionally untenable.

FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND — The respondent foregrounds the comparison
              between child pornography and immigration violations. The
              comparison is chosen for maximum impact: the reader must
              confront the fact that the statute criminalizes speech less
              serious than what Williams held protected.

              RECHARACTERIZE — The statute's scienter requirement ("know

CROSS-BRIEF INTERACTION SUMMARY
################################################################################

1. THE NARROWING-CONSTRUCTION BATTLE (Central Strategic Clash)
   Petitioner (AU-P1.1) proposes construing "encourage" as a criminal-law
   term of art meaning only solicitation or aiding and abetting, invoking
   the constitutional-avoidance canon. Respondent (AU-R1.4, R2.2-R2.4,
   R5.1-R5.3) attacks this construction from multiple directions:
   (a) the dictionaries uniformly define "encourage" broadly; (b) Congress
   deleted the narrower terms "solicit" and "assist" from the statute;
   (c) the statute is unambiguous, so constitutional avoidance does not
   apply; (d) Bostock and Stevens prohibit judicial rewriting. The
   petitioner's entire case depends on the viability of this narrowing
   construction; the respondent devotes three full sections to
   demolishing it. This is the defining cross-brief interaction.

2. COMPETING USES OF WILLIAMS
   Both sides cite United States v. Williams, 553 U.S. 285 (2008), but
   for opposite propositions. Petitioner (AU-P1.2) cites Williams for
   the overbreadth standard: invalidation requires a "substantial number"
   of unconstitutional applications. Respondent (AU-R3.4) cites Williams
   for the substantive First Amendment holding: the statement "I encourage
   you to obtain child pornography" is protected absent proof of
   intentional solicitation. The respondent's use of Williams is more
   tactically effective because it turns the government's own authority
   into an a fortiori argument: if encouraging child pornography is
   protected, encouraging immigration violations must be as well.

3. CONCESSION EXPLOITATION
   The respondent systematically captures the government's concessions
   and deploys them as structural pillars of its argument. Three distinct
   concessions are captured: (a) the government does not dispute that the
   statute is unconstitutional under its ordinary meaning (AU-R1.3, R5.1);
   (b) Black's Law Dictionary, cited by the government, defines "encourage"
   broadly (AU-R2.3); (c) the government rejected the narrowing
   construction at trial (AU-R1.5). Each concession-capture is positioned
   at a strategic moment in the brief: the first in the introduction,
   the second in the plain-meaning section, the third as the introduction's
   closing blow.

4. BRANDENBURG VS. INTEGRAL-TO-CONDUCT
   The petitioner's brief structure (inferred from authority citations)
   suggests a two-track argument: (a) under the narrowing construction,
   the statute reaches only unprotected conduct-adjacent speech; (b) even
   under the ordinary meaning, some categories of covered speech
   (solicitation, aiding and abetting) are unprotected. The respondent
   (AU-R3.1-R3.3) responds by establishing the First Amendment baseline
   through Brandenburg: even advocacy of violent crime is protected absent
   intent, likelihood, and imminence. The respondent then concedes the
   integral-to-conduct exception (AU-R3.3) but distinguishes it: the
   statute is not limited to speech integral to criminal conduct. The
   cross-brief dynamic forces the Court to choose between the
   petitioner's narrow framing (the statute targets solicitation) and
   the respondent's broad framing (the statute targets all encouragement).

5. LEGISLATIVE HISTORY AS DISPOSITIVE EVIDENCE
   The respondent's strongest single move (AU-R1.4) is the legislative-
   history argument that Congress deleted "solicit" and "assist" from the
   statute. This move goes unrebutted in the available petitioner text.
   The Table of Authorities cites the Illegal Immigration Reform and
   Immigrant Responsibility Act of 1996, which suggests the petitioner
   engaged with legislative history, but the specific response to the
   deletion argument is not visible. The respondent's deployment of this
   fact in the introduction signals its centrality; if the petitioner's
   response is weak, this becomes the case-defining point.

6. BONTA AS RECENT PRECEDENT
   Both briefs cite Americans for Prosperity Foundation v. Bonta, 141
   S. Ct. 2373 (2021). The respondent (AU-R4.5) uses Bonta for its
   "dramatic mismatch" language and its holding that less restrictive
   alternatives defeated the statute. The petitioner cites Bonta at page
   10 of its brief, likely attempting to distinguish it (perhaps arguing
   that the encouragement provision serves a more compelling interest
   than charitable-donor disclosure). The available text does not reveal
   the petitioner's specific Bonta argument, but the citation confirms
   engagement with this precedent.

7. TRIAL-LEVEL ESTOPPEL (Unrebutted in Available Text)
   The respondent's argument that the government rejected the narrowing
   construction at trial (AU-R1.5, referencing J.A. 99-101 and Part III)
   is one of the most tactically damaging moves in the brief. It
   transforms the narrowing construction from an interpretive argument
   into an act of appellate opportunism: the government tried the case
   on the broad reading and now seeks a narrow reading on appeal. No
   response to this argument appears in the available petitioner text.

NEW MOVES
################################################################################

NEW_MOVE_[AUTHORITY_INVERSION]
Definition: A move in which one side takes an authority cited by the
opposing side and deploys it for the opposite proposition, demonstrating
that the authority actually supports the citing party's position rather
than the opponent's. Distinguished from DISTINGUISH (which neutralizes an
opposing authority) and INVOKE_AUTHORITY (which cites a favorable
authority). AUTHORITY_INVERSION affirmatively captures the opponent's
authority and turns it against them.

Observed in: AU-R2.3 (Black's Law Dictionary, cited by the government for
the term-of-art argument, is shown to define "encourage" broadly) and
AU-R3.4 (Williams, the government's primary overbreadth authority, is
cited for its substantive holding that encouraging illegal conduct can
be protected speech).

Taxonomic note: AUTHORITY_INVERSION is more aggressive than DISTINGUISH.
Where DISTINGUISH neutralizes an unfavorable authority by showing it does
not apply, AUTHORITY_INVERSION captures a favorable authority from the
opponent's brief and redeploys it. The move is especially effective
because it weakens the opponent's position while simultaneously
strengthening the inverter's position with a source the opponent already
endorsed.

NEW_MOVE_[APPELLATE_POSITION_REVERSAL]
Definition: A move in which one side exposes the other's inconsistency
between its position below (at trial or in a lower court) and its position
on appeal. Unlike OPPONENT_CONCESSION_CAPTURE (which captures a concession
within the current briefing), APPELLATE_POSITION_REVERSAL reaches back to
the opponent's conduct in prior proceedings to show that the current
appellate argument contradicts the position the opponent actually litigated.

Observed in: AU-R1.5, where the respondent points out that the government
rejected any narrowing construction when this case was tried to the jury
(citing the jury instructions at J.A. 99-101), yet now asks the Court to
adopt that same narrowing construction on appeal.

Taxonomic note: APPELLATE_POSITION_REVERSAL goes beyond
OPPONENT_CONCESSION_CAPTURE because it involves a temporal dimension: the
inconsistency is between the opponent's past conduct and present argument,
not between two statements in the same brief. It functions as a credibility
attack: if the government did not believe in the narrowing construction
when it mattered (at trial), its current advocacy of that construction is
opportunistic rather than principled.
