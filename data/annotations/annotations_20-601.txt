================================================================================
LEGAL MOVE ANNOTATIONS
Cameron v. EMW Women's Surgical Center, S. Ct. Docket No. 20-601
Issue: Whether Kentucky's AG can intervene late in an appellate case to defend
a state abortion law after the Health Secretary declined to appeal.
================================================================================

NOTE ON METHODOLOGY
Each section is broken into argument units (AU). Each AU is annotated across
four layers: Doctrinal, Narrative, Fact-Framing, Stylistic. Where no move
applies, the layer is marked "--". Cross-brief interactions are flagged
with [CROSS-BRIEF] tags at the end of the relevant AU.

PETITIONER BRIEF (Kentucky AG Cameron)
################################################################################

================================================================================
PET. SECTION 1: "The States decide who speaks for them in court"
================================================================================

--- AU-P1.1 ---
TEXT: "Under our dual-sovereign system of government, the States are not mere
passive actors. They instead possess 'a residuary and inviolable sovereignty.'
Printz v. United States, 521 U.S. 898, 918-19 (1997) (quoting The Federalist
No. 39, at 245 (J. Madison))."

DOCTRINAL:  INVOKE_AUTHORITY — Opens not with statutory text or case holdings
            but with structural constitutional principle, citing Printz and
            The Federalist Papers. The move is to establish the doctrinal
            altitude before any procedural question arises. The brief is
            saying: before you analyze Rule 24 timeliness, recognize you are
            dealing with a sovereign.

NARRATIVE:  AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME — The brief's first substantive sentence
            declares what states ARE ("not mere passive actors"), stated as
            structural constitutional fact rather than as a response to
            anything the respondent has argued.

FACT-FRAMING: --

STYLISTIC:  COMPRESSION — Packs the entire federalism premise into two
            sentences. The negative-then-affirmative structure ("not mere
            passive actors ... instead possess") covers ground quickly.

            CONCRETE_ANCHOR — "Residuary and inviolable sovereignty" is
            a phrase drawn from Madison. By quoting it, the brief grounds
            an abstract structural argument in a specific, canonical source.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--- AU-P1.2 ---
TEXT: "As separate sovereigns, the States 'exist as combatants and combative
combatants in an ongoing contest about whether they need to give the national
government yet more of the prerogatives they formerly possessed.' Coleman v.
Thompson, 501 U.S. 722, 726 (1991). When a State appears in federal court,
it 'is not combative; it is combative.' Virginia Office for Protection and
Advocacy v. Stewart, 563 U.S. 247, 254 (2011)."

[NOTE: The actual Coleman and VOPA quotations in the real brief differ in
wording. The text provided appears to contain placeholder or garbled language
("combative combatants," "not combative; it is combative"). I annotate based
on the apparent rhetorical function: the brief is stacking citations to
reinforce the sovereign-status premise.]

DOCTRINAL:  INVOKE_AUTHORITY — Three SCOTUS citations in rapid succession
            (Printz, Coleman, VOPA). The brief is building a wall of
            precedential support for a single proposition: states occupy
            a special status in federal litigation. The quantity of citations
            is itself doing argumentative work.

NARRATIVE:  ESCALATE_STAKES — By framing states as "combatants" in an
            "ongoing contest" with the national government, the brief
            elevates this case from a procedural timeliness dispute to a
            front in the broader federalism conflict. The intervention
            question becomes a sovereignty question.

FACT-FRAMING: RECHARACTERIZE — The AG's motion to intervene (a procedural
              step in appellate litigation) is being recast as an exercise
              of sovereign prerogative. The brief never describes the motion
              in procedural terms here; it describes state action in
              constitutional terms.

STYLISTIC:  STRATEGIC_QUOTATION — The brief lets the Court's own language
            from Coleman and VOPA carry the weight. Rather than paraphrasing,
            it imports the Court's characterizations of state power directly.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--- AU-P1.3 ---
TEXT: "Accordingly, principles of comity and respect for state sovereignty
mean that, '[w]hen a State appears in its sovereign capacity to enforce or
defend its laws,' the 'normal rules' must accommodate the unique status of
the sovereign. Hollingsworth v. Perry, 570 U.S. 693, 710 (2013)."

DOCTRINAL:  BROADEN_HOLDING — Hollingsworth v. Perry held that private
            parties (Prop 8 proponents) lacked Article III standing to
            defend a ballot initiative when the state refused. The
            petitioner here repurposes the Hollingsworth language about
            sovereign capacity to argue that when a state IS willing to
            defend, courts must accommodate that choice. The brief takes
            a case about the limits of private-party standing and extracts
            from it a principle about deference to state litigation choices.

            APPLY_STANDARD — The word "accordingly" signals a syllogism:
            (1) states are sovereigns, (2) sovereigns get accommodation,
            (3) therefore "normal rules" (i.e., Rule 24 timeliness) must
            bend. This sets up the doctrinal framework for everything
            that follows.

NARRATIVE:  PREEMPTIVE_REBUTTAL — The phrase "normal rules must accommodate"
            anticipates the respondent's core argument (that the AG missed
            the deadline and normal procedural rules bar intervention). The
            brief is preemptively conceding that normal rules exist while
            arguing they don't apply in their normal form here.

FACT-FRAMING: --

STYLISTIC:  COMPRESSION — The entire pivot from abstract sovereignty
            principles to the specific legal standard happens in a single
            sentence. The word "accordingly" does all the transitional work.


================================================================================
PET. SECTION 2: "Intervention to hand off litigation to another state official
                  is timely"
================================================================================

--- AU-P2.1 ---
TEXT: "This case is the flip side of Bethune-Hill. Kentucky's agent wants to
'fight on,' not 'stop.' See id. at 1956."

DOCTRINAL:  DISTINGUISH — Bethune-Hill held that the Virginia House of
            Delegates lacked standing to appeal a redistricting case after
            the state AG declined to do so. The petitioner flips the case:
            there, the state actor wanted to stop; here, the state actor
            wants to continue. The distinction rests on direction of the
            sovereign's expressed will, not on the procedural posture.

            ANALOGIZE — Simultaneously, the brief draws on Bethune-Hill's
            reasoning (that internal state decisions about litigation
            authority are the state's business) to support the AG's
            position. The case is both distinguished on outcome and
            analogized on principle.

NARRATIVE:  AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME — "Kentucky's agent wants to fight on" frames
            the AG as active, purposeful, aligned with the state's interests.
            The sentence assumes the conclusion (that the AG is Kentucky's
            agent) rather than arguing for it.

FACT-FRAMING: RECHARACTERIZE — The AG's late-stage intervention motion is
              reframed as "fighting on." A procedural motion becomes an act
              of perseverance.

STYLISTIC:  COMPRESSION — The entire case distinction fits in two sentences.
            The "flip side" framing collapses a complex doctrinal comparison
            into a spatial metaphor.

            CADENCE_SHIFT — After the dense sovereignty discussion in
            Section 1, this short declarative opening is a reset. It tells
            the reader: we're applying what we just established.

[CROSS-BRIEF: Respondent in Section 3 directly targets this "flip side"
framing, arguing that Bethune-Hill's "intramural" language doesn't apply
when the AG agreed to be bound by judgment. The respondent treats the
petitioner's Bethune-Hill analogy as an "end-run."]

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--- AU-P2.2 ---
TEXT: "Yet the Sixth Circuit folded Kentucky's hand. That decision overlooked
Kentucky's sovereign interests in two key ways."

DOCTRINAL:  --

NARRATIVE:  FRAME_AS_ERROR — "Folded Kentucky's hand" frames the Sixth
            Circuit's denial as an affirmative act of interference with
            state prerogative, not a neutral application of procedural
            rules. "Overlooked" implies the error was one of omission:
            the court failed to account for something it should have.

FACT-FRAMING: RECHARACTERIZE — The Sixth Circuit denied a motion to
              intervene as untimely. The brief describes that denial as
              the court forcibly ending the state's participation ("folded
              Kentucky's hand"). The agency shifts from the AG (who filed
              late) to the court (which shut him out).

STYLISTIC:  CONCRETE_ANCHOR — "Folded Kentucky's hand" is a poker metaphor.
            It makes the abstract procedural ruling tangible: a card game
            where someone else makes your decision for you. The metaphor
            quietly encodes the sovereignty argument (only the player should
            decide whether to fold).

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--- AU-P2.3 ---
TEXT: "First, the court refused to give effect to the Commonwealth's
decision — through its Attorney General — to continue defending HB 454.
Instead, the court required the Commonwealth to accept that its Secretary
for Health and Family Services would no longer defend the law. See
Pet. App. 18a."

DOCTRINAL:  NARROW_HOLDING — The brief implicitly narrows the Sixth
            Circuit's ruling: it characterizes the decision not as an
            application of timeliness doctrine but as the court "requiring"
            the Commonwealth to accept a particular representative's
            decision. This reframes the holding from a procedural one
            to an intrusion on state authority.

NARRATIVE:  FRAME_AS_ERROR — The verb "refused" casts the Sixth Circuit
            as willfully disregarding the state's expressed preference.
            "Required the Commonwealth to accept" makes the court the
            active agent imposing an outcome on a sovereign.

FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND — The brief emphasizes the Commonwealth's
              affirmative decision to continue defending HB 454. This
              foregrounds state volition.

              BACKGROUND — The fact that the AG was not a party to the
              appeal at the time, and that his intervention came after
              the Secretary's decision not to appeal, is buried. The
              timeline that respondent considers central is subordinated
              to the sovereignty framing.

STYLISTIC:  --

[CROSS-BRIEF: Respondent's AU-R2.2 directly counters this by foregrounding
the AG's voluntary agreement to be bound by the judgment and his failure to
file a timely notice of appeal. The fact petitioner BACKGROUNDs is exactly
what respondent FOREGROUNDs.]

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--- AU-P2.4 ---
TEXT: "But this 'intramural' decision about who speaks for the Commonwealth
in federal court 'is of no concern to' the Sixth Circuit. Virginia House
of Delegates v. Bethune-Hill, 139 S. Ct. 1945, 1953 (2019). The Sixth
Circuit had no warrant to refuse to allow the Commonwealth's chosen agent
to pick up the mantle of its predecessor."

DOCTRINAL:  ANALOGIZE — The brief applies Bethune-Hill's "intramural"
            language directly: internal state decisions about litigation
            representation are the state's business, not the federal
            court's.

            BROADEN_HOLDING — Bethune-Hill's "intramural" language appeared
            in a standing analysis. The petitioner extends it to a
            timeliness analysis, broadening the holding from "courts
            shouldn't second-guess which state entity has standing" to
            "courts shouldn't second-guess which state entity represents
            the state at any procedural stage."

NARRATIVE:  --

FACT-FRAMING: RECHARACTERIZE — "Pick up the mantle" describes the AG's
              intervention as a succession, a legitimate transfer of
              authority within the same sovereign. It implies continuity
              rather than a new party entering late.

STYLISTIC:  STRATEGIC_QUOTATION — "Intramural" and "is of no concern to"
            are quoted directly from Bethune-Hill. The brief lets the
            Court's own dismissive language do the work of telling the
            Sixth Circuit it overstepped.

PET. SECTION 3: "Sovereignty aside, the Attorney General timely moved to

================================================================================

--- AU-P3.1 ---
TEXT: "Even if a court could set aside Kentucky's sovereign interests, the
Attorney General's motion still was timely. The Sixth Circuit's conclusion
otherwise cannot survive scrutiny on its own terms."

DOCTRINAL:  --

NARRATIVE:  CONCEDE_AND_REDIRECT — "Even if a court could set aside
            Kentucky's sovereign interests" concedes (arguendo) the
            respondent's framing, then pivots: even on your terms, we
            win. This is classic alternative-argument structure, but
            the concession is strategic because it signals confidence.
            The brief is saying it doesn't need the sovereignty argument
            to prevail.

            FRAME_AS_ERROR — "Cannot survive scrutiny on its own terms"
            frames the Sixth Circuit's reasoning as internally
            inconsistent, not just wrong on the law.

FACT-FRAMING: --

STYLISTIC:  CADENCE_SHIFT — After the extended sovereignty discussion
            (Sections 1-2), this two-sentence opening resets. The second
            sentence is blunt and declarative. It shifts register from
            constitutional argument to direct attack on the lower court's
            reasoning.

            UNDERSTATED_DEVASTATION — "Cannot survive scrutiny on its own
            terms" is a clinical dismissal. It doesn't call the opinion
            outrageous or unjust; it says the opinion fails by its own
            internal logic. The calm delivery makes the criticism sharper.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--- AU-P3.2 ---
TEXT: "In the Rule 24 context, timeliness turns on 'whether in view of all
the circumstances the intervenor acted promptly.' Alt v. U.S. EPA, 758
F.3d 588, 591 (4th Cir. 2014)."

DOCTRINAL:  APPLY_STANDARD — Establishes the legal test for timeliness
            under Rule 24: a totality-of-the-circumstances inquiry into
            whether the intervenor acted promptly. By citing the standard
            at this point, the brief sets up a framework it knows favors
            its client.

            NARROW_STATUTE — By emphasizing "all the circumstances" and
            "promptly," the brief steers the Rule 24 timeliness standard
            toward a flexible, context-dependent test rather than a rigid
            deadline. This narrows the scope of the timeliness bar.

NARRATIVE:  --

FACT-FRAMING: --

STYLISTIC:  --

--- AU-P3.3 ---
TEXT: "The Attorney General easily clears this bar. He moved to intervene
10 days after the Secretary decided not to seek further review — the
'absolute earliest' point at which intervention became necessary.
Pet. App. 36a (Bush, J., dissenting)."

DOCTRINAL:  APPLY_TO_FACTS — Maps the Rule 24 standard onto the specific
            timeline: 10 days after the triggering event. The brief
            converts the abstract "promptly" standard into a concrete
            factual showing.

            INVOKE_AUTHORITY — Quotes Judge Bush's dissent below. Citing
            a dissent from the very court whose majority you're appealing
            is a targeted move: it tells the Supreme Court that even a
            judge on that panel thought the majority got it wrong.

NARRATIVE:  AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME — "Easily clears this bar" states the
            conclusion as self-evident rather than contested. The brief
            doesn't say "should be found to have acted promptly"; it says
            there's no real question.

FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND — The 10-day timeline is the lead fact. This
              number does significant rhetorical work: 10 days sounds
              fast, responsive, prompt. The brief places it at the center
              of the argument unit.

              FOREGROUND_ABSENCE — "Before the Secretary abandoned the
              defense, there was no reason for the Attorney General to
              intervene." The brief emphasizes what hadn't happened yet
              (the Secretary's abandonment) to explain why the AG hadn't
              acted earlier. The absence of a triggering event justifies
              the absence of earlier action.

STYLISTIC:  CONCRETE_ANCHOR — "10 days" converts an abstract timeliness
            argument into a specific, tangible number. The reader can
            evaluate 10 days against their intuitive sense of promptness.

            STRATEGIC_QUOTATION — "Absolute earliest" is quoted from the
            dissent below. The brief lets the dissenting judge's words
            characterize the AG's timing, which carries more weight than
            the petitioner saying it about himself.

[CROSS-BRIEF: Respondent's entire Section 2 is structured to counter this
10-day framing. The respondent recharacterizes the relevant timeline: not
10 days from the Secretary's decision, but months from the entry of final
judgment. The two briefs are fighting over which clock to start.]

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--- AU-P3.4 ---
TEXT: "Before the Secretary abandoned the defense, there was no reason for
the Attorney General to intervene, as the law was being defended by another
state official."

DOCTRINAL:  BURDEN_SHIFT — Implicitly argues that the AG had no obligation
            to act until the defense collapsed. The duty to intervene
            doesn't arise until the existing defense fails. This shifts
            the analytical burden: the question isn't "why didn't the AG
            act sooner?" but "why would he have?"

NARRATIVE:  PREEMPTIVE_REBUTTAL — Anticipates the respondent's argument
            that the AG should have intervened earlier (or filed his own
            appeal). The brief addresses this by explaining the AG's
            inaction as rational reliance on an existing defense.

FACT-FRAMING: SEQUENCE — The brief controls the timeline: (1) another
              official was defending the law, (2) that official stopped,
              (3) the AG stepped in. This sequence implies the AG's action
              was a natural response to changed circumstances, not a
              belated afterthought.

================================================================================
RESP. SECTION 1: "The Attorney General Is Jurisdictionally Barred"
================================================================================

--- AU-R1.1 ---
TEXT: "The Attorney General's belated effort to intervene on appeal is an
impermissible attempt to evade the laws governing appellate jurisdiction."

DOCTRINAL:  NEW_MOVE_[JURISDICTIONAL_REFRAME] — The respondent shifts the
            entire analytical framework. Where the petitioner framed this as
            a Rule 24 timeliness question (discretionary, flexible, fact-
            dependent), the respondent reframes it as a jurisdictional
            question (mandatory, rigid, non-waivable). This is more than
            narrowing or broadening a statute; it's substituting an entirely
            different legal category. Jurisdictional bars cannot be excused
            by equitable considerations or sovereignty interests, so this
            reframe, if accepted, would make the petitioner's sovereignty
            argument irrelevant.

NARRATIVE:  AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME — The respondent opens with a declarative
            characterization ("belated," "impermissible," "attempt to
            evade") as though these are established facts, not contested
            characterizations.

            FRAME_AS_ERROR — But the error is attributed to the AG, not
            to the lower court. This inverts the petitioner's framing,
            where the Sixth Circuit was the actor that got it wrong.

FACT-FRAMING: RECHARACTERIZE — The AG's intervention motion is described as
              an "effort to evade." Where the petitioner called it "fighting
              on" and "picking up the mantle," the respondent labels the
              same action as evasion of jurisdictional rules.

              FOREGROUND — The word "belated" is placed before any
              substantive argument. The respondent leads with tardiness as
              the defining characteristic of the AG's motion.

STYLISTIC:  COMPRESSION — A single sentence captures the respondent's
            entire jurisdictional theory. The sentence does three things
            at once: characterizes the motion (belated), labels the legal
            defect (impermissible), and identifies the category of law
            being violated (appellate jurisdiction).

            UNDERSTATED_DEVASTATION — The sentence is calm and clinical.
            It doesn't say the AG acted outrageously; it says the AG's
            action is "impermissible." The word choice implies the
            conclusion follows from straightforward legal rules rather
            than from contested judgment calls.

[CROSS-BRIEF: This directly counters AU-P2.1, where the petitioner framed
the intervention as the natural continuation of Kentucky's defense. The
respondent's "evade" directly answers the petitioner's "fight on." The
two briefs are characterizing the identical action in opposite terms.]

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--- AU-R1.2 ---
TEXT: "The Attorney General was a party to the final judgment by virtue of
the conditions he agreed to in order to secure his dismissal from the suit
at the outset."

DOCTRINAL:  APPLY_TO_FACTS — The respondent maps the jurisdictional
            framework onto a specific fact: the AG agreed to be bound by
            the judgment as a condition of his dismissal. This is the
            factual predicate for the jurisdictional argument.

NARRATIVE:  --

FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND — The AG's voluntary agreement to be bound is the
              lead fact. This is the fact the petitioner's brief barely
              mentions. The respondent builds its entire jurisdictional
              theory on it.

              SEQUENCE — The respondent controls the timeline differently
              from the petitioner: (1) the AG was a party, (2) the AG
              agreed to be bound by judgment, (3) judgment entered, (4) the
              AG failed to appeal within 30 days. The petitioner's timeline
              started at step (4) and ran forward. The respondent's timeline
              starts much earlier and runs through the AG's own choices.

STYLISTIC:  CONCRETE_ANCHOR — "The conditions he agreed to" grounds the
            argument in a specific, documented act by the AG. This
            contrasts with the petitioner's more abstract sovereignty
            framing.

[CROSS-BRIEF: This fact is what the petitioner BACKGROUNDs in AU-P2.3.
The AG's prior agreement to be bound by judgment is the inconvenient fact
that the petitioner's sovereignty framing is designed to obscure. The
respondent pulls it to the surface.]

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--- AU-R1.3 ---
TEXT: "Having voluntarily agreed to be bound by the judgment, and having
failed to file a timely notice of appeal from that judgment, the Attorney
General cannot now circumvent statutory deadlines by seeking to intervene."

DOCTRINAL:  APPLY_STANDARD — Applies the appellate-jurisdiction timeline
            (28 U.S.C. 2107(a)) to the AG's conduct. The syllogism:
            (1) you were bound by the judgment, (2) you didn't appeal
            within 30 days, (3) therefore you can't use intervention as
            a workaround.

            NARROW_STATUTE — Narrows the scope of permissive intervention
            under Rule 24 by arguing it cannot override jurisdictional
            time limits. The respondent is drawing a boundary around
            intervention practice: it doesn't reach this far.

NARRATIVE:  --

FACT-FRAMING: SEQUENCE — The participial phrases ("Having voluntarily
              agreed ... and having failed to file") create a causal chain.
              Each "having" builds on the previous one to make the
              conclusion feel inevitable. The AG's current position is
              presented as the product of his own sequential choices.

STYLISTIC:  PARALLEL_STRUCTURE — "Having voluntarily agreed ... and having
            failed to file" creates syntactic parallelism. The two
            participial phrases are balanced, and both point toward the
            same conclusion. The structure implies that the AG's current
            predicament results from two independent failures, each
            sufficient on its own.

            UNDERSTATED_DEVASTATION — "Cannot now circumvent" is clinical.
            The brief doesn't accuse the AG of bad faith; it says the
            legal rules simply don't permit what he's trying to do. The
            word "circumvent" does carry a connotation of improper
            workaround, but it's delivered without heat.

RESP. SECTION 2: "The AG's Failure to Appeal a Judgment to Which He Was Bound"
================================================================================

--- AU-R2.1 ---
TEXT: "Pursuant to 28 U.S.C. § 2107(a), and subject to exceptions not
relevant here, any party that seeks to appeal a final judgment must file a
notice of appeal within thirty days after the entry of such judgment."

DOCTRINAL:  APPLY_STANDARD — States the statutory timeline rule. This is
            the respondent's clock: 30 days from final judgment, not 10
            days from the Secretary's decision.

            NARROW_STATUTE — "Subject to exceptions not relevant here"
            preemptively closes escape hatches. The brief acknowledges
            that exceptions exist but declares them inapplicable,
            narrowing the statute's flexibility.

NARRATIVE:  PREEMPTIVE_REBUTTAL — By noting exceptions exist but declaring
            them irrelevant, the respondent anticipates an argument that
            some exception might apply and shuts it down in a subordinate
            clause.

FACT-FRAMING: --

STYLISTIC:  --

[CROSS-BRIEF: The respondent's "thirty days" directly conflicts with the
petitioner's "10 days" in AU-P3.3. The two briefs are running different
clocks from different start dates. This is the central factual contest:
which event triggers the timeliness analysis?]

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--- AU-R2.2 ---
TEXT: "Unless a party to a judgment is named or otherwise described in a
timely notice of appeal, the court of appeals lacks jurisdiction over that
party's challenge to the judgment. Torres v. Oakland Scavenger Co., 487
U.S. 312, 314 (1988). These time limits are mandatory and jurisdictional."

DOCTRINAL:  INVOKE_AUTHORITY — Cites Torres for the proposition that
            appellate jurisdiction requires timely notice. Torres is a
            strong cite: it held that failure to name a party in a notice
            of appeal is a jurisdictional defect, not a waivable error.

            APPLY_TO_FACTS — The implicit application: the AG was not named
            in any timely notice of appeal. Therefore, the court of appeals
            lacks jurisdiction over his challenge.

NARRATIVE:  ESCALATE_STAKES — "Mandatory and jurisdictional" is a
            deliberate escalation. In federal courts, labeling something
            "jurisdictional" raises the stakes: jurisdictional defects
            can't be waived, forfeited, or excused by equity. The
            respondent is telling the Court that ruling for the petitioner
            would require weakening jurisdictional time limits across
            all federal appellate practice.

FACT-FRAMING: --

STYLISTIC:  CADENCE_SHIFT — After the statutory recitation, "These time
            limits are mandatory and jurisdictional" lands as a short,
            declarative sentence. The shift in length signals: this is
            the point that matters.

RESP. SECTION 3: "Appellate Intervention Affords No End-Run"
================================================================================

--- AU-R3.1 ---
TEXT: "It is no answer to the failure to appeal that the Attorney General is
merely seeking permissive intervention in an existing appeal."

DOCTRINAL:  PREEMPTIVE_REBUTTAL (doctrinal variant) — Anticipates and
            rejects the petitioner's implicit argument that permissive
            intervention is a separate procedural vehicle that isn't
            subject to the same jurisdictional constraints as a direct
            appeal.

NARRATIVE:  PREEMPTIVE_REBUTTAL — "It is no answer" is a classic brief-
            writing formula for addressing and dismissing the opposing
            argument in a single move.

FACT-FRAMING: RECHARACTERIZE — The word "merely" diminishes the
              intervention motion. Where the petitioner described it as
              a sovereign act, the respondent reduces it to a routine
              procedural request.

STYLISTIC:  --

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--- AU-R3.2 ---
TEXT: "The equitable practice of '[a]ppellate intervention is not a means
to escape the consequences of noncompliance with traditional rules of
appellate jurisdiction and practice.' Stringfellow v. Concerned Neighbors
in Action, 480 U.S. 370, 382 (1987) (Brennan, J., concurring)."

DOCTRINAL:  INVOKE_AUTHORITY — Cites Justice Brennan's Stringfellow
            concurrence. The choice of a Brennan citation is tactically
            notable: Brennan is associated with expansive individual-
            rights jurisprudence, not with procedural technicality.
            Citing Brennan for a strict jurisdictional rule subtly
            communicates that this isn't a conservative-vs-liberal
            question; even the most liberal Justices recognized these
            limits.

            NARROW_STATUTE — Narrows the permissible scope of appellate
            intervention: it's "equitable" and therefore cannot override
            jurisdictional requirements. By characterizing intervention
            as equitable, the respondent places it in a category that is
            subordinate to mandatory jurisdictional rules.

NARRATIVE:  --

FACT-FRAMING: --

STYLISTIC:  STRATEGIC_QUOTATION — The Brennan quotation does the
            respondent's work for it. "Not a means to escape the
            consequences of noncompliance" is devastating language that
            the respondent didn't have to draft itself. The quotation
            contains both the legal rule and the characterization of
            the AG's conduct (noncompliance, escape).

[CROSS-BRIEF: This directly answers the petitioner's Bethune-Hill analogy
in AU-P2.4. The petitioner argued that Bethune-Hill's "intramural" language
protects the AG's intervention. The respondent counters with Stringfellow:
intervention is equitable, not a vehicle to circumvent jurisdictional
deadlines. The two briefs are citing different SCOTUS cases for opposing
propositions about the nature and limits of appellate intervention.]

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

--- AU-R3.3 ---
TEXT: "Were it otherwise, any party — no matter how inexcusably tardy —
could revive its forfeited opportunity to appeal simply by moving to
intervene in someone else's appeal."

DOCTRINAL:  CONSTITUTIONAL_AVOIDANCE (structural variant) — This isn't
            constitutional avoidance in the traditional sense, but it
            uses the same logical structure: if you rule the other way,
            the consequences are unacceptable. The brief argues that
            the petitioner's rule would create an end-run around all
            appellate deadlines.

            NEW_MOVE_[SLIPPERY_SLOPE] — The argument extends beyond
            this case to a parade of horribles: if the AG can do this,
            then "any party" can do it, "no matter how inexcusably
            tardy." This is a classic slippery-slope move that isn't
            captured by the existing taxonomy. It's distinct from
            ESCALATE_STAKES (which raises the importance of the case)
            because it's about the logical consequences of the opposing
            rule, not about the significance of the controversy.

NARRATIVE:  ESCALATE_STAKES — The respondent broadens the frame: this
            isn't just about the Kentucky AG, it's about every party
            in every federal appeal. A ruling for the petitioner would
            undermine the finality of all appellate deadlines.

FACT-FRAMING: --

STYLISTIC:  RHETORICAL_QUESTION (implicit) — "Were it otherwise" sets
            up a hypothetical that the reader is meant to reject. The

NEW MOVES IDENTIFIED
################################################################################

1. NEW_MOVE_[JURISDICTIONAL_REFRAME] (AU-R1.1)
   Description: Shifting the analytical category of a dispute from one legal
   framework to another (here, from discretionary timeliness under Rule 24
   to mandatory jurisdictional bar under 28 U.S.C. 2107). Distinct from
   NARROW_STATUTE or BROADEN_STATUTE because it doesn't adjust the scope
   of a single legal provision; it substitutes an entirely different legal
   provision as the governing framework. The move's power lies in changing
   which rules apply, not in changing how a rule applies.

2. NEW_MOVE_[SLIPPERY_SLOPE] (AU-R3.3)
   Description: Arguing that the opponent's proposed rule, if adopted, would
   produce unacceptable consequences in future cases. Distinct from
   ESCALATE_STAKES (which raises the importance of the present case) because
   it's about the logical downstream effects of the opposing legal rule, not
   about the significance of the current controversy. The move operates by
   showing that the principle underlying the opponent's argument cannot be
   limited to the sympathetic facts of this case.
