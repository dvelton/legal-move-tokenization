(Arguing: The QTA's statute of limitations is NOT jurisdictional)

--- Section 1: I. The Quiet Title Act Does Not Clearly State That Its Statute of Limitations Is Jurisdictional ---

ARGUMENT UNIT 1.1: Establishing the clear-statement framework
Text: "we treat a procedural requirement as jurisdictional only if Congress 'clearly states' that it is." ... "the 'traditional tools of statutory construction must plainly show that Congress imbued a procedural bar with jurisdictional consequences.'"

  DOCTRINAL: INVOKE_AUTHORITY — Stacks Boechler, Arbaugh, Wong, Musacchio, Reed Elsevier to establish the clear-statement rule as settled, binding doctrine. APPLY_STANDARD — Frames the entire inquiry as governed by the "traditional tools of statutory construction" standard from Wong. BURDEN_SHIFT — Places the burden squarely on the party claiming jurisdictional status: "It is a steep burden to demonstrate that a rule is jurisdictional."
  NARRATIVE: AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME — Sets the default position: procedural rules are nonjurisdictional unless Congress speaks clearly. Petitioner frames itself as defending the status quo rather than seeking a change. ESCALATE_STAKES — Signals that getting the jurisdictional label wrong has systemic consequences for adversarial litigation.
  FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND — Elevates the "steep burden" and the fact that the Court has "not yet identified a single filing deadline" meeting the clear-statement test. FOREGROUND_ABSENCE — The Court has never found a filing deadline jurisdictional under this standard; absence becomes affirmative evidence.
  STYLISTIC: AUTHORITY_SIGNALING — Citation-dense opening paragraph signals deep doctrinal grounding. Cites six SCOTUS cases and a Gorsuch concurrence in the first paragraph. STRATEGIC_QUOTATION — Deploys "quintessential claim-processing rules" from Wong and the observation from Myers v. Commissioner that no filing deadline has ever met the test. COMPRESSION — Packs the entire doctrinal framework into a single opening paragraph to create a sense of overwhelming precedential weight.

ARGUMENT UNIT 1.2: Filing deadlines as claim-processing rules
Text: "Time and again, the Court has described filing deadlines as 'quintessential claim-processing rules,' which 'seek to promote the orderly progress of litigation,' but do not deprive a court of authority to hear a case."

  DOCTRINAL: BROADEN_HOLDING — Generalizes Wong's holding about the FTCA to establish that filing deadlines as a category are nonjurisdictional. ANALOGIZE — QTA's filing deadline is like every other filing deadline the Court has examined.
  NARRATIVE: PREEMPTIVE_REBUTTAL — Anticipates respondent's argument that the QTA is special by framing all filing deadlines as presumptively nonjurisdictional.
  FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND — Emphasizes the "time and again" repetition of this principle.
  STYLISTIC: STRATEGIC_QUOTATION — "Quintessential claim-processing rules" does heavy rhetorical lifting, making the nonjurisdictional characterization sound obvious.

--- Section 2: A. The Quiet Title Act Uses Only Mundane Statute-of-Limitations Language ---

ARGUMENT UNIT 2.1: The statutory text is ordinary limitations language
Text: The QTA provides that an action "shall be barred unless it is commenced within twelve years." Petitioner argues this is "mundane statute-of-limitations language, saying only what every time bar, by definition, must."

  DOCTRINAL: NARROW_STATUTE — Reads the QTA's text narrowly as saying nothing more than what any statute of limitations says. ANALOGIZE — Draws direct textual parallels to statutes in Wong (FTCA), Henderson (veterans claims), Musacchio (criminal SOL), and Fort Bend County (Title VII). APPLY_STANDARD — Applies the clear-statement test to the text and finds it absent.
  NARRATIVE: AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME — Characterizes the statutory language as "mundane," draining it of any special significance. PREEMPTIVE_REBUTTAL — Addresses "shall" by citing Henderson: the Court has "rejected the notion that all mandatory prescriptions, however emphatic," are jurisdictional.
  FACT-FRAMING: RECHARACTERIZE — Transforms the mandatory "shall be barred" from a powerful sovereign-immunity condition into unremarkable boilerplate. DIMINISHING_MODIFIER — "Only mundane" and "merely mandatory" minimize the statutory text. FOREGROUND_ABSENCE — The statute "does not expressly refer to subject-matter jurisdiction or speak in jurisdictional terms."
  STYLISTIC: COMPRESSION — "Saying only what every time bar, by definition, must" reduces the entire statutory provision to a tautology. STRATEGIC_QUOTATION — Borrows Wong's "mundane" characterization and applies it directly.

ARGUMENT UNIT 2.2: Comparison to analogous statutes held nonjurisdictional
Text: "The Quiet Title Act uses terms similar to other statutes of limitations that the Court has held to be nonjurisdictional." (Henderson, Musacchio, Fort Bend County comparisons.)

  DOCTRINAL: ANALOGIZE — Systematic comparison of QTA language to Henderson's 120-day limit, Musacchio's criminal SOL, and Fort Bend County's Title VII charge-filing requirement. Each was held nonjurisdictional. APPLY_TO_FACTS — Walks through the textual parallels to show the QTA is indistinguishable from these statutes.
  NARRATIVE: CONCEDE_AND_REDIRECT — Implicitly concedes the QTA says "shall" but redirects to show that Henderson's "shall" was held nonjurisdictional.
  FACT-FRAMING: SEQUENCE — Lines up case after case in a parade of analogies, creating cumulative weight through repetition.
  STYLISTIC: PARALLEL_STRUCTURE — Repeats the pattern: cite statute, quote language, note it was held nonjurisdictional. The repetitive structure emphasizes that the QTA is one more example of the same pattern. AUTHORITY_SIGNALING — Each analogy is anchored to a specific SCOTUS holding.

--- Section 3: B. Congress Indicated That the QTA's Statute of Limitations Is Nonjurisdictional By Codifying It in a Separate Section ---

ARGUMENT UNIT 3.1: Structural separation of jurisdictional grant from SOL
Text: "This Court has often explained that Congress's separation of a filing deadline from a jurisdictional grant indicates that the time bar is not jurisdictional." SOL in Section 2409a(g); jurisdictional grant in Section 1346(f).

  DOCTRINAL: APPLY_STANDARD — Applies the structural-separation principle from Wong, Henderson, Reed Elsevier, Arbaugh, and Zipes. ANALOGIZE — The QTA's jurisdictional grant sits alongside the FTCA's jurisdictional grant in Section 1346, and the FTCA's SOL was held nonjurisdictional in Wong. DISTINGUISH_PURPOSE — The jurisdictional-grant section serves a different function than the limitations provision.
  NARRATIVE: AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME — Congress's organizational choices are evidence of intent. The structural argument turns the Code's architecture into an affirmative indicator.
  FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND — Emphasizes the physical separation: SOL in 2409a(g), jurisdiction in 1346(f). SEQUENCE — Walks through the parallel structure of the two sections to show Congress grouped QTA's jurisdictional grant with other nonjurisdictional-SOL statutes.
  STYLISTIC: CONCRETE_ANCHOR — Specific section numbers (2409a(g) vs. 1346(f)) ground the argument in verifiable statutory architecture. AUTHORITY_SIGNALING — Five SCOTUS citations support the structural-separation principle.

ARGUMENT UNIT 3.2: The jurisdictional grant does not condition jurisdiction on the SOL
Text: Nothing in Section 1346(f) "'conditions its jurisdictional grant on' compliance with" the statute of limitations.

  DOCTRINAL: FOREGROUND_ABSENCE — The absence of a cross-reference between the jurisdictional grant and the SOL is treated as affirmative evidence. APPLY_TO_FACTS — Applies Musacchio/Reed Elsevier's "conditioning" test to the QTA's specific statutory structure.
  NARRATIVE: PREEMPTIVE_REBUTTAL — Blocks any argument that the jurisdictional grant implicitly incorporates the SOL.
  FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND_ABSENCE — Absence of textual linkage between Sections 1346(f) and 2409a(g) is the central factual predicate.
  STYLISTIC: COMPRESSION — A single, clean observation that would require respondent to find an affirmative textual link that doesn't exist.

--- Section 4: C. The Legislative and Statutory History Shows That Both Congress and the Department of the Interior Understood the SOL to Be Nonjurisdictional ---

ARGUMENT UNIT 4.1: Legislative history affirmatively indicates nonjurisdictional intent
Text: "the legislative history doesn't just lack a clear statement that the Quiet Title Act's statute of limitations is jurisdictional; it states directly that the statute of limitations was intended to be a waivable affirmative defense."

  DOCTRINAL: INVOKE_AUTHORITY — Cites Wong's acknowledgment that legislative history provides "further insight," then deploys it affirmatively. APPLY_TO_FACTS — Applies the legislative-history inquiry to the specific Senate Report and statutory background. DISTINGUISH — Distinguishes the QTA's legislative history from cases where history was silent; here it speaks directly.
  NARRATIVE: ESCALATE_STAKES — Frames the legislative history as going beyond mere silence to affirmative evidence of nonjurisdictional intent. AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME — The QTA was designed to give citizens "their day in court" against the government; a jurisdictional reading would frustrate this remedial purpose.
  FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND — Elevates the Senate Report language about "grave inequity" and citizens' need for a judicial forum. RECHARACTERIZE — Reframes sovereign immunity not as a bar to suit but as an injustice that the QTA was designed to correct. SEQUENCE — Traces the chronological arc from pre-QTA injustice (no remedy) to the 1972 enactment (remedy provided) to demonstrate Congress's remedial purpose.
  STYLISTIC: STRATEGIC_QUOTATION — "Grave inequity often has resulted to private citizens" from the Senate Report conveys congressional sympathy for the petitioners' position. CADENCE_SHIFT — Moves from technical statutory analysis in prior sections to a purposive, narrative-driven argument about legislative history.

ARGUMENT UNIT 4.2: Sovereign immunity waivers and Congress's remedial purpose
Text: "Because the United States had never waived sovereign immunity for quiet title actions, title that was disputed between private parties and the federal government could be quieted only when the government initiated suit."

  DOCTRINAL: DISTINGUISH_PURPOSE — The QTA's purpose was to open the courts to citizens, not to restrict access. A jurisdictional reading of the SOL would undermine that purpose.
  NARRATIVE: ESCALATE_STAKES — A jurisdictional reading slams the courthouse door on citizens with meritorious claims, re-creating the "grave inequity" Congress sought to eliminate.
  FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND — The pre-QTA asymmetry (only the government could initiate suit) highlights the injustice of a jurisdictional bar. RECHARACTERIZE — Transforms the SOL from a neutral time limit into a potential tool of government advantage.
  STYLISTIC: CONCRETE_ANCHOR — Specific historical context (pre-1972 sovereign immunity regime) grounds the abstract argument.

--- Section 5: II. The Court Has Never Held That the QTA's Statute of Limitations Is Jurisdictional ---

ARGUMENT UNIT 5.1: Prior references to "jurisdiction" were imprecise drive-by rulings
Text: "sporadic and casual use of the word 'jurisdiction' in this Court's previous Quiet Title Act cases" ... "these Quiet Title Act cases 'predate this Court's effort to bring some discipline to the use of the term jurisdictional.'"

  DOCTRINAL: DISTINGUISH — Distinguishes Block, Beggerly, and Mottaz as pre-discipline cases where "jurisdictional" was used loosely. NARROW_HOLDING — Reads prior QTA cases narrowly: the jurisdictional language was not the holding but obiter dicta. INVOKE_AUTHORITY — Deploys Arbaugh's "drive-by jurisdictional rulings" framework and Eberhart's acknowledgment of historical imprecision. STARE_DECISIS_SHIELD — Uses the Court's own self-correction doctrine (Boechler, Henderson, Fort Bend County) to argue that stare decisis does not protect imprecise past usage.
  NARRATIVE: FRAME_AS_ERROR — The lower courts' reliance on prior QTA language is characterized as mistaking dicta for holdings. INSTITUTIONAL_REBUKE — Implicitly criticizes lower courts for mechanically applying outdated terminology without engaging the clear-statement framework.
  FACT-FRAMING: RECHARACTERIZE — Transforms what respondent treats as binding precedent into "resulting imprecision" and "passing references." DIMINISHING_MODIFIER — "Sporadic and casual," "drive-by," "less than meticulous" minimize the precedential significance of prior usage.
  STYLISTIC: STRATEGIC_QUOTATION — "Drive-by jurisdictional rulings that should be accorded no precedential effect" from Arbaugh is deployed as a decisive label. UNDERSTATED_DEVASTATION — Characterizing SCOTUS's own prior language as "less than meticulous" is a restrained but effective critique. REGISTER_CONTRAST — Shifts between the Court's formal doctrinal language and the colloquial force of "drive-by."

ARGUMENT UNIT 5.2: The jurisdictional/nonjurisdictional distinction has practical consequences
Text: "'Branding a rule as going to a court's subject-matter jurisdiction alters the normal operation of our adversarial system.'"

  DOCTRINAL: APPLY_STANDARD — The jurisdictional label carries consequences (can't be waived, must be raised sua sponte, no equitable tolling), which is why the clear-statement rule exists.
  NARRATIVE: ESCALATE_STAKES — The label isn't academic; it determines whether courts can consider untimely-but-meritorious claims.
  FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND — Emphasizes the practical downstream effects of jurisdictional classification.
  STYLISTIC: STRATEGIC_QUOTATION — Henderson's language about "altering the normal operation of our adversarial system" carries institutional weight.

CROSS-BRIEF INTERACTION SUMMARY
================================================================================

1. THE CLEAR-STATEMENT FRAMEWORK (Petitioner Units 1.1, 2.1 vs. Respondent Unit R2.1)
   Both sides invoke the same doctrinal framework (Boechler, Wong, Musacchio) but frame its demands differently. Petitioner emphasizes strictness ("steep burden," "not yet identified a single filing deadline"). Respondent emphasizes flexibility ("no magic words," "precedent and practice" count). This is the central interpretive contest: how demanding is the clear-statement rule?

2. "MUNDANE LANGUAGE" vs. "CONDITIONS ON SOVEREIGN IMMUNITY" (Petitioner Unit 2.1 vs. Respondent Unit R3.2)
   Petitioner characterizes the QTA's SOL as ordinary limitations language indistinguishable from nonjurisdictional statutes. Respondent recharacterizes the same language as a condition on the sovereign's consent to be sued, which carries inherently jurisdictional weight under the sovereign-immunity framework. This is a RECHARACTERIZE vs. RECHARACTERIZE contest — same text, two entirely different doctrinal frames.

3. "DRIVE-BY RULINGS" vs. "DELIBERATE HOLDINGS" (Petitioner Unit 5.1 vs. Respondent Units R1.1, R1.2)
   Petitioner invokes Arbaugh's "drive-by jurisdictional rulings" to dismiss North Dakota and Mottaz. Respondent argues these were consequential holdings that determined the course of the proceedings and were subsequently treated by the Court itself (in Dalm) as establishing a "settled principle." This is a NARROW_HOLDING vs. BROADEN_HOLDING contest over the precedential status of prior cases.

4. LEGISLATIVE HISTORY: PURPOSE vs. ACQUIESCENCE (Petitioner Unit 4.1 vs. Respondent Unit R4.1)
   Petitioner reads the legislative history purposively: Congress intended to give citizens their day in court. Respondent reads it structurally: Congress amended the QTA in 1986 in response to North Dakota but left the jurisdictional characterization untouched, implicitly ratifying it. Both deploy FOREGROUND and SEQUENCE moves to organize the same historical record into opposing narratives.

5. IRWIN AND TEMPORAL SCOPE (Petitioner's framework reliance vs. Respondent Unit R1.3)
   Petitioner's entire argument assumes the clear-statement framework applies retroactively to pre-existing jurisdictional characterizations. Respondent blocks this by arguing Irwin (and by implication the clear-statement framework) announced a prospective rule that cannot reach back to disturb pre-Irwin holdings. This is a jurisdictional-reframing contest over which temporal framework governs.

6. BEGGERLY FLIP (Petitioner Unit 5.1 vs. Respondent Unit R1.4)
   Both sides cite Beggerly but derive opposite conclusions. Petitioner treats it as part of the modern, discipline-era case law. Respondent treats its holding that the QTA SOL cannot be equitably tolled as consistent with (and evidence of) jurisdictional status. A CONCEDE_AND_REDIRECT move by respondent that converts a petitioner citation into respondent support.

7. STARE DECISIS AS INDEPENDENT BARRIER (Respondent Units R1.5, R2.2, R4.1)
   Respondent deploys stare decisis not merely as a tiebreaker but as an independent, dispositive barrier: even if petitioner wins the de novo clear-statement analysis, the prior holdings plus congressional acquiescence plus workability foreclose relief. This creates a two-layer defense that petitioner must penetrate twice (first on the merits of the clear-statement analysis, then on stare decisis).
