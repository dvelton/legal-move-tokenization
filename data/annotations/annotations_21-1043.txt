--- Section 1: I. The Lanham Act Is Not Extraterritorial ---

ARGUMENT UNIT 1.1: The presumption against extraterritoriality is foundational law.

  DOCTRINAL: APPLY_STANDARD — Lays the RJR Nabisco two-step framework as the controlling analytical structure. INVOKE_AUTHORITY — Cites RJR Nabisco (2016) repeatedly to establish the standard as settled and recent.

  NARRATIVE: AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME — Opens the entire brief by establishing the presumption as a bedrock principle rather than a technical hurdle, casting the respondent as the party seeking deviation from normal law.

  FACT-FRAMING: BACKGROUND — The actual dispute facts (foreign sales, parties) are entirely absent from this opening. The section operates at pure doctrinal altitude. STRATEGIC_SILENCE — No mention of Steele v. Bulova or the 70-year history of lower courts applying the Act abroad.

  STYLISTIC: STRATEGIC_QUOTATION — The opening sentence is a quotation from RJR Nabisco ("United States law governs domestically but does not rule the world"), chosen for maximum rhetorical punch. AUTHORITY_SIGNALING — Heavy layering of Supreme Court language through block quotations to establish that this is not a novel argument but a simple application of existing rules. COMPRESSION — The two-step framework is reduced to a tight paragraph, then the conclusion snaps shut: "The Lanham Act does not extend to petitioners' foreign sales at either step."

ARGUMENT UNIT 1.2: The presumption serves a policy function of avoiding international discord.

  DOCTRINAL: DISTINGUISH_PURPOSE — Identifies the purpose behind the presumption (avoiding international friction) and deploys it as a substantive argument, not merely a canon of construction.

  NARRATIVE: ESCALATE_STAKES — Moves beyond statutory interpretation to invoke international consequences: "international discord that can result when U.S. law is applied to conduct in foreign countries."

  FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND — Elevates the "international discord" rationale from one of several RJR considerations to a central justification.

  STYLISTIC: STRATEGIC_QUOTATION — Uses the Court's own "commonsense notion" language to characterize the presumption as intuitive rather than technical.


--- Section 2: A. The Act Lacks the Requisite Unmistakable Indication of Extraterritorial Effect ---

ARGUMENT UNIT 2.1: The statutory text is silent on extraterritorial application.

  DOCTRINAL: NARROW_STATUTE — Reads the Lanham Act's operative sections (32(1)(a), 43(a)(1)(A)) as facially domestic by pointing to the absence of extraterritorial language. APPLY_STANDARD — Applies the RJR step-one test: does the statute give a "clear, affirmative indication" of extraterritorial reach?

  NARRATIVE: AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME — Frames the argument as the textually obvious reading; it is the opponent who must overcome textual silence. PREEMPTIVE_REBUTTAL — Anticipates the counterargument that express language is not required, and pre-concedes the point ("While an 'express statement of extraterritoriality is not essential'") before arguing that the exception is vanishingly narrow.

  FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND_ABSENCE — The central move here. Petitioner foregrounds what the statute does NOT say, treating statutory silence as affirmative evidence of domestic-only application. RECHARACTERIZE — Reframes statutory breadth (the "commerce" definition) as irrelevant to the extraterritoriality question.

  STYLISTIC: CONCRETE_ANCHOR — Cites specific statutory provisions by section number (15 U.S.C. 1114(1)(a), 1125(a), 1116(a), 1117(a)) to create an impression of close textual engagement. UNDERSTATED_DEVASTATION — "That should be dispositive." A four-word sentence carrying the weight of the entire textual argument.

ARGUMENT UNIT 2.2: Congress knows how to legislate extraterritorially when it wants to.

  DOCTRINAL: DISTINGUISH — Draws a sharp contrast between the Lanham Act and other statutes where Congress explicitly provided for extraterritorial reach (18 U.S.C. 351(i), 1751(k), 1957(d)(2)). NARROW_STATUTE — Uses the negative-inference canon: Congress's express extraterritorial language elsewhere implies its absence here is intentional.

  NARRATIVE: FRAME_AS_ERROR — Implies that reading the Lanham Act as extraterritorial would require ignoring a well-established pattern of how Congress signals extraterritorial intent.

  FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND — Foregrounds the specific textual examples of extraterritorial statutes to make the Lanham Act's silence look conspicuous by comparison.

  STYLISTIC: PARALLEL_STRUCTURE — Stacks examples of explicit extraterritorial provisions in rapid succession to emphasize the contrast. STRATEGIC_QUOTATION — Quotes the actual text of other statutes ("There is extraterritorial jurisdiction over the conduct prohibited by this section") to make the Lanham Act's silence visceral.

ARGUMENT UNIT 2.3: RJR's exception for statutes that "clearly evidence" extraterritorial effect without express language does not apply here.

  DOCTRINAL: DISTINGUISH — Distinguishes the Lanham Act from RICO (the statute at issue in RJR), which overcame the presumption by incorporating statutes that themselves applied extraterritorially. The Lanham Act does no such thing.

  NARRATIVE: CONCEDE_REDIRECT — Concedes that express language is not the only path to extraterritoriality under RJR, then redirects to show that the alternative path (incorporation by reference) is equally unavailable.

  FACT-FRAMING: DIMINISHING_MODIFIER — Characterizes the non-express path as "exceedingly rare" to minimize its availability.

  STYLISTIC: AUTHORITY_SIGNALING — Relies entirely on the Court's own words from RJR to frame the exception as narrow.


--- Section 3: B. Extraterritoriality Defies Traditional Trademark Principles ---

ARGUMENT UNIT 3.1: Trademark law has a separate, independent territoriality doctrine.

  DOCTRINAL: ANALOGIZE — Analogizes the general presumption against extraterritoriality to trademark law's own, independent territoriality doctrine, reinforcing the domestic-only reading from two directions. INVOKE_AUTHORITY — Invokes McCarthy on Trademarks (the leading treatise) as establishing that territoriality "is basic to American trademark law."

  NARRATIVE: AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME — Frames territoriality not as a contested principle but as universally accepted bedrock: "should be no surprise." ESCALATE_STAKES — The argument raises the stakes from statutory construction to disrupting an entire legal tradition.

  FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND — Foregrounding the treatise-level consensus on territoriality to make the respondent's position look like an outlier.

  STYLISTIC: STRATEGIC_QUOTATION — Uses McCarthy's language ("basic to American trademark law") to establish doctrinal consensus. AUTHORITY_SIGNALING — The layering of the McCarthy treatise, Person's Co. (Fed. Cir.), and historical Supreme Court cases creates an impression of overwhelming authority.

ARGUMENT UNIT 3.2: Historical Supreme Court precedent confirms trademark territoriality.

  DOCTRINAL: INVOKE_AUTHORITY — Cites Baglin v. Cusenier Co. (1911) and A. Bourjois & Co. v. Katzel (1923) as establishing territorial limits long before the Lanham Act. ANALOGIZE — Uses the fact that the Court refused to apply foreign trademark law to U.S. conduct (Baglin) as the mirror image of the current question: applying U.S. trademark law to foreign conduct.

  NARRATIVE: PREEMPTIVE_REBUTTAL — The Katzel discussion anticipates a possible respondent argument that trademark rights flow from the mark owner's rights globally. Petitioner shows that even the "same powder" sold by the genuine foreign manufacturer could infringe U.S. marks, confirming that trademark rights are territorially bounded.

  FACT-FRAMING: SEQUENCE — Walks through Baglin (1911) to Katzel (1923) in chronological order to construct a narrative of consistent, century-old precedent.

  STYLISTIC: CONCRETE_ANCHOR — The Katzel facts (French face powder, identical boxes) make the abstract territoriality principle tangible.

  CROSS-BRIEF INTERACTION: This section preemptively responds to Respondent Section 3's characterization of Steele as establishing broad extraterritorial reach. Petitioner's move is to surround Steele with older precedent (Baglin, Katzel) that cuts the other way, making Steele look like the exception rather than the rule.


--- Section 4: C. The Act's Commerce Provision Reinforces Rather than Lifts Territorial Limits ---

ARGUMENT UNIT 4.1: The "commerce" definition does not rebut the presumption.

  DOCTRINAL: NARROW_STATUTE — Reads the Act's "all commerce which may lawfully be regulated by Congress" as a constitutional-scope limitation, not an extraterritoriality provision. INVOKE_AUTHORITY — Cites RJR, Morrison, and Kiobel for the proposition that broad "commerce" definitions do not rebut the presumption. APPLY_STANDARD — Applies the established rule that "even statutes that contain broad language in their definitions of 'commerce' that expressly refer to 'foreign commerce' do not apply abroad."

  NARRATIVE: AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME — Frames the commerce definition as "the only conceivable hook," thereby limiting the respondent's available textual arguments to a single provision and then closing it off. PREEMPTIVE_REBUTTAL — Directly addresses the respondent's strongest textual argument (the breadth of "commerce") and systematically dismantles it.

  FACT-FRAMING: RECHARACTERIZE — Recharacterizes the word "all" from a signal of breadth to a generic quantifier indistinguishable from "any" or "every," which prior decisions have held insufficient. OPPONENT_CONCESSION_CAPTURE — Uses the constitutional equivalence ("all commerce which may lawfully be regulated by Congress" = Commerce Clause commerce) against the respondent: since the Court has already held that explicit "foreign commerce" language is insufficient, the implicit reference here fails a fortiori.

  STYLISTIC: COMPRESSION — "A fortiori, the Lanham Act's at-best implicit reference is insufficient to extend the statute abroad." Packs a complex logical chain into one sentence. CADENCE_SHIFT — Moves from detailed doctrinal analysis to the punchy a-fortiori conclusion. REGISTER_CONTRAST — Shifts from heavy citation to a plain-language constitutional-synonymy argument (the "commerce" definition just restates the Commerce Clause).

ARGUMENT UNIT 4.2: Legislative history confirms the domestic purpose.

  DOCTRINAL: DISTINGUISH_PURPOSE — Argues the "commerce" definition was adopted to cure a constitutional defect (the Trade-Mark Cases holding), not to expand geographic reach.

  NARRATIVE: FRAME_AS_ERROR — Casts the respondent's reading as a misunderstanding of why Congress chose the "all commerce" language.

  FACT-FRAMING: SEQUENCE — Walks through the historical sequence: the 1879 invalidation, Congress's narrowing of trademark statutes, and the eventual Lanham Act definition, to show that "all commerce" was about constitutional authority, not geographic scope.

  STYLISTIC: AUTHORITY_SIGNALING — Cites the Trade-Mark Cases, 100 U.S. 82 (1879), grounding the argument in 19th-century precedent.

  CROSS-BRIEF INTERACTION: Directly counters Respondent Argument Unit 1.1's claim that the "commerce" definition is "as broad as it is clear." Petitioner inverts the argument: the breadth of the language proves only that Congress was tracking the Commerce Clause, not that Congress intended extraterritorial reach.


--- Section 5: D. Extraterritoriality Contravenes Treaty Obligations and Threatens International Friction ---

ARGUMENT UNIT 5.1: International trademark treaties are premised on territoriality.

  DOCTRINAL: INVOKE_AUTHORITY — Invokes the Paris Convention (1883, 179 members) and other treaties to establish that territoriality is an international legal obligation, not merely a domestic choice. CONST_AVOIDANCE — Implicitly argues that reading the Act as extraterritorial would put it in tension with treaty obligations, creating a reason to prefer the narrower reading.

  NARRATIVE: ESCALATE_STAKES — Elevates the dispute from statutory interpretation to potential violations of international obligations. Signals that a pro-respondent ruling would create friction with 179 countries.

  FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND — Foregrounds the breadth of international consensus (naming EU, Germany, Netherlands, Switzerland, Japan, common-law countries) to make the respondent's position look globally isolated. SEQUENCE — Traces the Paris Convention from 1883 to present, emphasizing its durability and near-universal adoption.

  STYLISTIC: CONCRETE_ANCHOR — The number "179 members" makes the international consensus tangible. AUTHORITY_SIGNALING — Heavy citation of international legal scholarship (Peukert, Hiebert) alongside McCarthy and the Paris Convention to signal that the petitioner's position has both domestic and international academic support.

ARGUMENT UNIT 5.2: Extraterritorial application invites retaliation and conflict.

  DOCTRINAL: SLIPPERY_SLOPE — If the U.S. projects its trademark law abroad, other nations may reciprocate, creating a "first-to-file vs. first-to-use" collision with countries that use different registration systems. JURISDICTIONAL_REFRAME — Reframes the question from "can the Act reach abroad?" to "should the U.S. risk a reciprocal breakdown of the international trademark order?"

  NARRATIVE: ESCALATE_STAKES — Moves beyond legal doctrine to practical geopolitical consequences.

  FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND — Foregrounds the risk of foreign-government retaliation to make the practical costs of the respondent's position vivid.

  STYLISTIC: REGISTER_CONTRAST — Shifts from doctrinal analysis to international-relations language (treaty obligations, international friction, retaliation).

  DOCTRINAL: BROADEN_STATUTE — Reads "all commerce which may lawfully be regulated by Congress" as a maximally broad grant that reaches to the outer limits of Congress's power, including foreign conduct affecting U.S. commerce. APPLY_STANDARD — Applies the RJR step-one test and argues the Lanham Act passes it. INVOKE_AUTHORITY — Cites Steele v. Bulova Watch Co. (1952), Aramco (1991), and Morrison (2010) as a consistent line confirming the Act's extraterritorial reach.

  NARRATIVE: AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME — Opens with an affirmative claim of extraterritorial reach rather than treating the presumption as an obstacle to overcome. The framing is: the Act plainly covers this; petitioner is the one running from the text. ESCALATE_STAKES — Warns that denying extraterritorial reach would "require U.S. mark owners to chase wrongdoers around the world."

  FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND — Foregrounds the "all commerce" language as the dispositive textual hook. RECHARACTERIZE — Recharacterizes the "commerce" definition from a routine constitutional-scope provision (petitioner's framing) to a "uniquely broad" provision that is singular in the U.S. Code: "the only civil statute in the U.S. Code that expressly reaches to the outer limits of Congress's authority."

  STYLISTIC: COMPRESSION — "That phrasing is as broad as it is clear" collapses the textual argument into a single declarative sentence. AUTHORITY_SIGNALING — Marshals three Supreme Court decisions in rapid succession (Steele, Aramco, Morrison) to show a consistent line of authority.

  CROSS-BRIEF INTERACTION: Directly counters Petitioner Section 4 (commerce provision). Where Petitioner argued the commerce definition is generic and interchangeable with "any" or "every," Respondent insists it is uniquely broad and distinguishable from every other federal statute's commerce definition.

ARGUMENT UNIT 1.2: Seventy years of lower-court practice confirm extraterritorial application.

  NARRATIVE: AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME — Presents the lower-court consensus as vindicating Congress's judgment, not as an error to be corrected.

  FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND — Foregrounds the duration ("seventy years") and consistency of lower-court practice. DIMINISHING_MODIFIER — Implicitly diminishes petitioner's position as requiring the Court to overturn decades of settled practice.

ARGUMENT UNIT 1.3: Background doctrines provide sufficient safeguards.

--- Section 4: C. The Lanham Act's Extraterritorial Reach Rightfully Encompasses Foreign Infringement ---

ARGUMENT UNIT 4.1: The relevant question is whether conduct is "in commerce," not where it occurs.

  DOCTRINAL: BROADEN_STATUTE — Reads the Act's "use in commerce" element as geographically unbounded: the question is whether the use falls within commerce regulable by Congress, regardless of location. JURISDICTIONAL_REFRAME — Reframes the extraterritoriality question from "Does the Act apply abroad?" to "Does the foreign conduct fall within regulable commerce?"

  NARRATIVE: AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME — Frames the legal test as straightforward: if Congress can regulate it, the Act covers it.

  FACT-FRAMING: RECHARACTERIZE — Recharacterizes the geographic question (domestic vs. foreign) as a Commerce Clause power question (substantial effect on U.S. commerce).

  STYLISTIC: COMPRESSION — "Both text and precedent thus establish that the Lanham Act governs 'use' of a trademark 'in commerce' -- regardless where that 'use' occurs."

ARGUMENT UNIT 4.2: The Commerce Clause analogy (interstate-to-foreign).

  DOCTRINAL: ANALOGIZE — Analogizes Congress's Foreign Commerce Clause power to its Interstate Commerce Clause power, arguing that just as Congress can regulate intrastate activity with a "substantial effect" on interstate commerce, it can regulate foreign activity with a substantial effect on U.S. commerce. INVOKE_AUTHORITY — Cites Gonzales v. Raich, Lopez, Japan Line, and Atlantic Cleaners to establish the analogy.

  NARRATIVE: AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME — Presents the commerce-power argument as uncontroversial established law, not a novel extension.

  FACT-FRAMING: RECHARACTERIZE — Recharacterizes foreign trademark infringement from a sovereignty/territoriality problem into a commerce-regulation problem, where Congress's authority is well established.

  STYLISTIC: AUTHORITY_SIGNALING — The density of citations (Raich, Lopez, Japan Line, Atlantic Cleaners) signals that this is thoroughly settled law.

ARGUMENT UNIT 4.3: Trademark infringement differs from patent/copyright infringement in ways that justify extraterritorial reach.

  DOCTRINAL: DISTINGUISH — Distinguishes trademark infringement from patent and copyright infringement to explain why Congress gave the Lanham Act broader geographic reach than the Patent Act or Copyright Act. DISTINGUISH_PURPOSE — The purpose distinction: foreign trademark infringement causes domestic reputational harm and consumer confusion in a way that foreign patent/copyright infringement does not.

  NARRATIVE: PREEMPTIVE_REBUTTAL — Anticipates the argument that if patent and copyright law do not apply extraterritorially, trademark law should not either. Respondent preempts this by explaining the functional distinction.

  FACT-FRAMING: RECHARACTERIZE — Recharacterizes the comparison between IP statutes from a uniformity argument (petitioner's framing: all IP law should have the same territorial scope) to a functional argument (trademark infringement uniquely causes domestic harm through reputational injury).

  STYLISTIC: AUTHORITY_SIGNALING — Cites Impression Products and Microsoft v. AT&T as establishing the patent/copyright rule while distinguishing them.

ARGUMENT UNIT 4.4: The U.S. government's reading of Steele is wrong.

  DOCTRINAL: DISTINGUISH — Rejects the U.S. government's characterization of Steele as involving a domestic application rather than extraterritorial reach. FRAME_AS_ERROR — "Having not learned its lesson from Morrison, the United States again urges its strained interpretation."

  NARRATIVE: FRAME_AS_ERROR — Frames the government's position as a recurring mistake: the government took a similar losing position in Morrison and is repeating it here.

  FACT-FRAMING: RECHARACTERIZE — Recharacterizes the government's Steele reading as "implausible" because Steele predated the "focus" concept by 60 years.

  STYLISTIC: UNDERSTATED_DEVASTATION — "Having not learned its lesson from Morrison" is a sharp jab at the Solicitor General's office, unusual in SCOTUS briefing. NEW_MOVE_[INSTITUTIONAL_REBUKE] — Directly criticizing a repeat-player amicus (the U.S. government) for making the same mistake twice, leveraging the institutional relationship to cast doubt on the government's position.

  CROSS-BRIEF INTERACTION: Directly counters Petitioner Section 5.    - Respondent: BROADEN_STATUTE -- "all commerce" is "uniquely broad" and distinguishable from boilerplate commerce definitions in other statutes. The Act is the "only civil statute in the U.S. Code" that reaches to the outer limits of Congress's authority.
   - Structural dynamic: Both sides accept that the commerce definition is the key textual battleground, but they characterize it in opposite directions. Petitioner uses the a-fortiori move (if express "foreign commerce" language is insufficient, implicit references are even weaker). Respondent uses the uniqueness move (no other statute goes this far, so this one was designed to go further).

2. STEELE v. BULOVA (Precedent Fight)
   Pet. Sections 3 and implied Section E vs. Resp. Sections 1, 3
   - Petitioner: DISTINGUISH -- Surrounds Steele with older territoriality precedent (Baglin, Katzel) and attempts to recharacterize it as a case about U.S. defendants or a domestic application, not a holding about extraterritoriality per se.
   - Respondent: BROADEN_HOLDING -- Reads Steele as a categorical holding that the Act applies extraterritorially, confirmed by Aramco and Morrison. Deploys the Steele dissent (footnote 6) to show the extraterritoriality question was squarely joined. Labels petitioner's re-reading as "revisionist" and invokes statutory stare decisis.
   - Structural dynamic: Petitioner tries to shrink Steele; Respondent tries to expand it. Respondent has the stronger positional advantage because statutory stare decisis places the burden on the party seeking to change the reading.

3. INTERNATIONAL FRICTION (Stakes Framing)
   Pet. Section 5 vs. Resp. Section 2 (Unit 2.4) and Section 5
   - Petitioner: ESCALATE_STAKES + SLIPPERY_SLOPE -- Extraterritoriality contravenes treaty obligations (Paris Convention), threatens retaliation, and disrupts the global trademark order.
   - Respondent: PREEMPTIVE_REBUTTAL + FOREGROUND_ABSENCE -- Seventy years of extraterritorial application with no significant international friction. Background doctrines (comity, forum non conveniens) adequately manage conflicts. The feared harms are speculative; the empirical record refutes them.
   - Structural dynamic: Petitioner argues from theory (treaty structure, potential consequences); Respondent argues from practice (70 years of evidence). Respondent holds the stronger ground here because empirical evidence generally outweighs hypothetical risk in judicial reasoning.

4. TEXTUAL SILENCE vs. TEXTUAL BREADTH
   Pet. Section 2 vs. Resp. Section 2 (Unit 2.1)
   - Petitioner: FOREGROUND_ABSENCE -- The Act's silence on extraterritoriality is dispositive. Congress knows how to legislate extraterritorially when it wants to.
   - Respondent: BURDEN_SHIFT + FRAME_AS_ERROR -- Petitioner is arguing from what the Act does NOT say rather than engaging with what it DOES say. The "all commerce" language is not silence; it is an affirmative textual indication of breadth.
   - Structural dynamic: Petitioner treats the absence of express extraterritoriality language as the end of the inquiry. Respondent reframes the question from "Did Congress use the magic words?" to "What does the language Congress actually chose mean?"

5. TRADEMARK vs. OTHER IP (Distinctiveness Argument)
   Pet. implicit (no specific section) vs. Resp. Section 4 (Unit 4.3)
   - Petitioner: Implicitly argues all IP statutes should be treated similarly (patent and copyright law do not apply extraterritorially, so trademark law should not either).
   - Respondent: DISTINGUISH -- Trademark infringement uniquely causes domestic harm (consumer confusion, reputational injury) even when consummated abroad, justifying Congress's different treatment.
   - Structural dynamic: Respondent has a structural advantage: by distinguishing rather than defending a broad principle, it only needs to show one relevant difference between trademark and other IP.

6. U.S. GOVERNMENT POSITION (Three-Way Fight)
   Resp. Section 4 (Unit 4.4)
   - Respondent takes the unusual step of sharply criticizing the U.S. government's amicus position, calling its Steele reading "strained" and accusing it of not having "learned its lesson from Morrison." This NEW_MOVE_[INSTITUTIONAL_REBUKE] is noteworthy: it signals confidence and attempts to isolate petitioner by denying them the benefit of governmental support, even though the government's position was partially favorable to petitioner.

1. NEW_MOVE_[STARE_DECISIS_SHIELD]
   Definition: Invoking the heightened standard for overruling statutory precedent (statutory stare decisis) as an affirmative barrier to the opposing party's argument, requiring them to meet a threshold showing before the Court even reaches the merits of their doctrinal claim.
   Location: Respondent Section 2, Unit 2.2. Respondent argues petitioner's suggestion that Steele should be overruled "falls well short of the necessary showing to overcome statutory stare decisis," converting a procedural standard into a substantive shield.

2. NEW_MOVE_[INSTITUTIONAL_REBUKE]
   Definition: Directly criticizing a repeat-player institutional amicus (here, the U.S. government/Solicitor General) for advancing a position the Court has previously rejected, leveraging the institution's track record to undermine its credibility on the current issue.
   Location: Respondent Section 4, Unit 4.4. "Having not learned its lesson from Morrison, the United States again urges its strained interpretation." This move is distinct from ordinary FRAME_AS_ERROR because it targets an institution's pattern of error across cases, not just a single argument's logical flaws.
