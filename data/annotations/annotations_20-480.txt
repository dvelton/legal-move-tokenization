  FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND
                The statutory cross-references (42 U.S.C. 415, 38 U.S.C.

  STYLISTIC:    CONCRETE_ANCHOR

"A. The plain text of the exemption covers a pension for dual-status technician

================================================================================

ARGUMENT UNIT P-IA-1
Text: "'Statutory interpretation begins with the text.' Ross v. Blake, 136 S.
Ct. 1850, 1856 (2016). The task is 'to afford the law's terms their ordinary
meaning.' Niz-Chavez v. Garland, 141 S. Ct. 1474, 1480 (2021). That the
government may prefer a different interpretation does not outweigh clear text."

  DOCTRINAL:    INVOKE_AUTHORITY
                Two recent SCOTUS opinions stacked to establish the textualist
                framework before any substantive analysis begins. Ross and
                Niz-Chavez are both majority opinions from the current Court
                era, signaling alignment with the Court's prevailing
                interpretive method.

  NARRATIVE:    PREEMPTIVE_REBUTTAL
                "That the government may prefer a different interpretation does
                not outweigh clear text" dispatches the opposing position
                before it has been stated. The sentence frames any departure
                from petitioner's reading as a preference-based departure from
                text -- not a legitimate interpretive disagreement.

  FACT-FRAMING: --
                No facts in play; this is pure interpretive framing.

  STYLISTIC:    AUTHORITY_SIGNALING
                Opening with two SCOTUS citations is standard, but the
                selection is pointed. Both cases involved the Court rejecting
                government arguments that departed from statutory text. The
                implicit message: this Court has recently done what petitioner
                is asking it to do again.

----------

ARGUMENT UNIT P-IA-2
Text: "The plain language of the uniformed-services exception contains only two
requirements. The first is a sourcing requirement: A payment is excluded from
the windfall elimination provision only if it is 'based on service as a member
of a uniformed service,' that is, if the retiree is entitled to the payment
because of that service. The second is a tracing requirement: A payment is
exempt from the windfall elimination provision only to the extent that it is
based 'wholly' on that service, that is, not on any other employment."

  DOCTRINAL:    APPLY_STANDARD
                Petitioner constructs a two-part test from the statutory text:
                (1) a "sourcing" requirement (payment must be based on
                uniformed service), and (2) a "tracing" requirement (payment
                must be based wholly on that service). This parsing does real
                doctrinal work: it separates the "uniformed service" question
                from the "wholly" question, making each easier to satisfy
                independently.

  DOCTRINAL:    NARROW_STATUTE
                By isolating "wholly" as a tracing requirement (addressing
                how much of the payment derives from uniformed service), the
                petitioner prevents "wholly" from being read as a qualifier on
                the nature of the service itself. This is the central
                interpretive dispute in the case.

  NARRATIVE:    AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME
                "Contains only two requirements" is a simplification move.
                Framing the exception as having exactly two elements makes
                petitioner's reading appear clean and manageable, while the
                opposing interpretation (which reads "wholly" differently)
                appears to add complexity the statute does not demand.

  FACT-FRAMING: RECHARACTERIZE
                The statutory phrase is repackaged using petitioner's own
                labels ("sourcing" and "tracing"). These are not terms from
                the statute; they are analytical categories petitioner
                invented to organize the text in its favor. Once the reader
                adopts these labels, the interpretive outcome follows.

  STYLISTIC:    CONCRETE_ANCHOR
                "Sourcing" and "tracing" are memorable, quasi-technical labels
                that give the Court a vocabulary for adopting petitioner's
                reading. If these terms appear in the opinion, petitioner wins.

----------

ARGUMENT UNIT P-IA-3
Text: "The words 'based on service as a member of a uniformed service' form a
phrasal adjective that modifies the noun 'payment.' Thus, 'service as a member
of a uniformed service' must be a but-for cause of the payment for it to fall
within the exception. See Babb v. Wilkie, 140 S. Ct. 1168, 1173 (2020)
(reading 'based on age' in the phrase 'discrimination based on age' to mean
that 'age must be a but-for cause of [the] discrimination')."

  DOCTRINAL:    ANALOGIZE
                Imports the Court's own reading of "based on" from Babb v.
                Wilkie, an age-discrimination case, to this Social Security
                context. The analogy works at the level of statutory grammar:
                "based on X" means X is a but-for cause, regardless of the
                substantive domain. This gives petitioner's reading the
                imprimatur of recent precedent.

  DOCTRINAL:    APPLY_STANDARD
                Translates the "sourcing" requirement into a but-for
                causation test. Once that test is in place, the analysis is
                straightforward: would the technician receive a CSRS pension
                but for service as a Guard member? Obviously not, because
                Guard membership is a job prerequisite.

  NARRATIVE:    AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME
                The grammatical analysis ("phrasal adjective that modifies the
                noun 'payment'") presents petitioner's reading as a product
                of sentence diagramming, not advocacy. The interpretation is
                framed as something the syntax requires.

  FACT-FRAMING: --
                No new facts; this unit operates at the level of statutory
                grammar and precedent.

  STYLISTIC:    AUTHORITY_SIGNALING
                Babb v. Wilkie is a 2020 decision -- recent and directly on
                point for the "based on" construction. The citation does
                double duty: it supplies a legal standard and signals that the
                current Court has already endorsed the reading petitioner
                proposes.

  FACT-FRAMING: SEQUENCE

  STYLISTIC:    UNDERSTATED_DEVASTATION

  STYLISTIC:    STRATEGIC_QUOTATION
                Four circuit court quotations are deployed not as authority

ARGUMENT UNIT P-IB-3

RESPONDENT BRIEF -- SECTION II.B
"B. Petitioner does not account for the inherently civilian nature of dual

================================================================================

ARGUMENT UNIT R-IIB-1
Text: "The technician's civilian attributes strongly indicate that work
performed in that role does not constitute qualifying service. Title 5 draws a

  DOCTRINAL:    DISTINGUISH_PURPOSE
                Invokes the Title 5 distinction between "civil service" and
                "uniformed service" as a structural feature of federal
                employment law. The argument is that Congress maintained this
                distinction deliberately, and collapsing it for the windfall
                elimination provision would be inconsistent with the broader
                statutory architecture.

  NARRATIVE:    AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME
                "Inherently civilian" is the core counter-frame to
                petitioner's "membership is in their job description." Where
                petitioner foregrounds military attributes, respondent
                foregrounds civilian ones.

  FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND
                The civilian attributes of the technician position -- not
                specified in this fragment but referenced -- are presented
                as the dominant characteristic of the role, with military
                membership as incidental rather than defining.

  STYLISTIC:    CADENCE_SHIFT
                "Title 5 draws a clear and relevant distinction" is a short,
                declarative sentence deployed after the longer opening
                assertion. The brevity lends it the quality of a settled
                proposition rather than an argument.

  CROSS-BRIEF INTERACTION: Directly responds to P-I-2 through P-I-4, where
  petitioner catalogs the military attributes of the technician position.
  Respondent's counter-move is not to deny those attributes but to invoke
  an entirely separate statutory framework (Title 5) that categorizes the

ARGUMENT UNIT R-IIB-2
Text: "And the fact that Congress included commissioned officers of the National
Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) Corps and Public Health Service
(PHS) Corps within the definition of 'member of a uniformed service' supports
the distinction between civilian and uniformed service because those officers do
not generally have civilian characteristics and receive military pay and

  DOCTRINAL:    ANALOGIZE
                NOAA Corps and PHS Corps officers are used as a benchmark for
                what Congress considers genuine uniformed service. These
                officers lack civilian characteristics and receive military

                technicians have civilian characteristics and receive civilian

  NARRATIVE:    ESCALATE_STAKES
                The NOAA/PHS comparison raises the standard for what counts
                as uniformed service. If even these non-military officers
                (who still lack civilian attributes) are the baseline, then
                dual-status technicians with their civilian pay and benefits
                fall well short.

  FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND_ABSENCE
                The move highlights what dual-status technicians lack relative
                to NOAA/PHS officers: military pay and military retirement
                benefits. The absence of these attributes becomes evidence
                that technicians are not performing "service as a member of a
                uniformed service."

  STYLISTIC:    CONCRETE_ANCHOR
                NOAA Corps and PHS Corps are specific, named entities. They
                give the Court a concrete reference point for the category
                "uniformed service" that is harder to argue with than
                abstract definitional analysis.

  FACT-FRAMING: RECHARACTERIZE

  STYLISTIC:    COMPRESSION
                A complex doctrinal distinction (FTCA sovereign immunity vs.

                The pro-veteran canon, which petitioner presumably invokes as

  STYLISTIC:    UNDERSTATED_DEVASTATION

ARGUMENT UNIT R-IB-1
Text: "The definition of 'uniformed service' incorporated in the uniformed

to the civilian role of a dual status technician. That definition provides that

  DOCTRINAL:    NARROW_STATUTE
                Respondent reads the 410(m) definition as imposing an
                additional constraint: the person must be operating in a
                reserve-component role. Because technicians are hired into
                civilian positions (not "appointed, enlisted, or inducted"
                as technicians), they do not enter the technician role

  DOCTRINAL:    DISTINGUISH
                Separates two relationships the technician has with the
                military: (1) Guard membership (which respondent concedes)
                and (2) reserve-component service (which respondent argues
                is not occurring when the technician performs civilian
                duties). The distinction prevents petitioner from collapsing
                Guard membership into reserve-component status.

  NARRATIVE:    CONCEDE_REDIRECT
                Respondent implicitly concedes that technicians are Guard

  FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND
                The verbs of military accession -- "appointed, enlisted, or
                inducted" -- are foregrounded to highlight that technicians
                do not enter the technician position through any of these

  STYLISTIC:    STRATEGIC_QUOTATION
                The statutory verbs "appointed, enlisted, or inducted" are
                quoted to underscore their military character. Each verb
                connotes a formal military-accession process that is foreign

ARGUMENT UNIT R-IB-2
Text: "'Army National Guard' and 'Army National Guard of the United States'
refer to two different entities. The term 'Army National Guard' refers to state

  DOCTRINAL:    DISTINGUISH
                This is the most granular distinction in either brief.
                Respondent separates two entities that petitioner's argument
                treats as interchangeable: the state-level Army National

                Guard of the United States (which is defined as a "reserve
                component" under 38 U.S.C. 101(27)(G)). If these are
                different entities, then technicians working for the state
                Guard are not necessarily serving in a "reserve component."

  DOCTRINAL:    NARROW_STATUTE
                The statutory cross-reference chain (42 U.S.C. 410(m) to
                38 U.S.C. 101(27)(G)) defines "reserve component" to include
                the Army National Guard of the United States. Respondent
                argues this federal entity is distinct from the state Army
                National Guard, and technicians serve the state entity.

                chain.

  NARRATIVE:    --
                This unit is purely definitional. The narrative work is

  FACT-FRAMING: FOREGROUND

  state Army National Guard that employs dual-status technicians. If
  respondent is right, petitioner's chain from technician employment to
  "uniformed service" has a broken link.

CROSS-BRIEF INTERACTION SUMMARY
================================================================================

1. THE "WHOLLY" PLACEMENT BATTLE
   Petitioner (P-IA-2, P-IB-1, P-IB-3): "Wholly" modifies "based on," creating
   a tracing requirement about payment sourcing. A payment qualifies if it is
   entirely based on uniformed-service employment, even if that employment has
   some civilian characteristics.
   Respondent (R-IA-2, R-IA-3): "Wholly" modifies the entire predicate "service
   as a member of a uniformed service," requiring that the service itself be
   exclusively military in character.
   Assessment: This is the central interpretive dispute. Petitioner's reading
   isolates "wholly" syntactically; respondent's reading absorbs it into the
   substantive description of the service. Petitioner's "textual transfiguration"
   accusation (P-IB-1) is a direct attack on respondent's placement of "wholly."

2. THE NATURE-OF-SERVICE CHARACTERIZATION WAR
   Petitioner (P-I-2 through P-I-4): Catalogs military attributes -- Guard
   membership required, military grade required, uniform required, Guard-focused
   duties, military supervisor. Uses STRATEGIC_SILENCE on civilian attributes.
   Respondent (R-IIB-1, R-IIB-2): Catalogs civilian attributes -- civilian pay,
   civilian pension system, civilian employment protections. Uses NOAA/PHS
   officers as a benchmark for "real" uniformed service.
   Assessment: Each side foregrounds the attributes that support its reading and
   backgrounds the ones that don't. The Court must decide which set of attributes
   is dispositive -- or whether the statutory text resolves the question without
   reference to the position's practical characteristics.

3. THE PREPOSITION "AS"
   Petitioner (P-IA-2): Reads "as a member" as a status description. If you are
   a Guard member and you work as a technician, your technician service is
   "service as a member" because you hold that status throughout.
   Respondent (R-IA-3): Reads "as" to mean "in the capacity of." Service
   qualifies only if performed in the capacity or role of a uniformed-service
   member, not merely by a person who holds that status while doing something
   else.
   Assessment: The word "as" does more doctrinal work in this case than any
   single citation. Respondent's dictionaries support the role-limitation
   reading; petitioner's structural argument (the job requires membership at
   every point) supports the status-description reading.

4. THE ENTITY-DISTINCTION ARGUMENT
   Petitioner (P-I-1): Cites 38 U.S.C. 101(27)(G)-(H) to show the National
   Guard is a uniformed service under the statute.
   Respondent (R-IB-2): Argues that 101(27)(G) references the Army National
   Guard of the United States (a federal reserve component), not the Army
   National Guard (the state entity that employs technicians). These are
   different entities with different statutory definitions.
   Assessment: This is respondent's most technically granular argument. If the
   Court accepts the entity-level distinction, it could resolve the case
   without reaching the "wholly" question at all. The Kavanaugh concurrence
   citation (R-IB-2) is targeted: it puts the authoring Justice's own prior
   words on respondent's side.

5. THE DEFERENCE FRAMING
   Petitioner (P-II-1): Limits deference to Skidmore (persuasiveness only,
   no presumption of correctness).
   Respondent: Not addressed in available text, but the argument outline
   suggests respondent does not claim Chevron deference either.
   Assessment: By conceding that Skidmore governs, petitioner ensures the
   SSA's interpretation is evaluated on its persuasive merits. This is
   strategically sound: if the text is genuinely clear, deference is
   irrelevant; if it is ambiguous, Skidmore still allows the Court to
   reject the agency's reading.

6. THE FERES / PRO-VETERAN CANON SKIRMISH
   Petitioner (referenced in argument outline): Invokes Feres doctrine
   (technicians treated as military for sovereign-immunity purposes) and the
   pro-veteran canon of interpretation.
   Respondent (R-IIB-3, R-IIB-4): Deploys DISTINGUISH_PURPOSE to separate
   Feres from the Social Security context. Neutralizes the pro-veteran canon
   by denying ambiguity and arguing application here would produce perverse
   results.
   Assessment: Respondent's counter to both arguments is efficient. The Feres
   distinction is straightforward (different statutory purpose). The
   pro-veteran canon response is more aggressive: respondent argues that
   applying the canon would "turn the canon on its head," flipping its
   normative valence.
