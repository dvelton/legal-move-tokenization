LEGAL MOVE TOKENIZATION — Research Framework
=====================================================================

PROJECT: Can legal reasoning be compressed into a discrete vocabulary
of strategic "moves" that is more predictive and more interpretable
than raw text?

HYPOTHESIS: Legal briefs operate on four simultaneous layers —
doctrinal, narrative, fact-framing, and stylistic — and the strategic
choices at each layer can be identified, labeled, and modeled as
sequences of discrete tokens.

=====================================================================
TAXONOMY (v0.2)
=====================================================================

LAYER 1 — DOCTRINAL (legal reasoning operations)
  NARROW_STATUTE       Confine a statute's scope
  BROADEN_STATUTE      Expand a statute's reach
  NARROW_HOLDING       Contract what a precedent stands for
  BROADEN_HOLDING      Expand what a precedent stands for
  DISTINGUISH          Show why a cited case doesn't apply
  ANALOGIZE            Show why a favorable case controls
  APPLY_STANDARD       Walk through elements of a legal test
  APPLY_TO_FACTS       Map legal rule onto case-specific facts
  BURDEN_SHIFT         Argue the other side must prove something
  INVOKE_AUTHORITY     Cite court, judge count, or weight
  DISTINGUISH_PURPOSE  Line between two statutory purposes
  CONST_AVOIDANCE      Argue for reading avoiding const. issue

LAYER 2 — NARRATIVE (story/structural strategy)
  AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME    Position as declarative fact, not rebuttal
  CONCEDE_REDIRECT     Acknowledge weakness, pivot to irrelevance
  PREEMPTIVE_REBUTTAL  Address opponent's best point first
  ESCALATE_STAKES      Implications beyond the parties
  FRAME_AS_ERROR       Label lower court wrong from the start

LAYER 3 — FACT-FRAMING (selection, order, characterization)
  FOREGROUND           Lead with or emphasize favorable fact
  BACKGROUND           Bury unfavorable fact
  RECHARACTERIZE       Same event, different framing
  SEQUENCE             Control timeline to imply causation
  STRATEGIC_SILENCE    Omit where absence won't draw attention
  FOREGROUND_ABSENCE   Emphasize what hasn't happened

LAYER 4 — STYLISTIC (delivery/rhetoric)
  COMPRESSION          One sentence where opponent took a paragraph
  CADENCE_SHIFT        Complex passage then short declarative
  CONCRETE_ANCHOR      Ground abstraction in vivid detail
  UNDERSTATED_DEVASTATION  Calm language for devastating point
  STRATEGIC_QUOTATION  Opponent's own words make your point
  RHETORICAL_QUESTION  Obvious answer, reader supplies conclusion
  PARALLEL_STRUCTURE   Identical syntax, cumulative weight


=====================================================================
METHODOLOGY
=====================================================================

DATA SOURCE: BriefMe dataset (HuggingFace, jw4202/BriefMe)
  - 3,753 SCOTUS briefs, preprocessed config
  - 168 dockets with identifiable petitioner + respondent main briefs
  - Additional amicus briefs available for richer analysis

ANNOTATION PIPELINE:
  1. Extract argument sections from paired briefs
  2. LLM generates four-layer move annotations per paragraph
  3. Legal expert (human) reviews and corrects annotations
  4. Corrections feed back into taxonomy refinement
  5. Inter-annotator agreement measured between LLM and expert

EXPERIMENT DESIGN:
  Phase 1 — Taxonomy validation (current)
    - Annotate 3-5 cases, expert reviews
    - Refine move vocabulary based on gaps and errors
    - Establish annotation guidelines

  Phase 2 — Scale annotation
    - Annotate 25-50 adversarial pairs
    - Measure consistency of LLM annotations
    - Build gold-standard subset with expert review

  Phase 3 — Representation analysis
    - Compress each brief into move sequence
    - Compare move sequences across sides of same case
    - Test whether move sequences correlate with outcomes
    - Analyze which move combinations appear in winning briefs

  Phase 4 — Predictive modeling
    - Train sequence model on move tokens
    - Evaluate: can move-level representations predict
      which arguments the Court engages with in its opinion?
    - Compare predictive power vs. text-level baselines


=====================================================================
NOVEL CONTRIBUTION
=====================================================================

What exists: IRAC-level annotation (fact/issue/rule/analysis/conclusion)
What we add: STRATEGIC MOVE-level annotation across four layers

The difference: IRAC tells you WHAT a paragraph IS (it's "analysis").
Move annotation tells you WHAT IT'S DOING (it's narrowing a holding
while conceding a weak point and delivering the conclusion in
understated language for maximum impact).

This is analogous to the difference between part-of-speech tagging
(this word is a noun) and semantic role labeling (this noun is the
agent performing the action). Both are useful; the latter captures
far more about meaning and intent.


=====================================================================
OPEN QUESTIONS
=====================================================================

1. Granularity: Is paragraph-level the right unit, or should we go
   sentence-level? Paragraph seems right because moves often span
   multiple sentences.

2. Multi-label: Moves stack (a paragraph can be NARROW_HOLDING +
   CONCEDE_REDIRECT + UNDERSTATED_DEVASTATION simultaneously). Our
   representation needs to handle this naturally.

3. Sequencing: Does the ORDER of moves carry predictive signal?
   Intuition says yes — leading with ESCALATE_STAKES vs. leading
   with APPLY_TO_FACTS signals different strategies.

4. Cross-brief interactions: How do we model the adversarial
   relationship? Petitioner's FOREGROUND on a point may directly
   correspond to respondent's BACKGROUND on the same point.

5. Game-theoretic dimension: Some moves are "chess" (deterministic,
   the law is clear) and some are "poker" (probabilistic, managing
   risk). Should this be a fifth layer or an attribute?


=====================================================================
RESULTS FROM INITIAL ANNOTATION (2 cases, 33 argument units)
=====================================================================

CASES ANNOTATED:
  1. Cameron v. EMW Women's Surgical Center (20-601)
     - State sovereignty / appellate intervention
     - 16 argument units annotated
  2. Hughes v. Northwestern University (19-1401)
     - ERISA duty of prudence for retirement plans
     - 17 argument units annotated

NEW MOVES DISCOVERED (7 total):

  From 20-601:
    JURISDICTIONAL_REFRAME — Swapping the entire legal framework,
      not adjusting a single rule's scope
    SLIPPERY_SLOPE — Downstream consequences of opponent's proposed
      rule in future cases

  From 19-1401:
    DIMINISHING_MODIFIER — Inserting adjectives that systematically
      minimize opposing facts ("marginal," "isolated," "paternalistic")
    AUTHORITY_SIGNALING — Citing a case for the identity of its author
      (e.g., Breyer as Chief Justice), not just the holding
    ALTERNATIVE_HOLDING — "Even if" argument that outcome is same
      regardless of which legal standard applies
    OPPONENT_CONCESSION_CAPTURE — Foregrounding a concession from
      the opposing brief and weaponizing it
    REGISTER_CONTRAST — Deliberate shift from formal to dismissive
      language to signal an argument is unworthy of engagement

EMERGING PATTERNS:
  1. RECHARACTERIZE is the most frequent move across both cases
  2. Cross-brief interactions cluster around key contested facts
  3. Both sides in both cases use CONCEDE_AND_REDIRECT as transitions
  4. Stylistic moves are harder to detect but carry real signal
  5. The taxonomy is growing organically — 30 initial moves + 7 new
