GAME-THEORETIC MODEL OF LEGAL BRIEFING
A Framework for Tokenizing Legal Reasoning Moves

========================================================================

This document lays out a game-theoretic framework for understanding legal
briefing, developed through brainstorming on the legal-moves tokenization
project. The audience is lawyers and legal researchers, not game theorists.
Where game theory concepts are referenced, they're explained in plain terms.


1. THE THREE-PLAYER PERSUASION GAME
========================================================================

The instinct when modeling litigation is to treat it as a two-player
contest: petitioner versus respondent. That framing misses the most
important structural feature of briefing.

Legal briefing is a three-player persuasion game. Two informed adversaries
are performing for a third party (the court) whose preferences and
decision criteria are only partially known. Both sides know the facts and
the law. Both sides know each other's probable arguments. But neither side
knows exactly how the court will weigh competing considerations, which
facts will matter most to the decisionmaker, or which doctrinal framing
will prove most persuasive.

This structure maps onto what game theorists call "persuasion games" (see
Milgrom & Roberts 1986, Glazer & Rubinstein 2004). In a persuasion game,
interested parties selectively present verifiable evidence to a
decisionmaker. The decisionmaker knows the parties are strategic and
accounts for that. A related concept is the "signaling game" (Spence
1973), where the signal's credibility depends on who's sending it and
what it costs them to send it. When a former Solicitor General files an
amicus brief saying the government's position is legally untenable, the
identity of the messenger is doing as much work as the argument itself.

The critical feature of the three-player structure: advocates are not
trying to "beat" each other. They're trying to move the beliefs of the
same audience. The opponent's brief matters only insofar as it affects the
court's reaction. This means the relevant strategic question is never
"what's my best argument?" in the abstract. It's always "what's my best
argument given what the court is likely to hear from the other side and
how the court is likely to process both?"


2. MIXED INFORMATION REGIMES: CHESS AND POKER THROUGHOUT
========================================================================

Earlier brainstorming identified two modes of legal reasoning. Some moves
are "chess moves": the law is clear, the facts are undisputed, and the
task is precise execution of a known framework. Think of applying a
multi-factor test where the factors are well-established and the facts
clearly satisfy each one. Other moves are "poker moves": the law is
unsettled, the outcome depends on how you frame competing considerations,
and you're managing probabilities rather than demonstrating certainties.

The first important insight is that these modes don't map cleanly onto
briefing sections or stages. Even a textbook doctrinal argument carries a
poker element because you're always gauging how it will land with a
specific decisionmaker. A Chevron deference argument is doctrinal chess
in the sense that the framework is well-defined. But whether the court
will actually defer, and how aggressively, depends on the court's
appetite for deference in this particular regulatory context. That
appetite is partially hidden.

The poker element is always present because the audience's reaction
function is never fully known. You can study a judge's prior opinions,
read their law review articles, note their questions at oral argument in
similar cases. But you're still working with an incomplete model of their
reasoning.

However, there is a dimension where the information regime does shift
systematically across the briefing sequence: information about the
opponent's strategy.

  Opening merits brief: Written with anticipated but unconfirmed
  knowledge of the opponent's strategy. You know the universe of
  plausible counterarguments and you can guess which ones they'll
  lead with, but you don't know their exact framing, emphasis,
  or which facts they'll highlight.

  Response brief: Written with full knowledge of the opponent's
  opening moves. You've seen their cards (at least the ones they
  chose to play). You can respond directly to their strongest
  arguments and exploit the gaps they left.

  Reply brief: Written with full knowledge of both sides' positions.
  The strategic picture is nearly complete on the adversarial
  dimension. The only remaining uncertainty is the court's reaction.

So the adversarial dimension becomes progressively more chess-like over
the briefing sequence. Each stage reveals more about the opponent's
strategy, reducing uncertainty about what you're arguing against. But the
audience dimension stays poker-like throughout. You never get full
information about how the court will process what it's heard.

This dual information regime is what makes briefing strategy genuinely
complex. You're playing two different games simultaneously: a sequential
revelation game against your opponent and a persistent persuasion game
aimed at the court.


3. MULTI-TARGETED MOVES
========================================================================

At the Supreme Court, and in any multi-judge appellate setting, the brief
is simultaneously performing for multiple audience members with different
preferences and different priors. This adds a layer of complexity that
single-judge litigation doesn't have.

Consider a SCOTUS merits brief. The advocate's audience includes justices
who are likely sympathetic, justices who are likely hostile, and one or
two justices who could go either way. The brief has to serve different
functions for each group:

  Holding friendly votes: Some sections exist to give sympathetic
  justices the doctrinal hooks they need to write or join a favorable
  opinion. These sections tend to be heavy on precedent analysis,
  narrow framing, and APPLY_STANDARD or NARROW_HOLDING type moves.
  The goal isn't persuasion so much as equipping allies with usable
  reasoning.

  Persuading swing votes: Other sections target the undecided. These
  might deploy ESCALATE_STAKES moves showing the real-world
  consequences of a bad rule, or CONCRETE_ANCHOR moves grounding
  abstract doctrine in specific, relatable facts. The advocate's
  theory of what matters to the swing justice drives the selection and
  emphasis of these moves.

  Preempting hostile votes: Some sections exist to limit the damage
  from justices who are likely to rule against you. A strong
  preemptive section might constrain the scope of an unfavorable
  opinion by showing the court why a broad adverse holding would
  create problems even for interests the hostile justices care about.

The order and emphasis of the brief reflects the advocate's theory of
which justices are in play and what each one needs to hear. A brief
aimed at Justice X as the swing vote will look structurally different
from a brief aimed at Justice Y, even on the same legal issue, because
the moves that resonate with each are different.

This means each tokenized move could carry an "audience target"
attribute: is this aimed at reinforcing a friendly vote, persuading an
undecided vote, or preempting a hostile objection?

In game theory terms, this is a form of mechanism design. The advocate
is constructing a document that must produce a favorable outcome from a
diverse audience with heterogeneous preferences. The challenge is that
a move optimized for one audience member might be suboptimal or even
counterproductive for another. An aggressive ESCALATE_STAKES move might
galvanize a swing vote while annoying a hostile justice into writing a
more pointed dissent. The advocate has to weigh these tradeoffs.


4. ORAL ARGUMENT AS INFORMATION REVELATION
========================================================================

At the Supreme Court, oral argument happens after full briefing and
before the opinion. From a game-theoretic perspective, this is a mid-game
information revelation event.

The justices' questions during oral argument reveal partial information
about the audience's reaction function. A justice who spends five minutes
pressing the petitioner on a particular hypothetical is revealing that
this hypothetical matters to their analysis. A justice who expresses
skepticism about a doctrinal framework is revealing that the brief's
framing didn't land. A justice who feeds the advocate a helpful question
is signaling sympathy.

Think of it as partial card revelation in poker. You don't see the
court's full hand, but you see enough to update your beliefs about where
each justice is leaning and which arguments are gaining or losing
traction.

Advocates adjust in real-time during oral argument based on these
signals. If a swing justice seems concerned about a particular
consequence, the advocate can spend more time on that point. If an
argument is clearly not working, the advocate can pivot to an alternative
framing.

For the tokenization project, oral argument data has a specific
analytical value: it provides a partial signal about which briefing moves
"landed." If a justice asks a question that tracks the language and
framing of a particular section of the brief, that move got their
attention. If an entire line of argument goes unmentioned during
questioning, it may not have registered.

Post-argument, the revealed information also influences downstream
decisions. In lower-court litigation, a judge's comments during oral
argument might prompt settlement discussions. At the Supreme Court, the
information from oral argument occasionally prompts supplemental letter
briefs under Rule 28(j) when new authority becomes available.


5. AMICUS BRIEFS AS COALITION SIGNALS
========================================================================

Amicus briefs serve a distinct game-theoretic function from merits
briefs, and the tokenization model should treat them differently.

The primary function of amicus briefs is signaling coalition breadth.
When forty-two states file an amicus brief supporting one side, the
substantive legal arguments in that brief are secondary to the signal
the filing sends: that a broad and diverse coalition of sovereign
entities has a stake in this outcome.

The identity of the amicus matters more than the specific arguments in
most cases. An amicus brief from a group of former Republican-appointed
judges supporting a position usually associated with Democratic policy
preferences sends a specific signal: this isn't a partisan issue. An
amicus brief from an industry association signals economic stakes. An
amicus brief from a civil rights organization signals equity concerns.
The court reads these signals as information about the real-world
implications and constituency alignment of each position.

In game theory terms, amicus participation is a signaling game nested
within the larger persuasion game. The signal is costly (filing amicus
briefs requires time, resources, and reputational expenditure), which
is what makes it credible. The costliness of the signal is precisely
what distinguishes it from cheap talk. When an organization files an
amicus brief, it's putting its institutional credibility behind a
position, which means the court can treat it as informative rather
than purely strategic.

Some amicus briefs do serve a substantive function: presenting technical
expertise the parties lack, or offering a doctrinal framework the merits
briefs didn't develop. These "expert amicus" briefs function more like
supplemental merits arguments than coalition signals. The tokenization
model should probably distinguish between signal-amicus and
substance-amicus filings.


6. IMPLICATIONS FOR THE MOVE TOKENIZATION MODEL
========================================================================

The game-theoretic analysis suggests several additions to the
tokenization framework beyond the four existing layers (doctrinal,
narrative, fact-framing, and stylistic).

Additional move attributes:

  Audience target: Each move should be tagged with its likely intended
  audience. Categories might include "reinforce friendly" (holding
  sympathetic votes), "persuade undecided" (targeting swing votes),
  and "preempt hostile" (constraining adverse reasoning). Some moves
  will be general-purpose, aimed at the court as a whole, and that
  should be a valid category too.

  Information regime: The model should capture how much was known about
  the opponent's strategy and the audience's preferences at the time
  of writing. An opening brief move operates under greater adversarial
  uncertainty than a reply brief move. This attribute positions the
  move within the sequential game structure.

  Risk profile: Rather than a binary chess/poker classification, each
  move should sit on a spectrum. A NARROW_HOLDING move applying settled
  precedent to undisputed facts is near the chess end. An
  ESCALATE_STAKES move predicting catastrophic policy consequences
  from a novel legal theory is near the poker end. Most moves fall
  somewhere in between.

Cross-brief interaction modeling:

  Cross-brief interactions should be modeled as response functions, not
  independent sequences. A response brief's moves are conditioned on
  the opening brief's moves. A reply brief's moves are conditioned on
  both prior stages. Treating each brief's moves in isolation would
  miss the strategic interdependence that defines the briefing
  sequence.

  The briefing sequence (open, respond, reply) is a multi-stage game
  where each stage's optimal strategy depends on all prior stages.
  This has practical implications for tokenization: a DISTINGUISH move
  in a response brief only makes sense in reference to the specific
  precedent the opening brief relied on. The move token should capture
  that cross-reference.

Validation data:

  Oral argument transcripts could serve as partial ground truth for
  which moves registered with the court. If a justice's question tracks
  the framing of a particular briefing move, that's evidence the move
  reached its intended audience. The strength of this signal varies:
  a hostile question might mean the move provoked engagement, while a
  friendly question might mean the move provided useful framing.

  The final opinion is the strongest outcome signal. Which arguments
  did the Court adopt? Which did it engage with and reject? Which did
  it ignore entirely? Mapping opinion language back to briefing moves
  could provide outcome data for studying which move types, in which
  combinations, prove most effective.


7. OPEN QUESTIONS
========================================================================

Several questions remain unresolved and worth investigating:

Can we infer audience targeting from the brief text alone? Or do we
need external data about judicial preferences (voting patterns, prior
opinions, ideological scores) to determine whether a move is aimed at
holding a friendly vote versus persuading a swing justice? The text
itself might carry clues: a section that spends unusual time on a
narrow doctrinal point might be aimed at a specific justice known to
care about that doctrine. But confirming that inference likely requires
external data.

How should we model the interaction between move selection and judicial
identity? A move that's optimal before one panel might be
counterproductive before another. An originalist framing that resonates
with some justices might alienate others. The same ESCALATE_STAKES move
might land as appropriately urgent with one judge and hyperbolic with
another. If the model is meant to generalize, it needs to account for
this audience dependence somehow.

Is there a measurable "meta-strategy" at the brief level? Some briefs
are predominantly chess-mode: heavy doctrinal analysis, lots of
NARROW_HOLDING and APPLY_STANDARD moves, methodical and precedent-driven.
Others are predominantly poker-mode: heavy narrative emphasis, lots of
ESCALATE_STAKES and AFFIRMATIVE_FRAME moves, aiming to shift the court's
framing of the problem rather than win within existing doctrine. Does the
meta-strategy correlate with case type, court, or outcome? A brief that
plays chess in a poker case (or vice versa) might be systematically less
effective, and that's a testable hypothesis.

Can we detect when a briefing exchange is "talking past" versus directly
engaging? In some cases, each side runs its own game: petitioner frames
the case as about X, respondent frames it as about Y, and neither side
engages the other's framing directly. In other cases, the exchange is a
genuine move-countermove sequence: petitioner raises an argument,
respondent distinguishes it, petitioner distinguishes the distinction.
The degree of direct engagement might itself be a useful feature for
predicting outcomes, or at least for characterizing different litigation
strategies.
